⚗ Lab 2: Freezing Point Depression

Instructor Teaching Guide — First 10-15 Minutes

◉ LEARNING OBJECTIVES (Write on Board)

By the end of this lab, students will be able to:

  1. Explain WHY solutes lower the freezing point of water
  2. Use the equation ΔT = i × kf × m to predict freezing points
  3. Connect this concept to real-world applications (roads, medicine, food)

🎬 THE HOOK (2-3 minutes)

Start with a Question:

"How many of you drove here today? Did you notice the roads were salted? Anyone know WHY we dump millions of dollars of salt on roads every winter?"

Wait for responses. Most will say "to melt ice" — but push deeper:

"But HOW does salt melt ice? Ice is frozen water. Salt is... salt. How does putting a solid on a solid make it liquid?"

The Reveal:

"Salt doesn't actually 'melt' ice. It lowers the temperature at which water can freeze. That's called freezing point depression — and it's the same chemistry that keeps your blood from freezing, makes ice cream possible, and determines how we formulate IV bags."

§ THE CONCEPT (5-7 minutes)

The Dance Floor Analogy

Draw this on the board or describe:

"Imagine pure water as a crowded dance floor. Everyone's dancing, but when the music slows down (temperature drops), they naturally organize into neat rows — that's ice crystals forming."
"Now throw a bunch of party crashers onto the dance floor — that's your salt dissolving. The party crashers (ions) get in the way! The dancers CAN'T organize into neat rows anymore. You have to slow the music down even MORE (lower temperature) before they can finally form that organized pattern."
Key Point: "The more party crashers (solute particles), the harder it is to freeze. That's freezing point depression."

⚛ THE EQUATION (3-4 minutes)

ΔT = i × kf × m

Break it down:

Symbol Meaning Units "Plain English"
ΔT Change in freezing point °C "How much colder it needs to get"
i van't Hoff factor (none) "How many pieces does it break into?"
kf Freezing point constant °C/m "A property of the solvent (water = 1.86)"
m Molality mol/kg "How concentrated is your solution?"

The van't Hoff Factor — THE KEY CONCEPT

This is where students struggle. Emphasize:

Substance Formula i value WHY?
Glucose C6H12O6 1 Doesn't break apart (molecular)
Sodium Chloride NaCl 2 Na+ + Cl− = 2 particles
Calcium Chloride CaCl2 3 Ca2+ + 2Cl− = 3 particles
Aluminum Chloride AlCl3 4 Al3+ + 3Cl− = 4 particles
"So 1 mole of CaCl2 gives you 3 moles of particles. That means 3× the freezing point depression compared to glucose at the same concentration!"

Quick Check

Ask the class:

"If I have 1 molal NaCl, what's the freezing point? Work it out."
Answer:
ΔT = i × kf × m
ΔT = 2 × 1.86 × 1 = 3.72°C
Freezing point = 0°C − 3.72°C = −3.72°C

🌍 REAL-WORLD CONNECTIONS (2-3 minutes)

Why This Matters:

Application How Freezing Point Depression Is Used
Road Salt • Rock salt (NaCl) is cheap but only works to about −9°C
• CaCl2 works to −29°C (more particles per mole!)
• Cities choose based on typical temperatures and budget
Medicine • IV saline (0.9% NaCl) is carefully calculated to match blood properties
• Cryopreservation uses specific solutions to prevent ice crystal formation
Food Science • Ice cream makers add salt to ice to get cold enough to freeze cream
• The salt surrounds the container — it doesn't go IN the ice cream!

⚗️ TODAY'S LAB OVERVIEW (1-2 minutes)

"Today you're going to:"
  1. Make solutions of different salts (NaCl, CaCl2, etc.)
  2. Freeze them and record the temperature
  3. Compare your experimental results to the theoretical equation
  4. See if real life matches the math — and explain why it might not
Key Skill: "You'll create cooling curves — graphs of temperature vs. time. The flat part (plateau) is where the water is freezing. That's your freezing point."

⚠ COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS TO ADDRESS

Students Think... Reality
"Salt makes ice colder" Salt lowers the freezing point; the ice-salt mixture can absorb more heat
"Molarity and molality are the same" Molarity = mol/L of solution; Molality = mol/kg of solvent
"All salts have i = 2" Different salts have different i values based on how they dissociate
"The lowest temperature is the freezing point" Supercooling can go below the true freezing point temporarily

"Any questions before we start? Remember — if your calculated and experimental values don't match perfectly, that's NORMAL. Real chemistry is messier than equations. Your job is to figure out WHY they don't match."
