🔢 Part 1 — Correct Chemical Formulas from Simple Ions (Q1–4)
Rules: Metals form cations (+), nonmetals form anions (−). Write formulas cation-first. The compound must be electrically neutral. For main-group elements: cation charge = group number; anion charge = group number − 8.
Trick: Cross-multiply charges to get subscripts — the cation charge becomes the anion subscript and vice versa. Reduce to smallest whole numbers.
💡 Example: Fe³⁺ + O²⁻ → cross the charges: Fe2O3.  NaCl: Na⁺ + Cl⁻ → 1:1 ratio → NaCl.

🔣 Part 2 — Formulas with Compound Ions (Q5–8)
Compound ions (polyatomic ions) contain more than one element. Treat them as a single unit. If more than one compound ion is needed, place it in parentheses with a subscript.
Key ions: carbonate CO₃²⁻ | nitrate NO₃⁻ | phosphate PO₄³⁻ | sulfate SO₄²⁻ | hydroxide OH⁻ | ammonium NH₄⁺ | sulfite SO₃²⁻
💡 Example: Al³⁺ + OH⁻ → Al(OH)₃  (3 hydroxides needed, placed in parentheses)

⚖️ Part 3 — Balance the Reaction Equations (Q9–12)
Rules: Never change a chemical formula to balance — only change the coefficients (numbers in front). The same number of each atom must appear on both sides. If no coefficient is written, it is 1.
💡 Treat compound ions (like NO₃⁻) as a single unit when counting. Balance C and H first in combustion reactions, then O last.

Functional Group Key: Hydrocarbons: alkane (C–C only) | alkene (C=C) | alkyne (C≡C) | aromatic (benzene ring)
With oxygen: alcohol (–OH) | ether (–O–) | ketone (C=O in chain) | aldehyde (–CHO at end) | ester (–COO–) | carboxylic acid (–COOH)
With nitrogen: amide (–CO–NH–) | amine (–NH₂)

⚗️ Part 5 — Reactions Involving Alkenes (Q21–26)
Hydrogenation (Q21–23): Alkene + H₂ →[Pt]→ Alkane
Hydrogenation adds H₂ across a double bond, converting an alkene to an alkane. A platinum (Pt) or palladium (Pd) catalyst is required. The C=C double bond becomes C–C single bond.

Hydration (Q24–26): Alkene + H₂O →[H⁺]→ Alcohol
Hydration adds water (H₂O) across the C=C double bond to form an alcohol. An acid catalyst (H⁺) is required. This is the reverse of dehydration.

💧 Part 7 — Hydrolysis of Esters and Amides (Q31–34)
Hydrolysis is the reverse of esterification/amidization. Water is added to break the ester or amide bond.
Ester + H₂O → Carboxylic acid + Alcohol
Amide + H₂O → Carboxylic acid + Amine
