The Big Picture

Every winter, cities spend millions dumping salt on roads. Why? Because salt lowers the freezing point of water, preventing ice from forming. Today you'll calculate exactly how much protection different salts provide — and discover why salt stops working in extreme cold.

The Formula

Worked Example: Calculating Freezing Point Depression

Example: Light Salt (2.5 g NaCl in 50 g water)

Given: Molar mass NaCl = 58.44 g/mol, kf = 1.86 °C/m, i = 2

Step 1: Calculate moles of NaCl

moles = mass ÷ molar mass = 2.5 g ÷ 58.44 g/mol = 0.0428 mol

Step 2: Calculate molality

m = moles ÷ kg solvent = 0.0428 mol ÷ 0.050 kg = 0.856 m

Step 3: Calculate ΔT

ΔT = i × kf × m = 2 × 1.86 × 0.856 = 3.18 °C

Step 4: Find new freezing point

FP = 0°C − ΔT = 0°C − 3.18°C = −3.18°C

Example: CaCl₂ Calculation (Note: i = 3!)

Given: 3.0 g CaCl₂ in 50 g water. Molar mass CaCl₂ = 110.98 g/mol

Step 1: moles = 3.0 g ÷ 110.98 g/mol = 0.0270 mol

Step 2: m = 0.0270 mol ÷ 0.050 kg = 0.541 m

Step 3: ΔT = 3 × 1.86 × 0.541 = 3.02 °C

Step 4: FP = 0°C − 3.02°C = −3.02°C

Notice: CaCl₂ uses i = 3 because it dissociates into 3 ions: Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻

% Error Calculation Example

If you predicted −3.18°C but measured −2.8°C:

% Error = |predicted − actual| ÷ |predicted| × 100

% Error = |−3.18 − (−2.8)| ÷ 3.18 × 100 = 0.38 ÷ 3.18 × 100 = 12%

⚠ Why |predicted| in the denominator?

Your predicted freezing points are negative numbers (like −3.18°C). If you divide by a negative number, you'd get a negative percent error — which doesn't make sense!

The absolute value bars | | around "predicted" turn it positive: |−3.18| = 3.18

This is correct. Always divide by the absolute value of your theoretical value.
