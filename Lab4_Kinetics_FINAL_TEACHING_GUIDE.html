🧪 Chemistry You Need to Know

CH₃COCH₃ + Br₂ → CH₃COCH₂Br + HBr   (H⁺ is catalyst)
rate = k [acetone]ⁿ [H⁺]ᵐ [Br₂]ᵖ

Why Zero Order in Br₂ — Teach This First, Don't Let Students Discover It Confused

The reaction has two steps. Step 1 (slow): acetone + H⁺ → enol. Step 2 (fast): enol + Br₂ → product. The slow step controls the rate. Br₂ is only in the fast step. More Br₂ = faster fast step, but the bottleneck is Step 1, which doesn't involve Br₂ at all.

What to say at the start of lab

"Before you touch anything — I want you to predict something. If you put 4× more Br₂ in your flask, what happens to the rate? Write it down. We'll come back to it after you run your experiment."

Almost all students will say "faster." When Run 1's [Br₂] vs time graph comes out linear and identical in slope to another run — the prediction was wrong. That's the teaching moment. Not the graph comparison, not the R² numbers. The prediction they got wrong.

Why [Br₂] vs Time Is the Right Plot (Zero Order)

If p = 0: rate = −d[Br₂]/dt = k' (constant). So [Br₂] decreases at a constant rate → straight line. You don't need to compare it to first or second order plots. Teach them to look at the line: is it straight? Yes. Done. p = 0.

If it curves downward (decelerates) → first order. If it curves upward (decelerates faster) → second order. In this reaction, it's straight.

💻 Week 2 — Excel Analysis (Simplified)

WEEK 2 — DATA ANALYSIS
🎯
The key simplification: Students make ONE type of graph — [Br₂] vs time — for all three runs. No choosing between zero/first/second order plots. The straight line on Run 1 is enough to establish p = 0. Then all three runs go on one graph and slopes get compared.

Step 1 — Standard Curve (Sheet 1)

Enter [Br₂] in Column A, Absorbance in Column B → Scatter plot → Linear trendline → Display equation + R²

You'll get: A = slope × [Br₂] + intercept (expect slope ≈ 121, R² ≈ 0.999)

Invert it: [Br₂] = (A − intercept) / slope — this is your decoder for all three runs.

Step 2 — Convert Absorbance → [Br₂] for Each Run

For each run (Sheet 2, 3, 4):
Column A: absorbance  |  Column B: time (s)  |  Column C: =(A2−intercept)/slope
Drag C2 formula down for all rows.

Step 3 — One Graph: All 3 Runs Together

Plot time vs [Br₂] for Runs 1, 2, and 3 as three separate series on the same chart.
Add a linear trendline to each. Display equation (slope) and R² for each.

Check Run 1: Is [Br₂] vs time a straight line (R² ≥ 0.99)? If yes → p = 0. That's the whole p determination. Done.
👁️
Visual check for students: A straight line means the rate of Br₂ disappearance is constant — it doesn't care about how much Br₂ is left. That IS zero order. You don't need to test ln[Br₂] or 1/[Br₂] unless the line curves.

Step 5 — Calculate k

k = k' / ([acetone] × [H⁺])   →   use this formula with each run's values, then average

Example: k = k'₁ / (0.80 × 0.20) = k'₁ / 0.16

Units: M/s ÷ M ÷ M = M⁻¹s⁻¹

🧠 Three Misconceptions — Address These

1. "Run 2 and 3 finish faster — does that mean more Br₂ was used up?"

No. All three runs start with the same [Br₂]₀ = 2×10⁻³ M. Runs 2 and 3 finish faster because the RATE is higher (more HCl or more acetone), not because there's more Br₂. They have the same amount of Br₂ to consume, just consumed faster.

2. "The R² is 0.99 not 1.00 — is the line good enough?"

Yes. No real experiment gives R² = 1.000. An R² of 0.990–0.999 is excellent. The question is: is it much better than 0.97 or 0.95? If so, the linear fit wins. For this reaction, R² for [Br₂] vs t is typically 0.995–0.999.

3. "How can Br₂ be zero order if it's a reactant?"

Zero order means its concentration doesn't control the rate — not that it's absent. Br₂ still reacts and gets used up. It's just not at the bottleneck. The slow step (enolization) doesn't involve it, so adding more Br₂ doesn't help.

❓ Discussion Questions — Instructor Answer Key

Q1: Why is the reaction zero order in Br₂?

The rate-determining step is enolization of acetone (Step 1), which requires acetone and H⁺ but not Br₂. Br₂ only participates in the fast second step. Since the fast step is not the bottleneck, its concentration doesn't affect the overall rate.

Q2: Why is H⁺ in the rate law if it's a catalyst?

H⁺ participates in the slow step (enolization) and is regenerated in the fast step. Net: H⁺ is not consumed. But its concentration directly affects how fast the enolization occurs — more H⁺ = faster Step 1 = faster overall rate. Catalysts appear in rate laws when they're involved in the rate-determining step.

Q3: Why do Runs 2 and 3 finish faster than Run 1?

Both have higher rates (Run 2 has double H⁺; Run 3 has double acetone). Since all three start with the same [Br₂]₀ = 2×10⁻³ M, the one with the faster rate consumes that fixed amount of Br₂ in less time. Time to finish = [Br₂]₀ / rate.

Q4: What would happen to the rate if you doubled [Br₂] instead?

Nothing — the rate would stay exactly the same. p = 0. The slope of [Br₂] vs time would be unchanged, but the line would start at a higher y-intercept and take twice as long to reach zero.

Q5: Write a mechanism consistent with your rate law.

Step 1 (slow): CH₃COCH₃ + H⁺ → CH₂=C(OH)CH₃ + H⁺ (enolization)

Step 2 (fast): CH₂=C(OH)CH₃ + Br₂ → CH₂BrCOCH₃ + HBr (bromination of enol)

This gives rate = k[acetone][H⁺], consistent with n=1, m=1, p=0.
