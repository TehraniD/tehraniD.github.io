Experiment A — Effect of Surface Area

Independent variable: tablet surface area  |  Controlled: 100 mL room-temp distilled water

What to notice: The crushed powder exposes many more reactant molecules to the water simultaneously, so productive collisions happen far more often per second. The whole tablet can only react at its surface.

Experiment B — Effect of Temperature

Independent variable: water temperature  |  Controlled: whole tablet, 100 mL water

What to notice: Hot water molecules have greater average kinetic energy. More molecules simultaneously exceed the activation energy (Eₐ), so more collisions are productive per second — faster dissolution.

Experiment C — Effect of pH / Concentration

Independent variable: solvent composition  |  Controlled: whole tablet, 100 mL total volume, room temp

What to notice: Acetic acid (C2) adds H⁺ ions — higher [H⁺] increases collision frequency with HCO₃⁻, speeding CO₂ production (simulates acidic stomach, pH 1.5–3.5). Baking soda solution (C3) raises pH — fewer H⁺ ions available means fewer productive collisions and slower dissolution (simulates the intestine or post-antacid stomach, pH ≈ 8). Expected order: Acidic < Neutral < Basic (dissolution time).
The Chemical Reaction
H₃C₆H₅O₇(s) + 3 NaHCO₃(s) → Na₃C₆H₅O₇(aq) + 3 H₂O(l) + 3 CO₂(g)
Citric acid and sodium bicarbonate are both solids inside the tablet. Dissolution in water is necessary before they can react. CO₂ gas is responsible for the vigorous fizzing you observe. The reaction is complete when bubbling stops AND no solid pieces remain.
Collision Theory
Criterion 1 — Kinetic Energy
Colliding molecules must possess kinetic energy ≥ the activation energy (Eₐ). Without this minimum threshold, existing bonds cannot break and the reaction does not occur.
Criterion 2 — Orientation
Molecules must collide at the correct geometric angle so reacting atoms or functional groups make direct contact. A wrong-angle collision fails even with sufficient energy.
The Arrhenius Equation
k = A · e^(−Eₐ/RT)
As temperature T increases, e^(−Eₐ/RT) increases → k increases → faster reaction rate.
Clinical Connections — Pharmacokinetics
Surface Area
Crushing a tablet maximizes dissolution rate. Extended-release (XR) capsules are engineered to minimize exposed surface area — never crush or chew them. Doing so releases the full dose at once, risking toxicity or overdose.
Temperature
Body temperature (37°C) provides consistent drug dissolution. In hypothermia (low T), enzymatic reaction rates slow. In fever (high T), metabolic reactions modestly accelerate. Critically ill patients may have unpredictable drug absorption.
pH / Concentration
The stomach is acidic (pH 1.5–3.5), accelerating dissolution of basic drugs like antacids. Enteric-coated tablets resist this environment and only dissolve in the neutral intestine (pH 7–8) — protecting the stomach lining and targeting intestinal absorption.
Quick Reference — Lab Procedures
Use this during the lab to keep your place. Each experiment uses a whole tablet unless noted.
Endpoint definition: The reaction is complete the exact moment vigorous bubbling ceases AND no solid tablet pieces remain visible. Both conditions must be met.

Experiment A — Surface Area

  1. 100 mL room-temp distilled water
  2. Drop whole tablet → time → record
  3. Rinse beaker; fresh 100 mL water
  4. Break tablet into 4 quarters → drop all at once → time
  5. Rinse; fresh water; crush tablet to powder (mortar/pestle) → transfer → time

Experiment B — Temperature

  1. 100 mL cold water (ice bath, no ice) → measure temp → whole tablet → time
  2. Transfer room-temp data from Exp A, Trial 1
  3. 100 mL hot water (~50°C) → measure temp → whole tablet → time
  4. Caution: use tongs or heat-resistant gloves for hot beaker

Experiment C — pH / Concentration

  1. Transfer distilled water data from Exp A, Trial 1 → record as C1
  2. Mix 50 mL distilled water + 50 mL acetic acid (vinegar, pH ≈ 3) → drop tablet → time → C2
  3. Mix 50 mL distilled water + 50 mL baking soda solution (0.1 M NaHCO₃, pH ≈ 8.3) → drop tablet → time → C3
