⚡ Quick Concept Refresher

🔺
Pathophysiology Core Concept
Pathophysiology studies how normal physiology is disrupted by disease. Understanding what is normal first lets you recognize what is abnormal. Disease = deviation from homeostasis.
💡
Key Vocabulary: Know These Definitions
Etiology = cause of disease  |  Pathogenesis = mechanism of disease development  |  Sign = objective (measurable) finding  |  Symptom = subjective (patient-reported) experience  |  Prognosis = expected outcome

5 Types of Cellular Adaptation

💪Hypertrophytap to flip
Cell SIZE increases
Cell number stays the same

✅ Physiologic: athlete's heart, pregnant uterus
⚠️ Pathologic: heart failure (cardiomegaly)

Trigger: ↑ workload / demand
📉Atrophytap to flip
Cell SIZE decreases
Function also decreases

Causes: Disuse, malnutrition, ↓ nerve supply, ↓ blood flow, aging

Example: Bed-rest muscle wasting; organ atrophy in denervation
📈Hyperplasiatap to flip
Cell NUMBER increases
Cell size stays the same

✅ Physiologic: breast tissue in pregnancy
⚠️ Pathologic: BPH (enlarged prostate), goiter (↑ TSH stimulation)
🔄Metaplasiatap to flip
One cell type → another cell type
Reversible, protective response

🚬 Smoking: ciliated columnar → squamous (↓ airway defense)
🔥 GERD: squamous → columnar = Barrett's esophagus
⚠️Dysplasiatap to flip
Abnormal cell growth + organization
NOT yet cancer, but a precursor

From: chronic inflammation, chronic irritation

Example: Cervical dysplasia on Pap smear → may progress to cervical cancer
💀Apoptosistap to flip
Programmed cell death
Orderly, regulated, intentional

Purpose: Remove damaged or unnecessary cells
⚠️ If apoptosis fails → cells survive when they shouldn't → cancer / neoplasia
🔥Necrosistap to flip
Unplanned cell death
Pathological, uncontrolled

Causes: ischemia, toxins, infection
Releases inflammatory contents into surrounding tissue → inflammatory response

Contrast: Apoptosis = clean; Necrosis = messy

Chronic Irritation → Metaplasia → Dysplasia → Carcinoma in situ → Invasive Cancer

🔥 Inflammation

🛡️
Inflammation = Protective Response
Inflammation is the body's non-specific defense mechanism against injury, infection, or irritation. Its goal is to remove the harmful stimulus and begin healing.

5 Cardinal Signs of Inflammation

🔴
1. Redness
(Rubor)
Vasodilation → ↑ blood flow to area
🌡️
2. Heat
(Calor)
↑ blood flow + metabolic activity raise local temp
💧
3. Swelling
(Tumor)
↑ vascular permeability → fluid/cells leak into tissue
😣
4. Pain
(Dolor)
Prostaglandins + bradykinin stimulate sensory neurons
🚫
5. Loss of Function
(Functio Laesa)
Pain + swelling limit movement and use

🔬 Inflammatory Cascade — Step by Step

Step 1 — Tissue Injury / Pathogen Entry ▼

Injury, infection, or irritant triggers the initial response. Can be caused by:

  • Physical trauma (cut, burn, fracture)
  • Chemical agents (toxins, acids)
  • Biological agents (bacteria, viruses, fungi)
  • Immune reactions (autoimmune, hypersensitivity)
  • Ischemia (loss of blood supply)
Step 2 — Mast Cell Activation & Histamine Release ▼

Mast cells (found in connective tissue) are activated by injury and release histamine.

Effects of Histamine:

  • Causes vasodilation → ↑ blood flow → redness and heat
  • Increases vascular permeability → fluid and proteins leak into tissue → swelling
  • Contributes to pain by sensitizing pain receptors
🎯 NCLEX: Mast cells = PRIMARY source of histamine. Antihistamines block this response.
Step 3 — Chemotaxis: WBC Migration ▼

Chemotaxis = directed migration of leukocytes (WBCs) toward the injury site, guided by chemical signals.

How it works:

  • Injured tissue releases chemical mediators (cytokines, complement)
  • WBCs respond to the chemical gradient and travel toward the site
  • Diapedesis: WBCs squeeze through capillary walls to reach tissue
  • Neutrophils arrive first (first responders) → Monocytes/Macrophages follow
Step 4 — Phagocytosis: Cleanup ▼

WBCs (especially neutrophils and macrophages) engulf and destroy pathogens, debris, and dead cells.

  • Neutrophils: first responders; short-lived; destroy bacteria
  • Macrophages: arrive later; long-lived; clean up debris; present antigens
  • Pus = dead neutrophils + bacteria + tissue debris
Step 5 — Pyrogens & Fever ▼

Pyrogens = fever-inducing substances that act on the hypothalamus to raise body temperature.

Two Types:

  • Exogenous pyrogens: from outside the body (bacteria, bacterial toxins)
  • Endogenous pyrogens: made by our own immune cells (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α)

Why Fever Helps:

  • ↑ body temp makes environment less hospitable for pathogens
  • Stimulates immune cell activity
  • Promotes antibody production
⚠️
Danger Zone
Fever >104°F (40°C) is dangerous — can cause seizures, brain damage, organ failure.

💊 Key Inflammatory Mediators

Prostaglandins

Produced from arachidonic acid via the COX enzyme pathway. Cause:

  • Fever (reset hypothalamic set point)
  • Pain sensitization
  • Vasodilation and increased permeability

NSAIDs (ibuprofen, aspirin) block COX → reduce prostaglandins → reduce fever, pain, swelling

Cytokines (IL-1, TNF-α, IL-6)

Signaling proteins released by macrophages and other immune cells:

  • Trigger fever (endogenous pyrogens)
  • Recruit more WBCs to the site
  • Promote systemic inflammatory response
  • Elevated in sepsis, autoimmune disease
🎯 NCLEX TIPS — Inflammation
  • Mast cells = histamine. Histamine = vasodilation + ↑ permeability
  • Chemotaxis = WBC migration; Diapedesis = WBC squeezing through vessel walls
  • Neutrophils = first; Macrophages = cleanup crew
  • NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandins → anti-inflammatory, antipyretic, analgesic
  • Pus = neutrophils + bacteria + debris (sign of active infection)

🩹 Wound Healing

⏱️ 4 Stages of Wound Healing

Stage 1: Hemostasis (Clotting) Immediate → Minutes
Body's emergency stop-the-bleeding response.
  • Damaged blood vessels vasoconstrict to slow blood flow
  • Platelets aggregate and form a platelet plug
  • Clotting cascade activated → fibrin clot forms
  • Clot acts as a temporary seal and scaffold for future repair
Stage 2: Inflammation Days 1–4
Defense and cleanup phase — looks like infection but is normal.
  • WBCs (neutrophils then macrophages) migrate to site via chemotaxis
  • Classic signs of inflammation present: redness, heat, swelling, pain
  • Macrophages phagocytize debris and release growth factors
  • Wound may appear red, warm, and slightly swollen — this is expected
Stage 3: Proliferation (Rebuilding) Days 4–21+
New tissue replaces the clot.
  • Fibroblasts arrive and produce collagen — the structural protein of scar tissue
  • Granulation tissue forms: pink/red, moist, fragile — very healthy!
  • Angiogenesis: new blood vessels grow to supply the new tissue
  • Epithelialization: new skin cells grow across the wound surface
  • Wound starts to contract (myofibroblasts pull edges together)
Stage 4: Remodeling (Maturation) Weeks to 1 Year+
Scar tissue reorganizes and strengthens.
  • Collagen fibers reorganize along tension lines for maximum strength
  • Scar tissue reaches only ~80% of original tensile strength
  • Wound may fade from red to white/silver over time
  • Hypertrophic scarring or keloid formation may occur in some patients

🔀 Types of Wound Healing

✅ Primary Intention (1°)

  • Clean wound edges approximated (brought together)
  • Closed with sutures, staples, steri-strips, or tissue adhesive
  • Minimal scarring; fastest healing
  • Examples: Surgical incision, clean laceration closed in the ER

⚠️ Secondary Intention (2°)

  • Edges cannot be approximated — wound left open
  • Fills in from the base up (bottom to top)
  • Significant granulation tissue; larger scar
  • Examples: Pressure ulcers, large infected wounds, abscesses after drainage

⏳ Tertiary Intention (3° / Delayed Primary Closure)

  • Wound intentionally left open initially, then closed later (days later)
  • Used when wound is contaminated or infected
  • Allows infection to clear before closure
  • Examples: Contaminated traumatic wounds, animal bites

🚨 Wound Complications

💥 Dehiscence

  • Disruption / separation of wound edges
  • Skin layers split open (but organs stay inside)
  • Risk factors: obesity, malnutrition, infection, excessive coughing/straining, steroid use
  • Management: Cover with sterile, moist dressing; notify surgeon

😨 Evisceration (EMERGENCY)

  • Protrusion of internal organs through the wound opening
  • MORE severe than dehiscence
  • Surgical emergency — call for help immediately
  • Nursing Action: Cover organs with warm sterile saline-soaked gauze; do NOT attempt to push back; place patient in low Fowler's with knees bent; call surgeon

🛏️ Pressure Ulcers & Wound Care Concepts

Key Wound Care Terms

  • Eschar — Black, leathery dead tissue covering a wound. Must be removed for healing.
  • Debridement — Removal of dead tissue (eschar). Methods: surgical, enzymatic (collagenase), autolytic (moisture-retentive dressings), mechanical (wet-to-dry)
  • Granulation tissue — Healthy, red, moist tissue indicating healing in progress. Fragile — do not disturb.
  • Slough — Yellow/tan/white stringy devitalized tissue. Also needs removal.
  • Maceration — Softening/breakdown of surrounding skin from excess moisture

⚠️ Factors That Impair Wound Healing

Local Factors

  • Infection / contamination
  • Wound desiccation (too dry)
  • Poor blood supply to the area
  • Presence of foreign bodies
  • Repeated trauma / pressure

Systemic Factors

  • Diabetes mellitus (↓ circulation + ↑ infection risk)
  • Malnutrition / protein deficiency (collagen needs protein + Vitamin C)
  • Obesity (↓ blood supply to fat tissue)
  • Corticosteroid use (↓ inflammation and collagen synthesis)
  • Age (↓ healing rate in elderly)
  • Immunosuppression
🎯 NCLEX TIPS — Wound Healing
  • Granulation tissue (red, moist, beefy) = healthy healing — protect it!
  • Dehiscence: wound splits open. Evisceration: organs come out. Evisceration = EMERGENCY
  • Reposition patients every 2 hours to prevent pressure ulcers
  • Vitamin C and protein are essential for collagen synthesis and wound healing
  • Eschar must be debrided before secondary-intention healing can progress

🧬 Genetic Disorders

🔑
Inheritance Patterns Review
Autosomal Dominant (AD): One copy of the mutated gene is enough to cause disease. 50% risk if one parent is affected.
Autosomal Recessive (AR): Two copies needed. Parents are often unaffected carriers. 25% risk if both parents are carriers.
X-linked: Gene is on the X chromosome. Males (XY) more commonly affected.
💨 Cystic Fibrosis (CF) ▼
Autosomal RecessiveCFTR gene — Chromosome 7Both parents must carry25% risk per child

Pathophysiology:

Defective CFTR (Cystic Fibrosis Transmembrane conductance Regulator) protein → faulty chloride channel → chloride stays inside cells → water follows → mucus becomes abnormally thick and sticky

Most Common In:

Caucasians, Ashkenazi Jews; most common fatal genetic disease in white populations in the US

Systems Affected:

  • Lungs: Thick mucus → chronic bacterial infections (Pseudomonas, Staph aureus), bronchiectasis, respiratory failure
  • Pancreas: Mucus blocks pancreatic ducts → malabsorption, steatorrhea (fatty stools), diabetes
  • Reproductive: Males: vas deferens obstruction → infertility. Females: reduced fertility
  • Sweat glands: High sodium/chloride in sweat → salty-tasting skin (diagnostic clue)

Diagnosis:

  • Sweat chloride test — >60 mEq/L = positive (gold standard)
  • Newborn screening, genetic testing

Nursing Interventions & Treatments:

  • Chest physiotherapy (CPT) / airway clearance — percussion, vibration, postural drainage. Perform 1 hour before meals to prevent aspiration.
  • Bronchodilators — open airways before CPT
  • Mucolytics (DNase / dornase alfa) — break down DNA in mucus to thin it
  • Acetylcysteine (Mucomyst) — mucolytic; educate patient about the unpleasant odor (normal and expected)
  • Corticosteroid inhalers — decrease lung inflammation
  • Pancreatic enzyme replacement — taken with every meal and snack to aid digestion
  • High-calorie, high-protein diet — needed due to malabsorption
  • CFTR modulators (e.g., ivacaftor, elexacaftor) — newer targeted therapy that corrects the defective CFTR protein
🎯 NCLEX: CPT 1 hour BEFORE meals. Sweat test = diagnostic. Expect salty skin. Males are often infertile.
🧠 Tay-Sachs Disease ▼
Fatal (infantile form)Autosomal RecessiveMissing Hex-A enzymeChromosome 15

Pathophysiology:

Deficiency of Hexosaminidase-A (Hex-A) enzyme → GM2 ganglioside (a fatty substance) accumulates in neurons → progressive destruction of nerve cells in brain and spinal cord

Most Common In:

Ashkenazi Jews, French-Canadians, Cajun populations (carrier rates significantly higher)

Infantile Form (Classic — most severe):

  • Baby appears normal at birth
  • Symptoms begin ~3–6 months: exaggerated startle response, muscle weakness (floppy baby)
  • Progressive loss of: vision, hearing, swallowing ability, motor function
  • "Cherry-red spot" on macula (visible on fundoscopic exam)
  • Seizures, paralysis, dementia follow
  • Prognosis: Fatal by age 4–6 years

Late-Onset Form:

  • Occurs in teens to adults (30s–40s)
  • Usually NOT fatal
  • Muscle weakness, poor coordination, psychiatric symptoms

Treatment:

No cure. Supportive care only: seizure management, nutrition, respiratory support, comfort measures.

🧠 Huntington's Disease (HD) ▼
Autosomal Dominant50% risk if parent affectedHTT gene — Chromosome 4

Pathophysiology:

Mutation in the HTT gene — abnormal expansion of CAG trinucleotide repeats (>40 repeats) → toxic mutant huntingtin protein → progressive neurodegeneration (especially striatum, cerebral cortex)

Key Features:

  • Late onset: typically 30s–40s (adult onset)
  • Genetically inevitable — if you have the gene, you will develop HD
  • Genetic testing possible — but ethical complexities around testing asymptomatic adults

Symptom Progression:

  • Early: Mood changes, irritability, depression; subtle movement issues
  • Middle: Chorea — involuntary, jerky, dance-like movements (pathognomonic); cognitive decline; difficulty with ADLs
  • Late: Severe chorea, dysphagia, dementia, loss of independence; aspiration pneumonia is common cause of death

Treatment:

  • No cure; no disease-modifying therapy available
  • Tetrabenazine or deutetrabenazine — reduce chorea
  • Antidepressants, antipsychotics for psychiatric symptoms
  • Supportive: speech therapy, physical therapy, dysphagia precautions
🔢 Down Syndrome — Trisomy 21 ▼
Extra Chromosome 21~1 in 700–800 births↑ risk with maternal age

Cause:

Trisomy 21 — 3 copies of chromosome 21. Caused by meiotic non-disjunction (failure of chromosomes to separate) in 95% of cases. Translocation in ~4%; Mosaic in ~1%

Risk Factor:

Advanced maternal age is the strongest risk factor. Risk at 35 = 1 in 350; at 45 = 1 in 30

Diagnosis:

  • Prenatal: Chorionic villus sampling, amniocentesis, NIPT (non-invasive prenatal testing)
  • Postnatal: Karyotyping — gold standard to confirm chromosomal pattern

Clinical Features:

  • Flat facial profile, epicanthal folds, upslanting palpebral fissures
  • Hypotonia (decreased muscle tone) — especially at birth
  • Simian crease (single palmar crease)
  • Short stature, small hands/feet
  • Intellectual disability (variable severity)

Associated Complications:

  • Congenital heart defects — present in ~40–50% (AV canal most common)
  • Gastrointestinal atresias (duodenal atresia)
  • Hypothyroidism
  • ↑ Risk of acute leukemia (AML, ALL)
  • Early-onset Alzheimer's disease (nearly universal by 40s–50s)
  • Atlantoaxial instability (cervical spine instability) — screen before athletic activity

🦠 Infectious Diseases

🍯 Clostridium botulinum ▼
Gram-positiveAnaerobicSpore-forming

Mechanism:

Botulinum toxin is one of the most potent known toxins. It blocks the release of acetylcholine (ACh) at the neuromuscular junction → nerve impulse cannot be transmitted to muscle → flaccid paralysis (muscles are floppy, cannot contract)

Sources:

  • Improperly canned or home-canned foods (anaerobic environment)
  • Wound botulism (contaminated wounds)
  • Infant botulism (honey in infants <1 year — spores colonize gut)

Symptoms (Descending Paralysis):

  • Begins with cranial nerves: blurred/double vision, drooping eyelids, difficulty swallowing/speaking
  • Progresses downward: weakness, then respiratory failure
  • No fever; patient is alert

Treatment:

  • Antitoxin (if given early)
  • Mechanical ventilation for respiratory failure
🎯
Botulism = FLACCID paralysis. Descending. Blocks ACh RELEASE. No fever.
🔩 Clostridium tetani ▼
Gram-positiveAnaerobicSpore-forming

Mechanism:

Tetanus toxin (tetanospasmin) blocks inhibitory neurotransmitters (GABA, glycine) at the spinal cord → muscles cannot relax → sustained, painful muscle spasms (spastic paralysis)

Source:

  • Deep puncture wounds, contaminated soil, rusty objects
  • Can also occur in burns, surgical wounds

Symptoms:

  • Trismus (lockjaw) — masseter muscle spasm; cannot open mouth
  • Risus sardonicus — spastic facial expression ("evil grin")
  • Opisthotonos — severe arching of the back from spasm
  • Generalized muscle spasms triggered by stimuli (light, noise, touch)
  • Seizures, respiratory failure

Prevention:

  • DTaP immunization series in childhood
  • Tdap booster every 10 years in adults
  • Tetanus prophylaxis for wounds
🎯
Tetanus = SPASTIC paralysis. Ascending. Blocks inhibitory NTs. Lockjaw is the classic sign.
🫧 Staphylococcus aureus / MRSA / VRSA ▼
Gram-positive cocciClusters

Normal Staph aureus:

Common skin colonizer. Causes skin infections, abscesses, food poisoning (enterotoxin), pneumonia, bacteremia, septic arthritis, osteomyelitis.

MRSA — Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus:

  • Resistant to beta-lactam antibiotics (penicillin, methicillin, most cephalosporins)
  • Treatment: Vancomycin (IV) is the drug of choice
  • Contact precautions: gown + gloves when entering patient's room
  • HA-MRSA (hospital-acquired) vs. CA-MRSA (community-acquired)

VRSA — Vancomycin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus:

  • Also resistant to vancomycin — very limited treatment options
  • Extremely concerning — last-line antibiotics required
🧠 Bacterial Meningitis ▼
EMERGENCYBacterialPotentially Fatal

Definition:

Inflammation of the meninges (3 protective membranes surrounding the brain and spinal cord: dura mater, arachnoid mater, pia mater)

Most Common Pathogens:

  • Streptococcus pneumoniae (most common in adults)
  • Neisseria meningitidis (common in teens/young adults; can cause epidemic outbreaks)
  • Haemophilus influenzae (less common since Hib vaccine)
  • Listeria monocytogenes (neonates, elderly, immunocompromised)

Classic Triad:

  • Fever + Severe headache + Nuchal rigidity (neck stiffness)
  • Plus: photophobia, phonophobia, altered mental status
  • Petechial rash — seen with N. meningitidis (meningococcal), indicates septicemia (very serious)

Assessment Signs for Meningitis:

  • Kernig's Sign — Patient supine, hip flexed to 90°; pain/resistance when you try to extend the knee. Positive = meningitis irritation.
  • Brudzinski's Sign — Patient supine; passively flex the neck forward → knees involuntarily flex. Positive = meningeal irritation.

Diagnosis — Gold Standard: Lumbar Puncture (LP / Spinal Tap)

  • CSF findings in bacterial meningitis: ↑ Protein, ↓ Glucose, ↑ WBC (neutrophils), Positive Gram stain/culture
  • CT scan before LP if any papilledema or focal neurological signs (to rule out ↑ ICP)

Treatment:

  • Immediate antibiotics (do NOT delay for CT/LP if clinically suspicious)
  • Corticosteroids (dexamethasone) to reduce inflammation
  • Isolation precautions: droplet precautions for N. meningitidis
😴 Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) — Mononucleosis ▼
HerpesvirusTransmitted via saliva

Disease: Infectious Mononucleosis ("Mono" / "Kissing Disease")

Pathophysiology:

EBV infects B lymphocytes → immune response activates T lymphocytes → "atypical lymphocytes" on blood smear → lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly

Classic Triad:

  • Fever + Severe sore throat (pharyngitis) + Lymphadenopathy (swollen lymph nodes, especially posterior cervical)

Additional Findings:

  • Splenomegaly (enlarged spleen) — present in ~50%
  • Fatigue (can be profound and prolonged — weeks to months)
  • Hepatomegaly, elevated liver enzymes
  • Rash if amoxicillin/ampicillin given (almost always causes diffuse maculopapular rash)

Serious Complication:

  • Splenic rupture — rare but life-threatening
  • Nursing: Avoid contact sports and strenuous activity for 4–6 weeks until spleen normalizes

Diagnosis:

  • Monospot test (heterophile antibody test)
  • CBC: lymphocytosis with atypical lymphocytes

Treatment:

  • Supportive: rest, hydration, analgesics
  • Avoid amoxicillin/ampicillin (causes rash)
  • Corticosteroids for severe airway obstruction
💊 Expectorants & Mucolytics ▼

Expectorants

Help loosen and expel mucus from the respiratory tract by increasing respiratory tract secretions

  • Guaifenesin (Mucinex) — most common OTC expectorant
  • Encourage: 8–10 glasses of water daily (hydration thins secretions)
  • Used in: bronchitis, URTI, productive cough

Mucolytics

Break down chemical bonds in mucus to make it less viscous (thinner/easier to clear)

  • Acetylcysteine (Mucomyst / NAC) — also used for acetaminophen overdose antidote
  • Has very unpleasant sulfur odor — educate patient this is expected
  • Dornase alfa (Pulmozyme) — used in CF; breaks down DNA in mucus

Scenario 1 — Cellular Adaptation
A 68-year-old male has had urinary retention for years due to an enlarged prostate. His bladder wall has become significantly thickened and stronger over time as it works harder to push against the obstruction. What type of cellular adaptation is occurring in the bladder, and why?
Hypertrophy — The individual smooth muscle cells of the bladder wall are INCREASING IN SIZE in response to the increased workload (pushing against the obstruction). Cell number stays the same. This is a physiological adaptation to increased demand. If the obstruction is relieved, the hypertrophy can reverse. The enlarged prostate itself is likely an example of hyperplasia (increased cell number due to hormonal stimulation).
Scenario 2 — Wound Healing Complication
You are caring for a post-op patient on Day 5 after abdominal surgery. The patient calls you urgently — you arrive to find a loop of bowel visible through the abdominal wound opening. The patient is anxious and pale. What is happening, and what do you do first?
Evisceration — A surgical emergency. The wound has dehisced and internal organs (bowel) are protruding through the opening.

Immediate nursing actions (in order):
1. Stay calm; call for help (activate the team/call surgeon STAT)
2. Cover the exposed bowel with warm, sterile saline-moistened gauze — never push the bowel back
3. Place patient in low Fowler's position with knees slightly bent (reduce tension on abdomen)
4. Do NOT leave the patient
5. Monitor vital signs; anticipate emergency return to OR
Scenario 3 — Inflammation Interpretation
A 22-year-old athlete sprains her ankle playing basketball. Within 20 minutes, the ankle is red, warm, swollen, and painful. She cannot bear weight on it. Her family is worried and wants to give her cold medicine because "she must be getting an infection." How do you explain what is happening?
What she's experiencing is acute inflammation — a normal, protective response to tissue injury (ligament tearing). All 5 cardinal signs are present:
• Redness and heat = vasodilation and ↑ blood flow
• Swelling = ↑ vascular permeability → fluid leaks into tissue
• Pain = prostaglandins and bradykinin stimulating pain receptors
• Loss of function = pain and swelling limit movement

This is NOT an infection. It is the body's immediate repair response. Appropriate management: RICE (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) + NSAIDs if appropriate. The inflammation will subside as the healing process begins.
Scenario 4 — Meningitis Assessment
A 19-year-old college student is brought to the ED with a sudden severe headache (10/10), fever of 103.2°F, neck stiffness, and sensitivity to light. A petechial rash is noted on the trunk. You flex the patient's neck and his knees involuntarily bend. What do you suspect, and what is the first priority?
Suspected bacterial meningitis (likely meningococcal — Neisseria meningitidis) with meningococcemia.

Positive Brudzinski's sign (neck flex → knee flex). Petechial rash suggests septicemia and is an ominous sign.

First priorities:
1. Airway, Breathing, Circulation — stabilize the patient
2. Droplet isolation precautions immediately (meningococcal is spread via droplets)
3. IV antibiotics STAT — do NOT delay for LP; start treatment immediately in a critically ill patient
4. Notify provider for LP, CT if indicated, blood cultures
5. Lumbar puncture is the gold standard diagnostic test — expect: ↑ protein, ↓ glucose, ↑ WBC
Scenario 5 — Genetic Disorder Nursing Care
You are caring for a 10-year-old with Cystic Fibrosis. Before starting airway clearance therapy (chest physiotherapy), the child says she's hungry and wants to eat first. What do you tell her and why?
Explain kindly that she needs to wait to eat until after the CPT is done. Chest physiotherapy (percussion, vibration, postural drainage) should be performed 1 hour before meals.

Why? The therapy involves positional changes that loosen and mobilize large amounts of mucus. If done after eating, the repositioning can trigger vomiting and aspiration of food contents into the lungs — which would be dangerous and counterproductive for someone who already has compromised airways.

She can have a light snack now, then do CPT, then eat her full meal afterward.
Scenario 6 — Botulism vs. Tetanus
Two patients present to the ED. Patient A: stepped on a rusty nail 4 days ago, now has jaw stiffness and painful muscle spasms triggered by noise. Patient B: ate home-canned green beans at a church potluck 3 days ago; now has drooping eyelids, blurred vision, and difficulty swallowing — but no fever. Which toxin is affecting each patient, and what is the key difference in mechanism?
Patient A → Clostridium tetani (Tetanus)
Source: puncture wound (rusty nail, soil contamination). Toxin blocks inhibitory neurotransmitters → muscles cannot relax → SPASTIC paralysis. Lockjaw (trismus) is the hallmark sign.

Patient B → Clostridium botulinum (Botulism)
Source: improperly home-canned food (anaerobic conditions allow spore germination). Toxin blocks ACh release at neuromuscular junction → muscles cannot contract → FLACCID (floppy) paralysis. Descending pattern: cranial nerves first (eyelids, vision, swallowing) → respiratory failure risk. No fever distinguishes it from infection.

Memory trick: Botulism = Block ACh = no contraction = FLOPPY. Tetanus = TENSE (lockjaw).
