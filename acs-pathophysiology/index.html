Acute Coronary Syndrome

An interactive pathophysiology module connecting coronary plaque biology, thrombosis, myocardial ischemia, ECG findings, biomarkers, treatment targets, and clinical reasoning.

Core idea

ACS is acute myocardial ischemia caused most commonly by sudden coronary thrombosis after disruption of an atherosclerotic plaque. The teaching goal is to make students follow the chain from vessel-wall inflammation to bedside signs.

Endothelial injury
→
Atherosclerotic plaque
→
Plaque rupture
→
Platelet-fibrin clot
→
Symptoms, ECG, troponin
Instructor emphasis: Students should stop saying “heart attack equals clogged artery” and instead explain: plaque rupture exposes thrombogenic material, platelets and fibrin form a thrombus, oxygen delivery falls, myocytes switch to anaerobic metabolism, and necrotic cells release troponin.

What students should explain

Why chest pain happens, why troponin rises, why ST elevation matters, and why aspirin/heparin/PCI target different steps.

Best use

Lecture opener, flipped-class activity, exam review, clinical judgment simulation, or pathophysiology-to-nursing-care bridge.

Key vocabulary

Atherosclerosis, plaque rupture, thrombosis, ischemia, necrosis, subendocardial injury, transmural injury, reperfusion.

2. Plaque rupture converts chronic disease into ACS

A stable plaque may narrow flow for months or years. ACS begins when the plaque cap ruptures or erodes and exposes thrombogenic contents to circulating blood.

Cap rupture
→
Collagen/tissue factor exposed
→
Platelet adhesion
→
Platelet activation
→
Aggregation + fibrin
→
Thrombus
Clinical translation: This is why antiplatelet drugs and anticoagulants are central. They do not “heal plaque”; they reduce clot growth and vessel occlusion.

Bedside link: Chest pain can be ischemia. Positive troponin means myocardial cell injury has occurred. The more tissue injured, the greater the risk for pump failure, arrhythmia, and shock.

5. ECG and biomarker timeline

MinutesIschemia begins

Oxygen supply drops below demand. Patients may develop pressure-like chest pain, diaphoresis, dyspnea, nausea, or atypical symptoms.

EarlyECG changes

Hyperacute T waves may appear early. STEMI produces ST elevation when injury is transmural.

HoursTroponin rises

Troponin enters blood after myocardial cell membrane injury. UA stays troponin-negative; NSTEMI/STEMI are troponin-positive.

LaterComplications emerge

Arrhythmias, reduced contractility, heart failure, papillary muscle dysfunction, ventricular septal rupture, and cardiogenic shock can occur depending on infarct size and location.

ECG teaching shortcut

ST elevation = injured full-thickness myocardium until proven otherwise. Tie the leads to the vessel territory: anterior often LAD, lateral often LCX/diagonal, inferior often RCA or LCX depending anatomy.

6. Treatment targets: match the intervention to the mechanism

Aspirin

Blocks thromboxane-mediated platelet activation.

P2Y12 inhibitor

Reduces ADP-driven platelet aggregation.

Heparin

Limits clot propagation through anticoagulation.

Nitroglycerin

Reduces preload and myocardial oxygen demand; also causes vasodilation.

Beta blocker

Reduces heart rate and contractility, lowering oxygen demand when appropriate.

Statin

Stabilizes plaque and reduces future vascular risk.

PCI/reperfusion

Restores blood flow to threatened myocardium, especially urgent in STEMI.

Clinical simulation

Patient: 64-year-old male with crushing substernal chest pain radiating to the left arm, diaphoresis, nausea, BP 86/58, HR 112. Troponin is pending. ECG shows ST elevation in II, III, and aVF.

What is the likely ACS type?⌄

Inferior STEMI. ST elevation in II, III, and aVF indicates acute transmural injury in the inferior wall. RCA occlusion is common, though LCX can also supply the inferior wall in some patients.

Explain the pathophysiology in one chain.⌄

Plaque rupture exposes thrombogenic material → platelets adhere and aggregate → fibrin stabilizes a clot → coronary blood flow falls abruptly → inferior myocardium becomes ischemic → ATP depletion and calcium overload damage myocytes → troponin rises → reduced contractility can cause hypotension and shock.
