Aseptic Technique
What Is Aseptic Technique & Why It Matters

Key Concept

Aseptic technique refers to procedures that prevent the introduction of unwanted microorganisms into a sterile environment or culture. It protects both the sample and the handler.

  • Prevents contamination of cultures with environmental microbes
  • Protects the lab worker from exposure to pathogens
  • Essential in clinical settings for wound care, IV insertion, catheter care
Clinical Pearl: In nursing, aseptic technique is used when setting up sterile fields, inserting catheters, and changing wound dressings. A break in technique can lead to healthcare-acquired infections (HAIs).
Which of these is the PRIMARY purpose of aseptic technique in the microbiology lab?
  • To speed up bacterial growth in cultures
  • To prevent contamination of cultures and protect the worker
  • To kill all bacteria in the lab
  • To identify unknown organisms
Aseptic technique is all about prevention — keeping unwanted microbes out of your sterile work and keeping you safe from pathogens. It does not kill organisms (that's sterilization) or identify them (that's differential testing).

Proper Inoculation Procedure

Inoculating a Culture — Step by Step

#StepWhy?
1Flame the inoculating loop until glowing redSterilizes the loop (incineration)
2Allow the loop to cool briefly (do NOT set on bench)Hot loop kills cells; bench contaminates loop
3Hold tube/plate lid in your hand (never set lid on table)Prevents airborne contamination of lid
4Gently streak the surface — do NOT stab the agarStabbing tears agar and embeds organisms
5Re-flame the loop when doneSterilizes before setting down
6Incubate plates upside down (agar on top)Prevents condensation from dripping onto colonies
Watch Out: "Set the loop on the lab bench after flaming" and "Incubate the agar plate lid side up" are both improper techniques — know why each one is wrong.
Which of these should be AVOIDED when inoculating a bacterial sample?
  • Flame loop until it glows bright red
  • Store the agar plate bottom side up
  • Set the lid on the table so you do not drop it
  • All of the above
Setting the lid on the table exposes the inner sterile surface to environmental contaminants. You should always hold the lid in your hand during inoculation. Flaming the loop and storing plates inverted are both correct practices.

Culture Media
General Purpose vs. Enriched Media

Types of Media by Function

TypePurposeExamples
General PurposeSupports growth of a wide variety of organismsNutrient agar, Tryptic Soy Agar (TSA)
EnrichedContains extra nutrients for fastidious (picky) organismsBlood agar, Chocolate agar
Think of it this way: General purpose media is like a basic cafeteria — most organisms can eat there. Enriched media is like a specialty restaurant with extra ingredients for organisms that need more to grow.
Nutrient agar or tryptic soy agar would be examples of _____ media.
  • Selective
  • Differential
  • General purpose
  • Enriched
Nutrient agar and TSA are the classic general purpose media — they provide basic nutrients that support a wide range of non-fastidious organisms without any selective or differential agents added.
B2
Culture Media
Selective vs. Differential Media

Key Distinction

TypeWhat It DoesExample
SelectiveInhibits growth of certain organisms; allows othersEMB agar (inhibits gram-positive), MacConkey agar
DifferentialDistinguishes between organisms by visible differences (color changes)Blood agar (hemolysis patterns), MacConkey (lactose fermenters turn pink)

Note: Some media can be BOTH selective AND differential (e.g., MacConkey agar).

Key Point: "Using an EMB plate to inhibit growth of gram-positive bacteria and only allowing growth of gram-negative is an example of _____ media." Answer = Selective (it's selecting which organisms can grow).
An EMB plate that inhibits gram-positive bacteria and only allows gram-negative growth is an example of what type of media?
  • Enriched
  • Selective
  • Differential
  • General or all-purpose
Selective media contains ingredients that inhibit certain organisms (here, gram-positive) while allowing others (gram-negative) to grow. The key word is inhibit = selective.

The growth of bacteria on a solid medium occurs by using which of these?
  • Agar plate only
  • Liquid broth
  • Both agar plate and a test tube slant
  • Agar plate, test tube slant, and liquid broth
Both agar plates and test tube slants contain solid media (agar). Liquid broth is not a solid medium. Bacteria grow on the surface of solid media, forming visible colonies.

How Microbes Grow
Binary Fission & Generation Time

Binary Fission

  • The primary way bacteria reproduce — asexual reproduction
  • One parent cell divides into two identical daughter cells
  • Process: DNA replicates → cell elongates → septum forms (FtsZ ring) → cells separate
  • Each daughter receives a complete copy of the genome

Generation (Doubling) Time

  • The time for one parent cell to divide into two daughter cells
  • E. coli: ~20 minutes | M. tuberculosis: 15–20 hours | M. leprae: 14 days
  • Population formula: Nn = N0 × 2n
The time interval from parent cell to two new daughter cells is called the:
  • Binary Fission
  • Doubling or Generation Time
  • Sexual Reproduction
  • Meiosis
Binary fission is the process; the generation/doubling time is the time interval it takes for one division to occur. Don't confuse the event with the measurement of time.
Which of the following is true of cells that undergo binary fission?
  • Two daughter cells are created
  • Only one cell typically lives
  • Not all genetic material is replicated
  • This process includes both prokaryotes and eukaryotes
Binary fission produces two identical daughter cells, each with a complete copy of the DNA. It is specific to prokaryotes (bacteria), not eukaryotes (which use mitosis/meiosis).

Growth Dynamics
The Bacterial Growth Curve — 4 Phases

The Four Phases

PhaseWhat HappensKey Detail
1. Lag PhaseCells adjust, metabolize, but do NOT divideNo increase in cell number; making enzymes/proteins
2. Log (Exponential) PhaseRapid, exponential growthCells most active and vulnerable to antibiotics
3. Stationary PhaseGrowth rate = death rateNutrients depleting, waste accumulating; population plateaus
4. Death (Decline) PhaseDeath rate exceeds growthExponential decrease in living cells
Clinical Connection: Understanding these phases helps predict infection progression. The log phase is when antibiotics are most effective because cells are actively dividing and building cell walls.
The phase where newly inoculated cells are adjusting, metabolizing but NOT growing is the:
  • Lag Phase
  • Log / Exponential Phase
  • Stationary Phase
  • Death Phase
During the lag phase, cells are adapting to their new environment — synthesizing enzymes, taking in nutrients — but the population count stays flat. Think: "lagging behind."
The phase where the rate of multiplication equals the rate of cell death is the:
  • Lag Phase
  • Log / Exponential Phase
  • Stationary Phase
  • Death Phase
In the stationary phase, the birth rate equals the death rate, so the total population remains constant — it "stations" (stays in place). Nutrients are running low and waste is building up.

Temperature Classifications

Temperature Groups

ClassificationOptimal RangeWhere Found
Psychrophile-10°C to 20°CArctic, deep ocean
Mesophile20°C to 45°CHuman body (37°C) — most human pathogens
Thermophile45°C to 80°CHot springs, compost heaps
Extreme Thermophile>80°CHydrothermal vents

Every organism has a minimum, optimal, and maximum growth temperature. The optimal temperature is where growth is fastest.

An organism found in extremely cold environments would be called a:
  • Mesophile
  • Psychrophile
  • Thermophile
  • Extreme thermophile
"Psychro" = cold, "phile" = loving. Psychrophiles thrive in cold environments. Most human pathogens are mesophiles because they prefer body temperature (~37°C).
A microorganism would grow best at a certain temperature. This is called the _____ temperature.
  • minimum
  • maximum
  • optimal
  • sub-optimal
The optimal temperature is the temperature at which a microorganism grows at the fastest rate. Below or above this, growth slows until the minimum or maximum is reached.

Environmental Factors
pH, Osmotic Pressure & Oxygen Requirements

pH

  • Most bacteria that infect humans grow between pH 6.5 and 7.5 (near neutral)
  • Acidophiles prefer acidic environments; alkaliphiles prefer basic

Osmotic Pressure & Halophiles

  • Halophiles thrive in high salt concentrations
  • Barophiles require high atmospheric pressure for growth

Oxygen Requirements

TypeO₂ Requirement
Obligate AerobeRequires O₂ — must have it
Obligate AnaerobeCannot tolerate O₂ — killed by it
Facultative AnaerobeCan grow with or without O₂ (flexible)
Aerotolerant AnaerobeDoesn't use O₂ but isn't harmed by it
MicroaerophileNeeds low levels of O₂

Terminology: Organisms without oxygen = anaerobic; with oxygen = aerobic

An organism that cannot tolerate an oxygen environment is a(n):
  • Obligate aerobe
  • Obligate anaerobe
  • Facultative anaerobe
  • Aerotolerant anaerobe
"Obligate" = required/mandatory. An obligate anaerobe is obligated to live without oxygen — O₂ is toxic to it. Example: Clostridium botulinum (causes botulism in anaerobic conditions like canned food).
A halophile would grow best in:
  • Hot temperatures
  • Cold temperatures
  • Areas of low salt concentration
  • Areas of high salt concentration
"Halo" = salt, "phile" = loving. Halophiles thrive in salty environments like the Dead Sea or salt flats. Don't confuse with thermophile (heat-loving) or psychrophile (cold-loving).
Most bacteria that infect humans grow between which pH?
  • pH 9.5 and 10.5
  • pH 1.5 and 3.5
  • pH 6.5 and 7.5
  • pH 2.0 and 3.0
Human blood has a pH of ~7.35–7.45. Pathogens that infect us have evolved to thrive at near-neutral pH (6.5–7.5), matching the internal environment of the human body.
Organisms that require high atmospheric pressure for growth are called:
  • Halophile
  • Mesophile
  • Thermophile
  • Barophile
"Baro" = pressure (like barometer). Barophiles are found in deep ocean trenches where pressure is extreme. Each suffix tells you what the organism loves.
Anaerobic bacteria require all of the following EXCEPT:
  • ATP
  • Glucose
  • Oxygen
  • All of these are required
By definition, anaerobic means "without oxygen." Anaerobic bacteria still need energy (ATP) and nutrients (glucose) but they do NOT use or require oxygen — in fact, oxygen may be toxic to obligate anaerobes.

Clinical Relevance
Biofilms & Their Clinical Importance

What Is a Biofilm?

  • A community of microorganisms encased in a sticky glycocalyx (slime layer)
  • Protects bacteria from the immune system and antibiotics
  • Resists routine cleaning and sterilization
  • Forms on medical devices (catheters, prosthetics, heart valves)

Where Biofilms Form in the Body

  • On teeth as dental plaque
  • In lungs of patients with cystic fibrosis
  • On cardiac tissue in patients with endocarditis
  • On indwelling catheters → causes HAIs (healthcare-acquired infections)
Nursing Connection: This is why catheter care protocols emphasize regular assessment and prompt removal — biofilms begin forming within hours of insertion, making the infection progressively harder to treat.
Which statement about biofilms is TRUE?
  • Biofilms consist of a single layer of bacteria
  • Biofilms are easily eradicated by antibiotics
  • Biofilms are only found in aquatic environments
  • Biofilms protect bacteria from the immune system and antibiotics
Biofilms are multi-layered communities embedded in a protective slime matrix. This matrix physically shields bacteria from immune cells and prevents antibiotics from penetrating — making biofilm infections notoriously difficult to treat.
