Introduction to Aspirin

Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid, ASA) is one of the most widely used medications in the world. As a future healthcare professional, understanding how medications are synthesized gives you insight into pharmaceutical chemistry and drug development.

History

Aspirin has been called the "wonder drug" of the 20th century. While willow bark (containing salicin) was used for centuries to reduce fever and pain, it wasn't until 1897 that Felix Hoffmann at Bayer synthesized acetylsalicylic acid in a pure, stable form. This discovery revolutionized medicine.

The Chemical Reaction

Salicylic Acid + Acetic Anhydride → Aspirin + Acetic Acid

C₇H₆O₃ + C₄H₆O₃ → C₉H₈O₄ + C₂H₄O₂

This is an esterification reaction where an ester bond is formed between salicylic acid and acetic anhydride, catalyzed by phosphoric acid.

Clinical Application

As nurses, you'll encounter patients taking aspirin for various reasons:

  • Pain relief (analgesic): Headaches, muscle pain
  • Fever reduction (antipyretic): Managing fevers
  • Anti-inflammatory: Arthritis and inflammation
  • Antiplatelet: Preventing heart attacks and strokes (low-dose aspirin)
⚠️ SAFETY CONSIDERATIONS
  • Salicylic acid and acetic anhydride are irritants - avoid skin contact
  • Acetic anhydride has a strong, vinegar-like odor - work in well-ventilated areas
  • Wear safety goggles and gloves at all times
  • Hot water bath can cause burns - handle with care
  • Never taste or consume laboratory-synthesized chemicals

Key Concepts Review

Limiting Reactant: The reactant that is completely consumed first, limiting the amount of product formed.
Theoretical Yield: The maximum amount of product that can be formed based on stoichiometry.
Percent Yield: (Actual Yield / Theoretical Yield) × 100%
Recrystallization: A purification technique where a solid is dissolved in hot solvent and reforms as pure crystals upon cooling.

Experimental Procedure

Materials Needed

Chemicals Equipment
• Salicylic acid (2.0 g)
• Acetic anhydride (5 mL)
• Phosphoric acid (5 drops, catalyst)
• Ethanol
• Distilled water
• Ice 		• 125 mL Erlenmeyer flask
• Hot water bath
• Graduated cylinder
• Stirring rod
• Büchner funnel and filter paper
• Vacuum filtration apparatus
• Thermometer
• Balance

Procedure

1 Weigh salicylic acid (5 min)
Place a 125 mL Erlenmeyer flask on the balance and tare (zero) it. Add approximately 2.0 g of salicylic acid. Record the exact mass in the data table below.
2 Add acetic anhydride (3 min)
Using a graduated cylinder, carefully measure 5 mL of acetic anhydride and add it to the flask containing salicylic acid. Note: Acetic anhydride has a strong odor - avoid inhaling vapors.
3 Add catalyst (2 min)
Add 5 drops of phosphoric acid to the flask using a dropper. This catalyst speeds up the esterification reaction.
4 Heat the reaction mixture (15 min)
Place the Erlenmeyer flask in a hot water bath (75-85°C). Swirl gently every 2-3 minutes. The salicylic acid should completely dissolve. Continue heating for 10-15 minutes to ensure complete reaction.
✅ Lab Partner Check: Has the salicylic acid completely dissolved?
Partner initials:
5 Add water and cool (10 min)
Carefully remove the flask from the water bath. Add 20 mL of distilled water to decompose excess acetic anhydride. Carefully add 50 mL of room temperature water to the flask. Place the flask in an ice bath to crystallize the aspirin. Crystals should begin forming within 5-10 minutes.
6 Vacuum filtration (15 min)
Set up the vacuum filtration apparatus with a Büchner funnel and filter paper. Pour the crystal slurry onto the filter paper and wash the crystals with two 10 mL portions of ice-cold distilled water. Continue vacuum for 5 minutes to dry the crystals.
7 Recrystallization (optional if time permits) (20 min)
Transfer crude aspirin to a clean Erlenmeyer flask. Add minimal hot ethanol (just enough to dissolve). Filter while hot to remove impurities. Allow to cool slowly, then place in ice bath. Filter and dry the purified aspirin crystals.
8 Weigh product (5 min)
Weigh a piece of filter paper and record the mass. Transfer your dried aspirin crystals to the filter paper and weigh again. Calculate the mass of aspirin produced.
9 Observe crystal appearance (5 min)
Examine your aspirin crystals. Note the color, crystal shape, and any other observations.
