Introduction: Why pH Matters in Healthcare

As a future nurse, you'll encounter pH in many clinical situations: blood gas analysis, urine tests, wound care, medication administration, and IV fluid management. Understanding pH is essential for recognizing acid-base imbalances, which can be life-threatening.

🩺 Clinical Insight: Normal blood pH is tightly regulated between 7.35-7.45. A pH below 7.35 (acidosis) or above 7.45 (alkalosis) can indicate serious medical conditions requiring immediate intervention!

What is pH?

pH stands for "power of hydrogen" and measures the concentration of hydrogen ions (H⁺) in a solution. The pH scale ranges from 0 to 14:

0 ← ACIDIC | 7 NEUTRAL | BASIC → 14

  • pH < 7: Acidic (more H⁺ ions)
  • pH = 7: Neutral (equal H⁺ and OH⁻ ions)
  • pH > 7: Basic/Alkaline (fewer H⁺ ions, more OH⁻ ions)
🔬 Chemistry Fact: The pH scale is logarithmic! This means each whole number change represents a 10-fold change in H⁺ concentration. A pH of 4 has 10 times more H⁺ ions than pH 5, and 100 times more than pH 6!

What is a pH Indicator?

A pH indicator is a substance that changes color depending on the pH of the solution. These compounds are weak acids or bases that exist in different colored forms depending on whether they gain or lose protons (H⁺).

Anthocyanins are natural pH indicators found in red cabbage (and other purple/red fruits and vegetables like blueberries, grapes, and red onions). These molecules change structure and color when pH changes:

Clinical Applications of pH

1. Blood pH (Acid-Base Balance)

  • Normal: 7.35-7.45
  • Acidosis: pH < 7.35 (causes: diabetic ketoacidosis, respiratory failure, kidney disease)
  • Alkalosis: pH > 7.45 (causes: hyperventilation, excessive vomiting, diuretic overuse)

2. Urine pH

  • Normal range: 4.5-8.0 (typically around 6.0)
  • Clinical use: Diagnosing kidney stones, urinary tract infections, metabolic disorders
  • Certain medications and diets affect urine pH

3. Wound Care

  • Healthy skin pH: 4.5-5.5 (slightly acidic)
  • Chronic wounds: Often have elevated pH (more alkaline), which impairs healing
  • pH-balanced wound dressings can promote healing

4. Stomach pH

  • Normal stomach acid: pH 1.5-3.5 (very acidic!)
  • Clinical relevance: GERD, ulcers, medication absorption
  • Antacids raise stomach pH to reduce heartburn
⚠️ SAFETY PRECAUTIONS
  • Some acids (like HCl) and bases (like NaOH) can cause chemical burns
  • Wear safety goggles and gloves when handling acids and bases
  • Never taste or smell chemicals directly
  • Clean up spills immediately
  • Hot cabbage water can cause burns - handle carefully

Key Chemistry Concepts

Acid: A substance that donates H⁺ ions (protons) in solution. Examples: HCl, vinegar (acetic acid), lemon juice (citric acid)
Base: A substance that accepts H⁺ ions or donates OH⁻ ions. Examples: NaOH, ammonia, baking soda (sodium bicarbonate)
Neutralization: When an acid and base react to form water and a salt: HCl + NaOH → H₂O + NaCl
Buffer: A solution that resists pH changes when small amounts of acid or base are added. Blood contains several buffer systems!
🎨 Natural pH Indicators in Nature: Many flowers change color based on soil pH! Hydrangeas bloom blue in acidic soil (pH 5-6) and pink in alkaline soil (pH 6.5-7.5). This is due to anthocyanins, the same compounds in red cabbage!

Part 1: Preparing the Cabbage pH Indicator (20 minutes)

1 Chop the cabbage (5 min)
Tear or chop about 1/4 head of red cabbage (approximately 100 g or 2 cups) into small pieces. The smaller the pieces, the more anthocyanins will be extracted.
2 Boil the cabbage (10 min)
Place the cabbage pieces in a 500 mL beaker. Add approximately 300 mL of distilled water. Heat on a hot plate or Bunsen burner until boiling. Reduce heat and simmer for 5-10 minutes. The water should turn deep purple/blue.
3 Strain and cool (5 min)
Carefully remove from heat. Strain the liquid through cheesecloth or a fine strainer into a clean container, removing all cabbage pieces. Allow the indicator solution to cool to room temperature. The solution should be deep purple/blue (approximately neutral pH 7).

Part 2: Creating a pH Standard Scale (20 minutes)

4 Prepare pH standards (15 min)
You'll create solutions of known pH to serve as color standards:
  • pH 2: Add 1 drop of concentrated HCl to 10 mL water (or use lemon juice straight)
  • pH 4: Vinegar (dilute with equal parts water)
  • pH 6: Mix small amounts of vinegar and baking soda solution until pink
  • pH 7: Distilled water (neutral)
  • pH 8: Dissolve 1/4 tsp baking soda in 20 mL water
  • pH 10: Dissolve 1/2 tsp baking soda in 20 mL water
  • pH 12: Diluted ammonia cleaner (1:1 with water)
5 Add cabbage indicator to standards (5 min)
To each pH standard (in a test tube), add approximately 5 mL of your cabbage indicator. Record the color that develops. Arrange tubes in order from lowest to highest pH. Take a photo for your records!

Part 3: Testing Unknown Solutions (35 minutes)

6 Prepare test solutions (10 min)
Select 8-10 household items or chemicals to test. Some suggestions:
  • Carbonated soda
  • Milk
  • Shampoo solution
  • Antacid tablet dissolved in water
  • Vitamin C tablet dissolved in water
  • Hand soap solution
  • Sports drink
  • Coffee
  • Orange juice
  • Tap water
7 Test each solution with cabbage indicator (20 min)
For each test solution:
  1. Place 5-10 mL of the solution in a test tube
  2. Add 5 mL of cabbage indicator
  3. Observe and record the color
  4. Compare to your pH standard scale
  5. Estimate the pH
  6. Test with pH paper to verify
8 Take photos (5 min)
Arrange all your test tubes on white paper and photograph them. This will help with your post-lab analysis and video assignment.

Part 4: Neutralization Demonstration (15 minutes)

9 Acid + Base = Neutral (15 min)
  1. Place 20 mL of vinegar in a beaker
  2. Add 10 mL of cabbage indicator - note the acidic color (pink/red)
  3. Slowly add baking soda solution drop by drop while stirring
  4. Watch the color change from acidic → neutral → basic
  5. Record your observations of the color changes and any gas produced (CO₂!)

Post-Lab Analysis and Questions

Analysis Questions

Question 1: Based on your results, which tested solution was the most acidic? Which was the most basic? How do you know?
Question 2: How accurately did your cabbage indicator estimate pH compared to pH paper? Were there any solutions where the estimates differed significantly? Why might this happen?
Question 3: Explain what happened during the neutralization demonstration at the molecular level. Write the chemical equation for the reaction between acetic acid (vinegar) and sodium bicarbonate (baking soda).
Question 4: Were any of your results surprising? For example, was a solution more acidic or basic than you expected? Explain.
Question 5: The anthocyanins in red cabbage are what type of molecule? Research and explain why they change color at different pH values (hint: think about protonation and deprotonation).
Question 6: Why is it important that pH indicators show different colors across the pH scale rather than just two colors (like litmus paper)?

Clinical Application Questions

Clinical Question 1: A patient comes to the ER with diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA). Their blood gas shows pH 7.10. Explain whether this is acidosis or alkalosis, how serious this is, and what you would expect to see clinically.
Clinical Question 2: You're conducting a urinalysis on a patient. The urine pH is 8.5 (more alkaline than normal). What might this indicate? What questions would you ask the patient?
Clinical Question 3: A patient taking antacids for GERD asks you how they work. Using your knowledge of pH and neutralization, explain to the patient (in simple terms) how antacids relieve heartburn.
Clinical Question 4: Chronic wounds often have an elevated pH (more alkaline than healthy skin). Research and explain why this impairs wound healing and what interventions nurses can use to address this.
Research & Reflection: Blood contains several buffer systems (bicarbonate, phosphate, protein) that maintain pH homeostasis. Research ONE of these buffer systems and explain how it works to resist pH changes. Why is this critical for survival?
