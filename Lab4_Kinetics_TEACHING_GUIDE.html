🧪 The Chemistry — What You Need to Know Cold

The Reaction

CH₃COCH₃ + Br₂ → CH₃COCH₂Br + HBr   (requires H⁺ catalyst)

Acetone reacts with molecular bromine in acidic solution to produce bromoacetone and hydrobromic acid. H⁺ is a catalyst — it's consumed and regenerated, which is why it appears in the rate law but doesn't appear to be "used up."

Why Br₂ Is the Observable

Bromine is orange/yellow in solution. Acetone, HCl, and bromoacetone are all colorless. This means only [Br₂] changes the absorbance at 395 nm — perfect for spectrophotometry. As Br₂ is consumed, the solution becomes colorless.

The Rate Law

rate = −d[Br₂]/dt = k[acetone]ⁿ [H⁺]ᵐ [Br₂]ᵖ

The experiment determines n, m, p, and k. The expected result: n=1, m=1, p=0. So the full rate law is:

rate = k[acetone][H⁺]

The Mechanism — Why Br₂ Is Zero Order (THE KEY INSIGHT)

🔑
This is the conceptual core of the lab. Students will be surprised that changing [Br₂] doesn't change the rate. You need to explain why.

The reaction proceeds in two steps:

1
Enolization (SLOW — rate-determining step):
CH₃COCH₃ + H⁺ → CH₂=C(OH)CH₃ + H⁺ (the "enol" form)
This step requires acetone and H⁺. It does NOT involve Br₂.
2
Bromination of the enol (FAST):
CH₂=C(OH)CH₃ + Br₂ → CH₂BrCOCH₃ + HBr
This step is so fast it doesn't limit the overall rate.

Since the slow step (enolization) controls the rate, and it doesn't involve Br₂ at all, the overall rate is independent of [Br₂]. Doubling [Br₂] doesn't help — the bottleneck is forming the enol, and Br₂ isn't involved in that.

💡
Analogy for students: It's like a car assembly line where painting the car (fast step) depends on having car bodies ready (slow step). Having more paint (= more Br₂) doesn't make cars come off the line faster — they're limited by how fast the bodies are built.

Pseudo-Order Conditions

Acetone (8M stock → 0.8M in reaction) and HCl (2M stock → 0.2M in reaction) are present in massive excess compared to Br₂ (~2×10⁻⁴ M). Their concentrations don't measurably change during the reaction, so we treat them as constants and measure only how Br₂ changes with time.

Getting k' for Each Run

Since p=0, plot [Br₂] vs time for all 3 runs. The slope of each trendline = −k'.

Runk' (slope of [Br₂] vs t)[Acetone][H⁺]
1k'₁ (measure from slope)0.80 M0.20 M
2k'₂ ≈ 2 × k'₁0.80 M0.40 M
3k'₃ ≈ 2 × k'₁1.60 M0.20 M

Determining n and m

k' = k[acetone]ⁿ[H⁺]ᵐ

Compare Runs 1 and 2 (same acetone, double H⁺):

k'₂ / k'₁ = ([H⁺]₂ / [H⁺]₁)ᵐ = (0.40/0.20)ᵐ = 2ᵐ

If k'₂/k'₁ ≈ 2 → m = 1 (first order in H⁺)

Compare Runs 1 and 3 (same H⁺, double acetone):

k'₃ / k'₁ = ([acetone]₃ / [acetone]₁)ⁿ = (1.60/0.80)ⁿ = 2ⁿ

If k'₃/k'₁ ≈ 2 → n = 1 (first order in acetone)

Calculating k

k = k'₁ / ([acetone]₁ × [H⁺]₁) = k'₁ / (0.80 × 0.20) = k'₁ / 0.16

Calculate k from all three runs and average. Units: L²/(mol²·s) or M⁻²s⁻¹ (since rate law is rate = k[acetone][H⁺] and rate is in M/s).

📐
Units check: rate (M/s) = k × [M] × [M] → k has units of M⁻¹s⁻¹ or L mol⁻¹ s⁻¹

📊 Expected Results Summary

Reaction Orders

  • p = 0 — Zero order in Br₂ ([Br₂] vs time is linear)
  • n = 1 — First order in acetone
  • m = 1 — First order in H⁺
rate = k[acetone][H⁺]

Rate Comparisons

  • Run 2 / Run 1 ≈ 2 (double H⁺ → double rate)
  • Run 3 / Run 1 ≈ 2 (double acetone → double rate)
  • k ≈ 1–5 × 10⁻³ M⁻¹s⁻¹ (varies with temperature)
⚠️
Data quality issues that produce wrong results: (1) Not mixing fast enough — first absorbance reading is too low because reaction already started. (2) Poor cuvette positioning — variable readings. (3) Air bubbles in cuvette. If students get p≠0, have them check if their [Br₂] vs time graph shows an obvious curve vs. straight line.

🧠 Common Student Misconceptions — Address These

❌ MISCONCEPTION 1

"More Br₂ should make the reaction faster because there's more reactant."

✅ CORRECTION

Rate depends on the slow step (enolization), not the fast step (bromination). Br₂ is consumed in the fast step. Adding more of it doesn't speed up the bottleneck. This is what "zero order" means — concentration doesn't affect rate.

❌ MISCONCEPTION 2

"If p=0, that means Br₂ isn't involved in the reaction."

✅ CORRECTION

Br₂ is absolutely involved — it's a reactant that gets consumed. Zero order means its concentration doesn't affect the rate. It still reacts; it just doesn't control how fast.

❌ MISCONCEPTION 3

"The R² closest to 1.00 must be 1.000." / "I can't tell which graph is the straightest."

✅ CORRECTION

They don't need to be 1.000. They compare: typically [Br₂] vs t gives R²≈0.999, ln[Br₂] vs t gives R²≈0.97, 1/[Br₂] vs t gives R²≈0.85. The first one wins clearly.

❌ MISCONCEPTION 4

"The slope equals k." (confusing k' with k)

✅ CORRECTION

The slope = −k' where k' = k[acetone]ⁿ[H⁺]ᵐ. You still need to divide k' by the concentrations of acetone and H⁺ to get the true rate constant k.

❌ MISCONCEPTION 5

"H⁺ is a reactant, so it should be consumed and not appear in the final rate law."

✅ CORRECTION

H⁺ is a catalyst. It's consumed in the first step and regenerated in the second step. Net effect: H⁺ is not consumed overall, but its concentration still affects the rate of enolization.

❓ Anticipated Discussion Questions & Model Answers

Q1: Why do we need excess acetone and HCl?

Answer: To isolate the effect of [Br₂] on the rate. If acetone and H⁺ were at similar concentrations to Br₂, all three would change during the reaction, making it impossible to determine individual orders. Excess concentrations stay essentially constant, letting us observe only [Br₂] changing.

Q2: Why is Br₂ measured at 395 nm?

Answer: 395 nm is in the visible range where Br₂ absorbs strongly (it appears orange/yellow). The other species — acetone, HCl, bromoacetone — are colorless and don't absorb at this wavelength. This makes [Br₂] the only contributor to absorbance, allowing Beer's Law to apply cleanly.

Q3: What does a straight line in [Br₂] vs time tell us?

Answer: It tells us the rate of disappearance of Br₂ is constant — it doesn't depend on [Br₂] itself. This is the definition of zero-order kinetics with respect to Br₂.

Q4: Why do we check three different plots (0th, 1st, 2nd order)?

Answer: We can't know the order in advance. We check which mathematical transformation gives the most linear plot (highest R²), then assign the order based on which linearization worked.

Q5: What would happen if you ran the reaction at a higher temperature?

Answer: The rate would increase. The orders (n, m, p) would remain the same, but k would increase (Arrhenius equation: k = A·e^(−Ea/RT)). The enolization step has an activation energy, so higher temperature means faster enolization → faster overall reaction.
