## Pre-Lab Calculation Answers

### Low Dose Solution (0.25 m NaCl):

**Step 1:** Calculate moles of NaCl
$$\text{moles} = \frac{1.46 \text{ g}}{58.44 \text{ g/mol}} = \textbf{0.0250 mol}$$

**Step 2:** Calculate molality
$$m = \frac{0.0250 \text{ mol}}{0.100 \text{ kg}} = \textbf{0.25 m}$$

**Step 3:** Predict freezing point depression
$$\Delta T = 2 \times 1.86 \times 0.25 = \textbf{0.93°C}$$

**Step 4:** Predict actual freezing point
$$T_{freezing} = 0°C - 0.93°C = \textbf{-0.93°C}$$

### Complete Solutions Table

| Solution | NaCl Mass | Molality | i | ΔT (°C) | Predicted FP (°C) |
|----------|-----------|----------|---|---------|-------------------|
| Control | 0 g | 0 m | — | 0 | 0°C |
| Low Dose | 1.46 g | 0.25 m | 2 | 0.93 | -0.93°C |
| Standard | 2.92 g | 0.50 m | 2 | 1.86 | -1.86°C |
| High Dose | 5.85 g | 1.00 m | 2 | 3.72 | -3.72°C |

**Detailed Calculations:**

**Standard Dose (0.50 m):**
- Moles = 2.92 g ÷ 58.44 g/mol = 0.0500 mol
- Molality = 0.0500 mol ÷ 0.100 kg = 0.50 m
- ΔT = 2 × 1.86 × 0.50 = 1.86°C
- FP = 0°C - 1.86°C = **-1.86°C**

**High Dose (1.00 m):**
- Moles = 5.85 g ÷ 58.44 g/mol = 0.100 mol
- Molality = 0.100 mol ÷ 0.100 kg = 1.00 m
- ΔT = 2 × 1.86 × 1.00 = 3.72°C
- FP = 0°C - 3.72°C = **-3.72°C**

### Common Issues Students Encounter:

1. **Supercooling:** Temperature drops below freezing point, then jumps back up when ice forms. Solution: Look for the plateau AFTER the temperature "spike"

2. **No clear plateau:** Stirring too vigorous, or bath not cold enough. Solution: Ensure bath is -10°C or colder, stir GENTLY

3. **Readings drift:** Thermometer not centered in solution, touching walls. Solution: Re-position thermometer

4. **Higher than expected FP:** Salt not fully dissolved, or water evaporated. Solution: Ensure complete dissolution before cooling

## Pharmaceutical Application Questions — ANSWER KEY

### Question 1: The Cold Chain Problem (Vaccine Storage)

**Model Answer:**

For designing a vaccine diluent intended for ultra-cold storage (-70°C), you would prefer a **LOWER** solute concentration (or a carefully selected concentration that doesn't significantly depress the freezing point).

**Reasoning:**
1. At -70°C, even solutions with high freezing point depression will freeze
2. The goal isn't to prevent freezing entirely—it's to **control how the solution freezes**
3. HIGH solute concentrations create problems:
   - As ice forms, the remaining liquid becomes more concentrated (freeze concentration)
   - This can damage biological components through osmotic stress
   - Uneven freezing can create damaging ice crystal patterns

4. Optimal diluents use:
   - Cryoprotectants like trehalose or sucrose that protect proteins
   - Controlled concentrations that freeze uniformly
   - Buffer systems that maintain pH during freeze/thaw

**Clinical note:** The Pfizer COVID-19 vaccine used sucrose as a cryoprotectant in its formulation, not to prevent freezing but to protect the mRNA during the freeze-thaw process.

### Question 2: Isotonic Eye Drops

**a) Classification:**
The eye drop with FP = -0.35°C is **HYPOTONIC** compared to tears (FP = -0.52°C).

**Explanation:** A less negative freezing point means fewer dissolved particles, therefore lower osmotic pressure (hypotonic).

**b) Stinging potential:**
Yes, this eye drop would likely cause **stinging and discomfort**.

**Mechanism:** Water will flow FROM the hypotonic eye drop INTO the cells of the cornea and conjunctiva (osmosis flows from low to high solute concentration). This causes the cells to swell, triggering pain receptors.

**Clinical relevance:** Pharmaceutical companies use freezing point depression as a quality control measure to ensure eye drops are isotonic before release.

### Question 3: Pediatric Liquid Medication for Alaska

**a) Survival during transport:**
**NO**, the medication will NOT survive transport. With a freezing point of -2°C and transport temperatures potentially reaching -30°C, the medication will freeze solid.

**b) Solutions using ΔT = ikm:**

To lower the freezing point further, you could:

1. **Increase m (molality):** Add more dissolved solute. However, this changes the formulation's osmolarity and may affect taste, stability, or efficacy.

2. **Use a solute with higher i value:** Instead of glucose (i=1), use a carefully selected ionic compound. However, this significantly changes the formulation.

3. **Use a different solvent system:** Add co-solvents like propylene glycol or glycerin that have much lower freezing points. This is the most common pharmaceutical approach.

**Practical solution:** Most pharmaceutical companies solve this by:
- Adding 5-20% propylene glycol (FP = -59°C) or glycerin (FP = -18°C)
- Using insulated packaging with phase-change materials
- Specifying shipping temperature requirements (controlled cold chain)

### Question 4: The Diabetes Connection

**Model Answer:**

More glucose molecules = lower freezing point because freezing point depression is a **colligative property** — it depends on the NUMBER of dissolved particles, not their identity.

**Step-by-step explanation:**

1. In diabetic blood, glucose concentration is elevated (normal: 70-100 mg/dL; diabetic: may exceed 200 mg/dL)

2. Each glucose molecule in solution interferes with water molecules trying to form ice crystals

3. More glucose = more "roadblocks" = more energy must be removed (lower temperature) for water to organize into ice

4. Using the equation: ΔT = i × kf × m
   - i = 1 for glucose (doesn't dissociate)
   - Higher glucose concentration means higher molality (m)
   - Higher m means higher ΔT
   - Higher ΔT means lower (more negative) freezing point

**Clinical application:** Osmometry (measuring freezing point depression) can be used as a diagnostic tool. Blood osmolality correlates with freezing point and can indicate:
- Diabetes mellitus (high glucose)
- Dehydration
- Electrolyte imbalances
- Toxic alcohol ingestion

### Question 5: Sports Drink Formulation

**Model Answer:**

To create a sports drink that won't freeze at -5°C, tastes acceptable, and replaces electrolytes:

**Strategy 1: Use a mixture of solutes**
- Electrolytes (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻) with i = 2 contribute more to freezing point depression per mole
- Sugars (glucose, sucrose) with i = 1 add sweetness and energy without as much "saltiness"
- The combination provides freezing protection with better taste

**Calculation for -5°C freezing point:**
Required ΔT = 5°C

If using only NaCl: m = ΔT/(i × kf) = 5/(2 × 1.86) = 1.34 m

That's about 78 g NaCl per kg water — VERY salty!

**Better approach using mixture:**
- 0.3 m NaCl: ΔT = 2 × 1.86 × 0.3 = 1.12°C
- 0.3 m KCl: ΔT = 2 × 1.86 × 0.3 = 1.12°C
- 1.5 m sucrose: ΔT = 1 × 1.86 × 1.5 = 2.79°C
- **Total ΔT ≈ 5.03°C** ✓

This combination:
- Provides electrolyte replacement (Na⁺, K⁺, Cl⁻)
- Freezes below -5°C
- Has sweetness from sucrose to mask saltiness
- Provides energy from sugar

**Real-world note:** Commercial sports drinks like Gatorade already use this principle, though they typically aren't designed for extreme cold storage.

## Critical Analysis Section — ANSWER KEY

### Error Analysis

**Sources of Error (with effects):**

| Error Source | Effect on Observed FP | Explanation |
|--------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Supercooling occurred | Initially TOO LOW, then corrects | Temperature drops past true FP before ice nucleates |
| Salt not fully dissolved | TOO HIGH (less negative) | Fewer particles in solution than calculated |
| Water evaporated | TOO LOW (more negative) | Same solute, less solvent = higher concentration |
| Thermometer calibration off | Could be either direction | Systematic error shifts all readings |
| Ice bath not cold enough | May not observe true FP | Solution doesn't cool enough to freeze |
| Reading taken before plateau | TOO HIGH | Cooling still occurring, not yet at equilibrium |

### Supercooling Discussion

**What students should observe:**
Supercooling often appears as a temperature dip below the true freezing point, followed by a sudden rise as ice formation releases latent heat (heat of fusion).

**Graph pattern:**
```
Temp
  |
  |  \
  |   \
  |    \_____  ← True freezing point (plateau)
  |     ↑
  |    / ← Supercooling dip
  |   /
  |_______________Time
```

**How it affects data:**
If students read the lowest temperature (supercooling dip), their observed FP will be TOO LOW (more negative than true value). They should look for the plateau that follows.

### Big Picture Questions

**Q3: Why is repeated freeze-thaw worse than constant cold storage?**

**Model Answer:**
1. **Ice crystal damage:** Each freezing cycle forms ice crystals that can mechanically damage emulsions, suspensions, proteins, and cell membranes

2. **Concentration effects:** As ice forms, solutes concentrate in remaining liquid (cryoconcentration). This creates localized regions of high/low concentration that stress active ingredients

3. **Physical separation:** Emulsions can "break" when oil/water phases separate during freezing. They may not properly remix upon thawing

4. **pH shifts:** Freeze-concentration can cause local pH extremes that denature proteins or degrade drugs

5. **Container stress:** Expansion/contraction cycles stress container seals, potentially compromising sterility

**Clinical example:** Insulin loses potency after freeze-thaw cycles because the protein structure is damaged. Diabetic patients are warned never to let insulin freeze.

---

**Q4: Why can't we just add tons of salt to IV fluids?**

**Model Answer:**

Adding excessive solute to prevent freezing would make the solution **hypertonic**, causing severe patient harm:

1. **Cellular dehydration:** Hypertonic solutions draw water OUT of cells by osmosis, causing cells to shrink (crenation)

2. **Electrolyte imbalance:** Excessive sodium (hypernatremia) causes:
   - Neurological symptoms (confusion, seizures)
   - Cardiac arrhythmias
   - Potential brain hemorrhage (as brain cells shrink)

3. **Hemolysis risk:** Red blood cells are particularly sensitive; severe tonicity changes cause them to burst or shrink

**The balance:** IV fluids must be ISOTONIC with blood plasma (freezing point ≈ -0.56°C). This is non-negotiable for patient safety. Preventing freezing must use other methods (controlled storage, insulated shipping).

---

**Q5: Organ Preservation Applications**

**Model Answer:**

Understanding freezing point depression could advance organ preservation by:

1. **Designing vitrification solutions:** Instead of freezing (which forms damaging crystals), organs could be cooled to a glass-like state (vitrification). This requires precise solute concentrations.

2. **Supercooling technology:** Keeping organs below 0°C but unfrozen extends preservation time. Understanding colligative properties helps optimize solutions that resist ice nucleation.

3. **Cryoprotectant optimization:** Balancing effective freezing point depression with acceptable toxicity levels requires understanding how different compounds contribute to total osmolarity.

4. **Thawing protocols:** Controlled warming rates combined with appropriate solutions prevent damage during the return to physiological temperatures.

**Current research:** Scientists are working on methods to preserve organs at -4°C to -6°C in supercooled state, potentially extending liver preservation from 12 hours to 27+ hours.

## Common Student Misconceptions

| Misconception | Reality | How to Address |
|--------------|---------|----------------|
| "More salt = colder ice" | Salt lowers melting/freezing point, doesn't make ice "colder" | Explain energy absorption during phase change |
| "Molarity and molality are the same" | Molarity = mol/L solution; Molality = mol/kg solvent | Emphasize temperature independence of molality |
| "All electrolytes have i=2" | Different electrolytes have different i values | Practice with CaCl₂, AlCl₃ examples |
| "The lowest temperature is the freezing point" | Supercooling can go lower | Show example graph with supercooling dip |
