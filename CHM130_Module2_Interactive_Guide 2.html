Atomic Structure

Key Topic
📖 Explanation

Every atom has three parts. Think of it like a hospital room:

ParticleChargeLocationHow to Find
Protons (p⁺)Positive (+)Nucleus= Atomic number
Neutrons (n⁰)Neutral (0)Nucleus= Mass # − Atomic #
Electrons (e⁻)Negative (−)Electron cloud= Protons (neutral atom)

The atomic number is the element's permanent ID — change it and you've got a different element entirely. The mass number can change (that's what isotopes are).

Atomic Size Trends: Big atoms live in the bottom-left corner of the periodic table. Going down a group = bigger (more shells). Going left = bigger (fewer protons pulling inward).

🧠 Memory Aid
The Hospital Wristband
PEN

Protons = Positive  |  Electrons = nEgative  |  Neutrons = Neutral

Think of the atomic number like a patient's wristband at the hospital — it never changes. You can gain weight (mass number), but your hospital ID stays the same. Change the ID and you're literally a different person.

✏️ Example Problem
Find all particles for Calcium-44
1
Look up Calcium on the periodic table → Atomic number = 20 → 20 protons
2
Mass number is in the name: "Calcium-44"
3
Neutrons = 44 − 20 = 24 neutrons
4
Neutral atom → electrons = protons = 20 electrons
Calcium-44: 20 p⁺, 24 n⁰, 20 e⁻
🎯 Your Turn

How many neutrons does Chlorine-37 have?

Chlorine atomic # = 17. Neutrons = 37 − 17 = 20 neutrons.

Molecular Weight & Molar Mass

Formula
📖 Explanation

Molar mass = the weight of ONE mole of a substance in g/mol. You find it by shopping the periodic table: look up each element's atomic mass, multiply by how many atoms you have, add it all up.

Once you know the molar mass, you can convert between grams and moles:

grams = moles × molar mass   |   moles = grams ÷ molar mass
🧠 Memory Aid
The Grocery Receipt
G = M × MM

Molar mass is like checking out at a grocery store. Each element has a price tag (atomic mass). You scan each item, multiply by quantity, and your receipt total is the molar mass.

Picture the GMM triangle like a pizza slice: Grams on top, Moles and Molar Mass on the bottom. Cover what you need — what's left is your formula.

✏️ Example Problem
Practice: Find the molar mass of Na₂SO₄
1
Na₂SO₄ = 2 Sodium + 1 Sulfur + 4 Oxygen
2
Na = 22.99 g/mol  |  S = 32.07 g/mol  |  O = 16.00 g/mol
3
2(22.99) + 32.07 + 4(16.00) = 45.98 + 32.07 + 64.00 = 142.05 g/mol
Na₂SO₄ = 142.05 g/mol
Practice: A lab needs 0.35 mol of NaCl. How many grams?
1
Molar mass of NaCl: Na(22.99) + Cl(35.45) = 58.44 g/mol
2
grams = 0.35 mol × 58.44 g/mol = 20.45 g
20.45 grams of NaCl
🎯 Your Turn

How many grams in 0.80 mol of CaCO₃?

CaCO₃ molar mass: 40.08 + 12.01 + 3(16.00) = 100.09 g/mol. Grams = 0.80 × 100.09 = 80.07 g

The Mole Concept

Key Topic
📖 Explanation

A mole is just a counting word. "Dozen" means 12. "Mole" means 6.02 × 10²³. That's it.

The subscript in a chemical formula tells you the mole ratio. In C₉H₈O₄ (aspirin), every 1 mole of aspirin contains 8 moles of hydrogen atoms.

The trick: moles of element = moles of compound × subscript.

🧠 Memory Aid
The Med Supply Closet

Nurses don't count individual gauze pads — they order by the box. Each box has a set number inside.

Chemists don't count atoms — they order by the mole. And the subscript on the formula tells you how many are packed in each "box."

If each box of a wound care kit has 8 gauze pads, then 4 boxes = 4 × 8 = 32 gauze pads. Same logic: 4 mol aspirin = 4 × 8 = 32 mol hydrogen.

✏️ Example Problem
Practice: 4 mol of aspirin (C₉H₈O₄) → how many moles of oxygen?
1
Subscript on O = 4
2
4 mol × 4 = 16 mol O
16 moles of oxygen
🎯 Your Turn

5 mol of caffeine (C₈H₁₀N₄O₂). How many moles of nitrogen?

Subscript on N = 4. So 5 × 4 = 20 mol N

Avogadro's Number

Key Topic
📖 Explanation
1 mole = 6.02 × 10²³ particles

Two conversions — that's all you need:

Molecules → Moles: DIVIDE by 6.02 × 10²³ (big → small)

Moles → Molecules: MULTIPLY by 6.02 × 10²³ (small → big)

🧠 Memory Aid
Mole Day: 6:02 on 10/23
6.02 × 10²³

Chemists actually celebrate Mole Day on October 23rd (10/23) at 6:02 AM.

Direction trick: Think of it like hospital shift changes. Going from a BIG number (molecules, like a packed ER) to a SMALL number (moles, like a summary count) → you DIVIDE. Going from a SMALL summary back out → you MULTIPLY.

✏️ Example Problem
Practice: 3.01 × 10²⁴ molecules of H₂O → how many moles?
1
Big → small, so divide
2
3.01 × 10²⁴ ÷ 6.02 × 10²³ = ~5.0 mol
≈ 5.0 moles of H₂O
🎯 Your Turn

9.03 × 10²³ molecules of NH₃. How many moles?

9.03 × 10²³ ÷ 6.02 × 10²³ = ~1.5 mol NH₃

Reactants vs. Products

Key Topic
📖 Explanation
REACTANTS → PRODUCTS

Left side = Reactants (what goes IN). Right side = Products (what comes OUT).

🧠 Memory Aid
The Kitchen Rule

Reactants are your ingredients. Products are the finished meal.

You READ left to right — Reactants are what you READ first.

Eggs + flour + sugar → cake. Nobody calls the cake an ingredient. "What are the substances produced on the right side?" → Products. Always.

✏️ Example Problem
Identify reactants and products: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
1
Reactants (left): H₂ and O₂
2
Products (right): H₂O
Reactants = H₂, O₂  |  Products = H₂O

Balancing Chemical Equations

Key Topic
📖 Explanation

Atoms don't appear or vanish. What goes in must come out — just rearranged. Same number of each atom on both sides.

Strategy: Balance metals first, nonmetals next, save H and O for last.

Golden rule: NEVER change subscripts. Subscripts change what the molecule is. Coefficients (the big numbers in front) change how many.

🧠 Memory Aid
The Hospital Census

Every patient who checks IN must also check OUT. No disappearances, no clones.

If 5 patients walk into the ER, 5 must be accounted for in discharge. You can move them to different rooms (rearrange into new compounds), but the head count stays the same. Changing a subscript is like changing a patient's identity — that's fraud. Only change coefficients (how many of each compound you have).

✏️ Example Problem
Balance: Al + O₂ → Al₂O₃
1
Count: Left: Al=1, O=2 | Right: Al=2, O=3. Not balanced!
2
Fix Al: Put 4 in front of Al (left), 2 in front of Al₂O₃ (right) → Al: 4=4
3
Fix O: Right has 2×3=6 O. Put 3 in front of O₂ → Left: 3×2=6 O
4
Check: Al: 4=4 ✓ O: 6=6 ✓
4 Al + 3 O₂ → 2 Al₂O₃
🎯 Your Turn

Balance: Fe + Cl₂ → FeCl₃. What are the coefficients?

Start with Cl: need 6 on each side. Put 3 in front of Cl₂ (6 Cl left) and 2 in front of FeCl₃ (6 Cl right). Then 2 Fe on left. Check: Fe 2=2 ✓ Cl 6=6 ✓. Coefficients: 2, 3, 2

The #1 Chemistry Mnemonic
OIL RIG

Think of an oil rig worker: he LOSES his hard hat on a windy day (Oxidation = Loss). His buddy finds it and GAINS a free hat (Reduction = Gain). Or remember LEO the lion says GER: Lose Electrons = Oxidation, Gain Electrons = Reduction.

OIL RIG → Oxidation Is Loss

💔

🔄

🧠 Memory Aid
The Dating App
Some Dudes Can't Sing Duets

Synthesis, Decomposition, Combustion, Single Replacement, Double Replacement

Quick ID trick: 2→1 = Synthesis (coming together). 1→2+ = Decomposition (breaking up). Element + Compound = Single Replacement (third wheel). Compound + Compound = Double Replacement (partner swap).

Endothermic vs. Exothermic

Key Topic
📖 Explanation

🔥 Exothermic

  • Energy is released
  • Surroundings get warmer
  • Fire, breathing, hot packs

🧊 Endothermic

  • Energy is absorbed
  • Surroundings get cooler
  • Ice packs, evaporation, melting
🧠 Memory Aid
The ER Doors
EXO = EXIT  |  ENDO = ENTER

Exothermic: energy exits the reaction (like a patient being discharged — heat leaves and warms the hallway).

Endothermic: energy enters the reaction (like an admission — the reaction sucks heat from the room, so the room feels cold).

The tricky part: "endothermic cools surroundings" sounds backwards. But the reaction is an energy vacuum — it's pulling heat FROM the room into itself. That's why an instant cold pack gets cold. It's not making cold; it's stealing your warmth.

✏️ Example Problem
Classify each scenario
1
"A campfire warms the campsite" → Exothermic (heat exits)
2
"Boiling water on the stove" → Endothermic (heat absorbed from burner)
3
"Dissolving ammonium nitrate makes an instant cold pack" → Endothermic
4
"A hand warmer heats up in your pocket" → Exothermic (releases heat)

Stoichiometry (Equation Conversions)

Key Topic
📖 Explanation

The coefficients in a balanced equation give you the mole ratio. This ratio is your conversion bridge.

N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃
1 mol N₂ : 3 mol H₂ : 2 mol NH₃
🧠 Memory Aid
GPS Navigation
Grams A → Moles A → Moles B → Grams B

Think of it like driving with GPS:

🛣️ On-ramp (grams → moles): Divide by molar mass to enter the highway
🔀 Highway (moles A → moles B): Use the coefficient ratio to change lanes
🛣️ Off-ramp (moles → grams): Multiply by molar mass to exit

If you already start in moles, skip the on-ramp. If you only need moles at the end, skip the off-ramp.

✏️ Example Problem
Practice: 4 mol NH₃ produced → how many mol H₂ were needed?
1
Ratio from equation: 2 mol NH₃ : 3 mol H₂
2
4 × (3/2) = 6.0 mol H₂
6.0 mol H₂
Practice: 2 mol N₂ → how many grams NH₃?
1
Ratio: 1 mol N₂ → 2 mol NH₃. So 2 mol N₂ = 4 mol NH₃
2
Molar mass NH₃: 14.01 + 3(1.008) = 17.03 g/mol
3
4 mol × 17.03 = 68.12 g
68.12 grams of NH₃

🧠 Memory Aid
The Elevator Analogy

🏢 Solid = Packed elevator at rush hour. Everyone's squished in place, vibrating with impatience, but nobody can move. HIGH density.

🍸 Liquid = Cocktail party. People slide past each other, take the shape of the room, but stay on the same floor.

🎈 Gas = Kids at recess. Running everywhere, filling whatever space is available, easy to push closer together (compress).

Quick shortcut: "Definite shape?" = Solid. "High density?" = Solid. "Compressible?" = Gas.

✏️ Example Problem
Practice: "Which state of matter can be compressed?"
1
Solids → particles locked tight. Can't squeeze them closer.
2
Liquids → particles touching but sliding. Still not compressible.
3
Gases → particles spread far apart. Lots of space to push them closer.
Gas — the only compressible state

Gas Laws

Key Topic
📖 Explanation

Ideal Gas Law:

PV = nRT
P = atm | V = L | n = moles | R = 0.0821 | T = Kelvin

⚠️ ALWAYS convert to Kelvin first: TK = T°C + 273. Forgetting this = wrong answer every time.

Pressure conversion: 1 atm = 760 mmHg. To convert mmHg → atm: divide by 760.

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures:

PTotal = PA + PB + ...

Each gas contributes its share. Missing one gas? Subtract the others from the total.

🧠 Memory Aid
Two Tricks That Cover All Gas Law Questions
PIVNERT (say it: "piv-nert")

That's PV = nRT out loud. Say it five times and you'll never forget.

Dalton's Law = "Everyone splits the check."

At a restaurant, each person pays their share (partial pressure). Add everyone's share = total bill (total pressure). One friend forgot their wallet? Total minus everyone else's share = what that friend owes. It's always just addition or subtraction.

✏️ Example Problem
Practice: 1.50 mol gas in a 3.00 L tank at 40.0°C → Find pressure
1
Convert T: 40.0 + 273 = 313 K
2
P = nRT/V = (1.50)(0.0821)(313) / 3.00
3
P = 38.55 / 3.00 = 12.8 atm
P = 12.8 atm
Practice: A tire gauge reads 380 mmHg → convert to atm
1
380 ÷ 760 = 0.500 atm
0.500 atm
🎯 Your Turn

Helium + Nitrogen mixture: Ptotal = 1.25 atm, PHe = 0.45 atm. Find PN₂.

PN₂ = 1.25 − 0.45 = 0.80 atm. Just subtraction.
