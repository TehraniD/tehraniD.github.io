Understanding Heat of Fusion

◇

Solid (Ice)

Molecules locked in hexagonal crystal. Hydrogen bonds hold structure rigid.

➡️ +333.55 J/g

Melting (Fusion)

Energy breaks H-bonds. Temperature stays at 0°C until ALL bonds broken.

∘

Liquid (Water)

Molecules free to move. Some H-bonds remain but constantly break and reform.

⚷ The Key Insight

Melting 1 g of ice at 0°C requires 333.55 J — almost the same energy as heating 1 g of water from 0°C all the way to 80°C! All that energy goes into breaking intermolecular bonds, NOT increasing temperature.

Water's Energy Numbers

Process Energy (J/g) Energy (kJ/mol)
Heating ice (per °C) 2.09 0.0376
Melting ice (ΔHf) 333.55 6.01
Heating water (per °C) 4.184 0.0753
Boiling water (ΔHv) 2,260 40.7

The Calorimetry Equations

1Heat transfer (no phase change):

Q = m × c × ΔT

where cwater = 4.184 J/(g·°C) and ΔT = Tf − Ti

2Conservation of energy:

Qice = −Qwarm water

Energy lost by warm water = Energy gained by ice

3Heat of fusion:

ΔHf = Qice / mice

Theoretical value: 333.55 J/g

Real-World Applications

🏥

Ice Packs

Absorb 333 J/g from swollen tissue — far more effective than a cold towel.

🌍

Climate Buffer

Polar ice absorbs enormous energy as it melts, slowing global temperature rise.

◇

Coolers

Ice keeps food at 0°C until ALL ice melts — the phase change absorbs all the heat.

🍦

Salt + ice gets cold enough to freeze cream because melting ice pulls energy from the cream.

⚠ Error Analysis Guide

Understanding why your answer differs from 333.55 J/g is more important than getting it "right."

◇ Excess Water on Ice Surface

↓ Makes ΔHf TOO LOW

Liquid water on the ice gets counted as "ice mass" but doesn't need fusion energy to melt. The denominator (mice) is too large → ΔHf = Q/m is too small.

Prevention: Pat ice dry with paper towels before adding to calorimeter.

Δt Heat Loss to Surroundings

↑ Makes ΔHf TOO HIGH

Warm water loses heat to both the ice AND the air/calorimeter walls. We attribute ALL heat loss to ice → Qice is overestimated → ΔHf is too high.

Prevention: Use water only ~10°C above room temp. Work quickly. Keep lid on.

✻ Ice Below 0°C (from freezer)

↑ Makes ΔHf TOO HIGH

Sub-zero ice first absorbs energy to warm TO 0°C, then absorbs fusion energy. We count all of it as fusion → ΔHf appears too high.

Prevention: Use ice from an ice-water slurry (guaranteed to be at 0°C).

🥄 Poor Stirring

↕ Could go EITHER way

Without stirring, the thermometer reads a local temperature, not the true average. If it's near warm water → Tf too high → ΔHf too low. If near ice → ΔHf too high.

Prevention: Stir continuously and gently throughout the experiment.

⏱️ Reading Tf Too Early

↑ Makes ΔHf TOO HIGH

If ice hasn't fully melted, temperature is still dropping. You record a Tf that's higher than the true equilibrium → |ΔT| appears larger → Q and ΔHf too high.

Prevention: Wait until Tf is stable for 30+ seconds with all ice melted.

📝 Practice Quiz

Test your understanding before and after the lab. Click an answer to check it!

1. During the melting of ice at 0°C, the added energy goes into:

a) Increasing the kinetic energy of water molecules
b) Breaking hydrogen bonds between water molecules in the crystal
c) Increasing the temperature of the ice
d) Converting water to steam
✓ During a phase change, temperature remains constant. ALL energy goes into breaking the intermolecular hydrogen bonds that hold the ice crystal together.

2. If warm water in a calorimeter loses 8,368 J of heat when ice is added, the ice gains:

a) 4,184 J
b) 8,368 J
c) 16,736 J
d) It depends on the mass of ice
✓ Conservation of energy: Qlost = -Qgained. The magnitude is EXACTLY the same. Energy is transferred, not created or destroyed.

3. A student's experimental ΔHf is 290 J/g (lower than the theoretical 333.55 J/g). The most likely cause is:

a) The ice had excess surface water that wasn't dried off
b) Heat was lost to the surrounding air
c) The ice came straight from a -20°C freezer
d) The student didn't stir enough
✓ Excess water on ice adds mass without requiring fusion energy. This inflates mice in the denominator of ΔHf = Q/m, making the result too LOW. Heat loss to air and sub-zero ice would make it too HIGH.

4. How much energy is needed to melt 50 g of ice at 0°C? (ΔHf = 333.55 J/g)

a) 333.55 J
b) 6,671 J
c) 16,678 J
d) 167 J
✓ Q = m × ΔHf = 50 g × 333.55 J/g = 16,677.5 J ≈ 16,678 J. That's about 16.7 kJ just to melt 50 grams of ice!

5. In a calorimetry experiment, ΔT for the warm water is:

a) Always positive because temperature drops
b) Negative because Tf < Ti
c) Zero because the calorimeter is insulated
d) Equal to the heat of fusion
✓ ΔT = Tf − Ti. Since the warm water cools down, Tf < Ti, making ΔT negative. This is correct! The negative sign means the water LOST energy.

6. Why does water have such a high heat of fusion compared to most substances?

a) Water molecules are very heavy
b) Water forms an extensive hydrogen bonding network in its solid state
c) Water has a low boiling point
d) Water is a poor conductor of heat
✓ Ice has each molecule hydrogen-bonded to ~4 neighbors in a hexagonal crystal. Breaking this extensive H-bonding network requires significant energy, giving water one of the highest heats of fusion among common substances.
