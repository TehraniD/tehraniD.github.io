⚗ Lab 3: Heat of Fusion of Water

Instructor Teaching Guide — First 10-15 Minutes

◉ LEARNING OBJECTIVES (Write on Board)

By the end of this lab, students will be able to:

  1. Explain WHY ice absorbs energy without changing temperature during melting
  2. Use calorimetry (Q = mcΔT) to measure heat transfer
  3. Calculate the experimental heat of fusion of water and compare to the accepted value
  4. Apply conservation of energy to a real physical system

🎬 THE HOOK (2-3 minutes)

Start with a Question:

"How many of you put ice in your drink today? Have you ever noticed that the ice takes FOREVER to melt, even though the water around it is much warmer? Think about it — warm water sitting on a table reaches room temperature in minutes. But ice in that same warm water? It sits there for 10, 15, 20 minutes. Why?"

Wait for responses. Most will say "because it's cold" or "because it's solid."

"But cold things warm up. We know that. A cold spoon warms up in your hand in seconds. So why doesn't the ice just... warm up and become water? What's taking so long?"

The Reveal:

"Melting ice isn't just about raising the temperature. The ice has to break apart its entire crystal structure — every single hydrogen bond connecting every molecule to its neighbors. That takes a MASSIVE amount of energy. And here's the wild part: while it's doing that, the temperature doesn't change at all. The thermometer stays at 0°C the whole time it's melting. All that energy is going into breaking bonds, not making molecules move faster."

§ THE CONCEPT (5-7 minutes)

The Moving Day Analogy

Draw this on the board or describe:

"Imagine you live in an apartment building where everyone is connected to their neighbors by bungee cords. That's ice — every molecule bonded to the molecules around it. Now you want to move out."
"Before you can go anywhere, you have to cut ALL the bungee cords attached to you. That takes work — that takes ENERGY. And while you're cutting cords, you haven't actually moved yet. You're still standing in the same spot at the same temperature."
"Only AFTER all the cords are cut can you start walking around freely. THEN you can warm up, move faster, etc. The energy of cutting those cords? That's the heat of fusion."
Key Point: "The heat of fusion is 333.55 J per gram. That means melting 1 gram of ice takes the same energy as heating 1 gram of water from 0°C to 80°C. That's ENORMOUS."

⚛ THE EQUATIONS (3-4 minutes)

Equation 1: Heat transfer (no phase change)

Q = m × c × ΔT
Symbol Meaning Units "Plain English"
Q Heat transferred J (joules) "How much energy moved"
m Mass g (grams) "How much stuff"
c Specific heat capacity J/(g·°C) "How stubborn is it about changing temperature?"
ΔT Temperature change °C "How much did the temperature change?" (Tf - Ti)

Equation 2: Phase change (melting)

Qice = mice × ΔHf

The Conservation Key

Qice = −Qwarm water
"Energy is like money in a closed bank account. If the warm water 'spends' 9,000 joules, the ice 'receives' exactly 9,000 joules. Nobody creates or destroys energy — it just transfers."

Quick Check

Ask the class:

"If 100 g of warm water at 35°C drops to 15°C, how much energy did it lose? Work it out."
Answer:
Q = m × c × ΔT
Q = 100 g × 4.184 J/(g·°C) × (15 - 35)°C
Q = 100 × 4.184 × (-20) = -8,368 J

The negative sign means it LOST 8,368 joules. That energy went into melting ice.

🌍 REAL-WORLD CONNECTIONS (2-3 minutes)

Why This Matters:

Application How Heat of Fusion Is Involved
Ice Packs • Ice absorbs massive energy from swollen tissue WITHOUT getting warm
• 333 J per gram! Much more effective than a cold towel
• That's why ice packs stay cold — they're busy breaking bonds
Climate & Polar Ice • Arctic ice absorbs enormous energy as it melts
• This SLOWS global warming — ice acts as a heat buffer
• Once the ice is gone, that buffer disappears and warming accelerates
Food Preservation • Ice in a cooler absorbs 333 J/g while melting at 0°C
• Keeps food at a constant temperature until ALL ice is melted
• Way more effective than just cold water
Ice Cream Making • Salt + ice mixture gets VERY cold (below 0°C)
• The salt forces ice to melt, pulling 333 J/g from surroundings
• The cream mixture drops below 0°C and freezes

⚗️ TODAY'S LAB OVERVIEW (1-2 minutes)

"Today you're going to:"
  1. Put warm water in a calorimeter and measure its temperature
  2. Add ice and watch the temperature DROP
  3. Calculate how much energy the warm water lost
  4. Use conservation of energy: that's exactly how much the ice gained
  5. Divide by the mass of ice → that's your heat of fusion
  6. Compare to the accepted value (333.55 J/g) and calculate percent error
The Goal: "You're essentially asking: how much energy does it cost to melt one gram of ice? The answer should be about 334 joules. Let's see how close you can get."

⚠ CRITICAL TECHNIQUE POINTS

◇ #1 SOURCE OF ERROR: WET ICE
If the ice has surface melt water, that water gets counted as "ice" but doesn't need fusion energy. Result: ΔHf too low. Have students pat ice dry with paper towels before adding!
Δt #2 SOURCE OF ERROR: NOT STIRRING
If students don't stir, the thermometer reads the local temperature (near the warm water or near the ice), not the average. Result: inaccurate Tf. Continuous gentle stirring is essential.
⏱️ #3 SOURCE OF ERROR: RUSHING Tf
Students often read the minimum temperature too early or too late. Tf is the lowest STABLE temperature — all ice must be melted and temperature must stabilize for 30+ seconds.

⚠ COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS TO ADDRESS

Students Think... Reality
"Adding heat always raises temperature" During phase changes, energy breaks bonds — temperature stays constant
"The ice makes the water cold" The warm water gives energy TO the ice. The ice absorbs energy. The water cools as a RESULT.
"Q should always be positive" Q is negative when a substance loses heat. Sign convention matters!
"ΔT is always positive" ΔT = Tf - Ti. If something cools down, ΔT is negative. Don't flip it!
"Percent error should be 0%" Real experiments always have error. 3-10% is excellent for this lab. Discuss WHY there's error.

📊 EXPECTED RESULTS & TROUBLESHOOTING

Result Likely Cause Fix
ΔHf much too LOW (<280 J/g) Wet ice (excess melt water) Pat ice dry before adding
ΔHf much too HIGH (>400 J/g) Heat loss to air/calorimeter walls; ice from deep freezer (below 0°C) Use ice from ice-water slurry (guaranteed 0°C); work quickly
Trials don't agree (>15% difference) Inconsistent technique between trials Emphasize same procedure both times
Temperature keeps dropping after adding ice Not all ice melted; or still reaching equilibrium Keep stirring until all ice melts and temp stabilizes

📊 QUICK REFERENCE FOR CIRCULATING

As students work, use these benchmarks to check their progress:

Checkpoint Expected Values Red Flags
Empty calorimeter mass 30-60 g (depends on model) Forgot stirrer; scale not zeroed
Warm water mass 80-130 g Too little (<50g) or too much (>200g)
Ti 33-40°C Too hot (>50°C) → excessive heat loss
Tf 10-20°C If above 25°C, not enough ice was added
ΔT About -20°C Less than -10°C → add more ice next trial
Ice mass 20-40 g If >60 g, they added too much → some ice may not melt
