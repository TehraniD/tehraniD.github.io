The Real-World Hook

You're at a restaurant. You order a glass of water with ice. Ten minutes later, the ice is STILL there, barely melted. But when you spill cold water on the table, it warms up to room temperature in minutes.

Why does ice take SO MUCH energy to melt? The answer is Heat of Fusion (ΔHf) — and it reveals something beautiful about how nature stores energy in molecular bonds.

Part 1: The Science

What Happens When Ice Melts?

When you add heat to ice at 0°C, the temperature doesn't go up. All that energy goes into breaking hydrogen bonds between water molecules in the crystal. This is the heat of fusion.

ΔHf (water) = 333.55 J/g = 6.01 kJ/mol
What You're DoingEnergy Required
Heating 1 g of water by 1°C4.184 J
Heating 1 g of water from 0°C to 80°C334.7 J
Melting 1 g of ice at 0°C333.55 J

Melting a single gram of ice takes almost the SAME energy as heating that gram of water from freezing to nearly boiling!

The Calorimetry Equations

Qwarm water = mwarm water × cwater × (Tf − Ti)

where cwater = 4.184 J/(g·°C)
Qice = −Qwarm water     (Conservation of Energy)
ΔHf = Qice / mice

Part 2: The Experiment

Materials

  • Calorimeter (aluminum cup + stirrer)

  • Balance/scale

Procedure — Step by Step

  1. Measure the mass of the empty calorimeter (cup + stirrer) and record.
  2. Fill the calorimeter halfway with warm water (~10°C above room temperature, so around 33-37°C).
  3. Measure the mass of calorimeter + warm water and record.
  4. Record the initial temperature of warm water (Ti). Stir gently, wait 30 sec.
  5. Obtain crushed ice and PAT DRY with paper towels.
  6. Add ice to the calorimeter a little at a time, stirring CONTINUOUSLY, until temperature drops about 20°C.
  7. Record the lowest temperature (Tf) — wait until ALL ice is melted and temp is stable.
  8. Measure the mass of calorimeter + warm water + melted ice.
  9. Repeat the experiment for Trial 2.

Fun Facts While Your Ice Melts

◇ It takes as much energy to melt 1 kg of ice as to heat 1 kg of water from 0°C to 80°C.

≋ The ocean absorbs ~90% of excess heat from climate change — much of it melting ice.

▲ Glaciers store about 69% of Earth's fresh water.

℞ Ice packs absorb 333 J/g from tissue without getting warm — targeted cooling!
