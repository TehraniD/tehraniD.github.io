⚷ Lab 2: Freezing Point Depression — KEY

⚠ INSTRUCTOR USE ONLY ⚠

Formula

ΔT = i × kf × m     (kf = 1.86 °C/m)

Pre-Lab Calculations — ANSWERS

Heavy Salt (7.5 g NaCl in 50 g water)

Step 1: moles = 7.5 g ÷ 58.44 g/mol = 0.128 mol

Step 2: m = 0.128 mol ÷ 0.050 kg = 2.57 m

Step 3: ΔT = 2 × 1.86 × 2.57 = 9.56 °C

Step 4: FP = 0°C − 9.56°C = −9.56 °C

Premium (5.0 g CaCl₂ in 50 g water)

Step 1: moles = 5.0 g ÷ 110.98 g/mol = 0.0451 mol

Step 2: m = 0.0451 mol ÷ 0.050 kg = 0.901 m

Step 3: ΔT = 3 × 1.86 × 0.901 = 5.03 °C

Step 4: FP = 0°C − 5.03°C = −5.03 °C

Analysis Questions — MODEL ANSWERS

1. Why does CaCl₂ produce greater ΔT than NaCl at same molality?

CaCl₂ has i = 3 (produces 3 particles: Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻) while NaCl has i = 2 (only 2 particles: Na⁺ + Cl⁻). Since ΔT = i × kf × m, higher i means greater freezing point depression.

2. Why switch to CaCl₂ below −10°C?

NaCl's practical effectiveness ends around −10°C due to its eutectic point. Below this, salt can't dissolve fast enough to melt ice. CaCl₂ works down to −29°C because of its higher i value and lower eutectic temperature.

3. Why spray brine BEFORE a storm?

Brine is already dissolved, so it works immediately. Dry salt needs liquid water to dissolve first — if ice is too cold and dry, salt just sits on top. Pre-treatment also prevents ice from bonding to pavement, making plowing easier.

4. Environmental damage from road salt?

(1) Chloride accumulation in lakes/streams — harms freshwater organisms, doesn't break down naturally
(2) Groundwater contamination — affects drinking water wells
(Also accept: soil damage to roadside plants, vehicle/infrastructure corrosion)

5. Storm at −5°C — which salt?

NaCl is sufficient. At −5°C, heavy NaCl (effective to −9.56°C) provides adequate protection and costs 5× less than CaCl₂. Save CaCl₂ for temperatures below −10°C.
