Theoretical Background

In absorption spectroscopy, we measure the fraction of light that passes through a sample. The Absorbance (A) is calculated using the intensity of light through the blank (I0) and the sample (I):

A = -log(I / I0)

According to Beer's Law (A = εlc), Absorbance is directly proportional to concentration. If you plot Absorbance vs. Concentration, you will get a straight line that can be used to determine unknown concentrations.

Experimental Procedure

Part A: Construction of the "Sample Box"

  1. Prepare the Box: Obtain a small cardboard box from your instructor.
  2. Cut the Window: On the front face, cut a small rectangular hole (approximately 2 cm wide) near the bottom. This is your viewing window.
  3. Open the Back: Completely remove the back panel of the box to allow light to enter.
  4. The Background: Place a sheet of Green Construction Paper behind the box. This provides the complementary light source for purple grape juice samples.
  5. Alignment: Position the box so that when you look through the front window, you see the green paper in the background.

Part B: Serial Dilutions

Prepare the following standard solutions using your 100% grape juice stock and distilled water:

  1. Label 6 clean cups: Blank, Std 1 (20%), Std 2 (40%), Std 3 (60%), Std 4 (80%), Std 5 (100%)
  2. Using the volumes you calculated in the prelab, prepare each standard solution
  3. Mix each solution thoroughly by swirling
  4. Also obtain your Unknown sample from your instructor

Part C: Data Collection

  1. Insert Blank: Place the cup with pure water (Blank) inside the box.
  2. Lock Exposure: Point your camera at the window. Tap and hold the screen until "AE/AF LOCK" appears. Do not touch the screen again during the entire experiment.
  3. Record I0: Using the Color Grab app (or similar), record the Green (G) value. This is your baseline (I0).
  4. Measure Standards: Replace the blank with each standard solution (going from lowest to highest concentration) and record the G value (I) for each.
  5. Measure Unknown: Finally, measure the G value for your unknown sample.
