## Essential Constants & Formulas

$$Q = m \times c \times \Delta T \qquad \text{(heating/cooling)}$$
$$Q_{\text{ice}} = m_{\text{ice}} \times \Delta H_f \qquad \text{(melting)}$$
$$Q_{\text{ice}} = -Q_{\text{warm water}} \qquad \text{(conservation of energy)}$$

| Constant | Value |
|----------|-------|
| $c_{\text{water}}$ | 4.184 J/(g·°C) |
| $\Delta H_f$ (theoretical) | 333.55 J/g |
| $\Delta H_f$ (theoretical) | 6.01 kJ/mol |
| Molar mass of water | 18.015 g/mol |

### Trial 1 — Complete Calculations

**Step 1:** Mass of warm water
$$m_w = 155.8 \text{ g} - 45.2 \text{ g} = \boxed{110.6 \text{ g}}$$

**Step 2:** Mass of ice
$$m_{\text{ice}} = 183.5 \text{ g} - 155.8 \text{ g} = \boxed{27.7 \text{ g}}$$

**Step 3:** Temperature change
$$\Delta T = T_f - T_i = 14.8°C - 35.4°C = \boxed{-20.6°C}$$

**Step 4:** Heat lost by warm water
$$Q_{\text{warm water}} = m_w \times c_w \times \Delta T$$
$$= 110.6 \text{ g} \times 4.184 \text{ J/(g·°C)} \times (-20.6°C)$$
$$= \boxed{-9{,}531.9 \text{ J}}$$

**Step 5:** Heat gained by ice (conservation of energy)
$$Q_{\text{ice}} = -Q_{\text{warm water}} = -(-9{,}531.9 \text{ J}) = \boxed{9{,}531.9 \text{ J}}$$

**Step 6:** Heat of Fusion
$$\Delta H_f = \frac{Q_{\text{ice}}}{m_{\text{ice}}} = \frac{9{,}531.9 \text{ J}}{27.7 \text{ g}} = \boxed{344.1 \text{ J/g}}$$

## Why the Experimental Value Is Slightly HIGH

A percent error of ~3% with the experimental value being HIGHER than theoretical is very typical. Here's why:

1. **Heat loss to surroundings:** The warm water loses some heat to the calorimeter walls and the air — NOT to the ice. This means $(T_f - T_i)$ is more negative than it should be → $|Q_{\text{warm water}}|$ is too large → $\Delta H_f$ appears too high.

2. **Wet ice:** If the ice has surface melt water at 0°C, that water contributes mass but doesn't absorb fusion energy. This reduces $m_{\text{ice}}$ (effective) while $Q$ stays the same → $\Delta H_f$ appears too high.

**If students get values TOO LOW (<300 J/g):** The ice was probably sub-zero (below 0°C), or they added ice too slowly allowing heat transfer to air.

## Post-Lab Questions — COMPLETE ANSWER KEY

### Question 1: Basic Calorimetry

**What quantity of heat is required to raise the temperature of 500 grams of water from 25°C to 85°C?**

**Given:**
- $m = 500$ g
- $c = 4.18$ J/(g·°C)
- $T_i = 25$°C
- $T_f = 85$°C
- $\Delta T = 85 - 25 = 60$°C

**Solution:**
$$Q = m \times c \times \Delta T$$
$$Q = 500 \text{ g} \times 4.18 \text{ J/(g·°C)} \times 60°C$$
$$\boxed{Q = 125{,}400 \text{ J} = 125.4 \text{ kJ}}$$

**In plain English:** It takes about 125 kJ to heat a half-liter of water by 60°C. That's roughly what your electric kettle does when you make tea.

### Question 2: Unknown Metal Identification

**An unknown metal (20 g, 25°C) is placed in 50.0 g water at 90°C. Final temperature = 80°C.**

**Given:**
- $m_{\text{metal}} = 20$ g, $T_{i,\text{metal}} = 25$°C
- $m_{\text{water}} = 50.0$ g, $T_{i,\text{water}} = 90$°C
- $T_f = 80$°C (both reach this temperature)
- $c_{\text{water}} = 4.184$ J/(g·°C)

**Find:** $c_{\text{metal}}$

**Solution:**

Heat lost by water = Heat gained by metal (conservation of energy):
$$Q_{\text{water}} = -Q_{\text{metal}}$$
$$m_w \times c_w \times (T_f - T_{i,w}) = -[m_m \times c_m \times (T_f - T_{i,m})]$$

Calculate heat lost by water:
$$Q_{\text{water}} = 50.0 \text{ g} \times 4.184 \text{ J/(g·°C)} \times (80 - 90)°C$$
$$Q_{\text{water}} = 50.0 \times 4.184 \times (-10) = -2{,}092 \text{ J}$$

Heat gained by metal:
$$Q_{\text{metal}} = -Q_{\text{water}} = 2{,}092 \text{ J}$$

Solve for $c_{\text{metal}}$:
$$c_{\text{metal}} = \frac{Q_{\text{metal}}}{m_m \times \Delta T_m}$$
$$c_{\text{metal}} = \frac{2{,}092 \text{ J}}{20 \text{ g} \times (80 - 25)°C}$$
$$c_{\text{metal}} = \frac{2{,}092}{20 \times 55}$$
$$c_{\text{metal}} = \frac{2{,}092}{1{,}100}$$
$$\boxed{c_{\text{metal}} = 1.90 \text{ J/(g·°C)}}$$

**Metal Identification:**

| Metal | Specific Heat (J/g·°C) |
|-------|----------------------|
| Aluminum | 0.897 |
| Iron | 0.449 |
| Copper | 0.385 |
| Lead | 0.129 |
| Silver | 0.235 |
| Gold | 0.129 |
| Zinc | 0.388 |

**Note:** The calculated value of 1.90 J/(g·°C) is higher than any common metal. This is an unusually high specific heat. Possible explanations:
- The metal could be a **beryllium alloy** (c ≈ 1.82 J/g·°C)
- There may be **experimental error** in the given data
- The problem demonstrates that real experiments sometimes give unexpected results

**Most likely answer students should give:** The value is closest to **aluminum** (highest specific heat among common metals), but is significantly higher. Students should note the discrepancy and suggest sources of error (heat loss to surroundings, measurement errors in temperature or mass).

> **Instructor Note:** This problem as written gives a specific heat higher than any common metal. If students struggle, acknowledge this and use it as a teaching moment about experimental realism. If you'd like a "cleaner" answer, the water temperature drop from 90°C to 80°C with 20 g of metal is simply too large for the metal mass given — which is actually realistic for poorly insulated systems.

### Question 3: Sources of Error — MODEL ANSWERS

| Source of Error | Too High or Too Low? | Explanation |
|-----------------|---------------------|-------------|
| **1. Excess water on ice surface** | **Too LOW** | Liquid water at 0°C that comes with the ice adds to the measured ice mass but doesn't need fusion energy (it's already liquid). So $m_{\text{ice}}$ is artificially large → $\Delta H_f = Q/m$ is too small. |
| **2. Heat loss to calorimeter walls and air** | **Too HIGH** | Warm water loses heat to both the ice AND the surroundings. We attribute ALL the heat loss to ice → $Q_{\text{ice}}$ is overestimated → $\Delta H_f$ is too high. |
| **3. Ice not at exactly 0°C (sub-zero ice from freezer)** | **Too HIGH** | If ice is below 0°C, some absorbed energy goes to warming the ice TO 0°C before melting begins. We attribute all energy to fusion → $\Delta H_f$ appears too high. |

**Other acceptable answers:**
- Poor stirring → uneven temperature → inaccurate $T_f$ reading (direction depends)
- Thermometer calibration error → could go either way
- Not waiting for thermal equilibrium → $T_f$ not truly the final temperature (too high usually)
- Adding ice too slowly → more heat loss to air → too high

### Question 4: Frozen Lake — MODEL ANSWER

> A frozen lake doesn't immediately melt when air temperature rises from -2°C to +2°C because of the enormous heat of fusion of water. 
>
> Even though the air is above 0°C, the ice must absorb **333.55 joules per gram** just to change from solid to liquid — without any temperature change. For a lake, this amounts to a colossal amount of energy.
>
> Consider: A small pond with a 10 cm thick ice layer over 1 hectare (10,000 m²) contains about 917,000 kg of ice. To melt it requires:
> $$Q = 917{,}000{,}000 \text{ g} \times 333.55 \text{ J/g} ≈ 3.06 \times 10^{11} \text{ J}$$
>
> That's 306 billion joules! The 4°C temperature difference between ice and air provides heat transfer, but the rate is slow. The air simply cannot deliver that much energy quickly — especially since air has very low thermal conductivity.
>
> This is why spring ice-out on lakes takes weeks, not hours, even when air temperatures are well above freezing.

### Question 5: Arctic Ice — MODEL ANSWER

**Given:** $m_{\text{ice}} = 2.6 \times 10^{19}$ g and $\Delta H_f = 333.55$ J/g

$$Q = m \times \Delta H_f$$
$$Q = 2.6 \times 10^{19} \text{ g} \times 333.55 \text{ J/g}$$
$$\boxed{Q = 8.67 \times 10^{21} \text{ J}}$$

**In context:** That's 8.67 sextillion joules, or about 8.67 ZJ (zettajoules). For reference, total global energy consumption is about 0.58 ZJ per year. So melting all Arctic ice would require about **15 years' worth of total human energy production**. This is why climate scientists emphasize that once large-scale melting begins, it's effectively irreversible on human timescales — the energy is already committed.

## Pre-Lab Quiz — ANSWER KEY

1. **b) Melt a solid at its melting point** — Heat of fusion specifically refers to the phase change from solid to liquid at constant temperature.

2. **c) Stays constant at 0°C** — During melting, ALL added energy breaks intermolecular bonds. Temperature doesn't change until all ice is melted.

3. **b) 8,000 J** — Conservation of energy: $Q_{\text{lost}} = -Q_{\text{gained}}$. The magnitude is the same.

4. **c) Isolating a system to measure heat transfer** — A calorimeter doesn't create or destroy energy. It minimizes exchange with surroundings so we can track energy flow within the system.

5. **a) 3.4%** — $\frac{|345 - 333.55|}{333.55} \times 100 = \frac{11.45}{333.55} \times 100 = 3.43\%$

## Common Student Misconceptions

| Misconception | Correction |
|---------------|------------|
| "Adding heat always raises temperature" | During phase changes, energy goes into breaking bonds, NOT increasing kinetic energy. Temperature stays constant. |
| "Ice has to be below 0°C" | Ice at exactly 0°C is normal. Ice and liquid water coexist at 0°C at atmospheric pressure. |
| "The calorimeter created the weird results" | The calorimeter just measures. If results are off, it's the technique or measurement errors. |
| "Percent error should be 0%" | Real experiments ALWAYS have error. The point is understanding WHY and minimizing it. 3-10% is excellent for this lab. |
| "Q is always positive" | Q is negative when a substance LOSES heat. Sign matters! |
| "$\Delta T$ is always $T_f - T_i$" | Correct — but students often flip it. Emphasize: $\Delta$ always means final minus initial. If temperature drops, $\Delta T$ is negative. |

### Materials per Group

- 1 calorimeter (aluminum cup + lid + stirrer)
- ~200 g crushed ice (drained, at 0°C)
- Access to warm water (~35-40°C)
- Digital thermometer (±0.1°C preferred)
- Balance (±0.1 g)
- Paper towels
- 250 mL beaker (for warm water transport)

### Critical Technique Tips to Emphasize

1. **DRY THE ICE.** This is the #1 source of error. Excess melt water on ice adds mass without requiring fusion energy → $\Delta H_f$ comes out too low. Pat with paper towels.

2. **Use warm water, not hot water.** ~10°C above room temp is ideal (~33-37°C). Hot water (>50°C) causes excessive heat loss to surroundings.

3. **Stir continuously.** Poor stirring creates temperature gradients → inaccurate $T_f$.

4. **Add ice until ΔT ≈ 20°C.** Too little ice → small temperature change → large relative error. Too much ice → ice may not all melt → invalid measurement.

5. **Read $T_f$ at equilibrium.** Wait until temperature stabilizes for 30+ seconds. Rushing this reading is a common mistake.

### Extensions

1. **Convert to kJ/mol:** Have students convert their J/g result to kJ/mol and compare to 6.01 kJ/mol
2. **Phase diagram connection:** Discuss where heat of fusion appears on a heating/cooling curve
3. **Compare substances:** Have students research $\Delta H_f$ for different substances and discuss why water's is so high
4. **Error analysis lab:** Have students deliberately introduce errors (wet ice, no stirring) and quantify the effect
