☰ Before You Start: Setting Expectations

Reality check for community college labs:

  • Old thermometers may be off by 0.5-1.0°C — that's okay!
  • Crushed ice quality varies — some will have larger chunks
  • Students work at different paces — have fast finishers help others
  • Expect 10-25% error — this is normal with basic equipment
  • The CONCEPTS matter more than perfect numbers

📝 Part 1: Pre-Lab Calculations

Do these BEFORE coming to lab. They take about 15-20 minutes.

Given Information

ConstantValue
kf (water)1.86 °C/m
Molar mass NaCl58.44 g/mol
Molar mass CaCl₂110.98 g/mol
i for NaCl2 (Na⁺ + Cl⁻)
i for CaCl₂3 (Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻)

Calculation 1: Heavy Salt (7.5 g NaCl in 50 g water)

Step 1: Calculate moles of NaCl

moles = mass ÷ molar mass
moles = 7.5 g ÷ 58.44 g/mol
moles = 0.128 mol

Step 2: Calculate molality

m = moles ÷ kg of solvent
m = 0.128 mol ÷ 0.050 kg
m = 2.57 m
※ Common confusion: Why 0.050 kg? Because 50 g = 0.050 kg. Molality uses kilograms!

Step 3: Calculate ΔT

ΔT = i × kf × m
ΔT = 2 × 1.86 × 2.57
ΔT = 9.56 °C

Step 4: Calculate expected freezing point

FP = 0°C − ΔT
FP = 0°C − 9.56°C
FP = −9.56 °C
✗ Common Mistake #1: Forgetting to convert grams to kilograms
Wrong: m = 0.128 mol ÷ 50 = 0.00256 m
Right: m = 0.128 mol ÷ 0.050 kg = 2.57 m

Calculation 2: Premium Salt (5.0 g CaCl₂ in 50 g water)

Step 1: Calculate moles of CaCl₂

moles = 5.0 g ÷ 110.98 g/mol
moles = 0.0451 mol

Step 2: Calculate molality

m = 0.0451 mol ÷ 0.050 kg
m = 0.901 m

Step 3: Calculate ΔT

ΔT = i × kf × m
ΔT = 3 × 1.86 × 0.901 ← Notice i = 3 for CaCl₂!
ΔT = 5.03 °C

Step 4: Calculate expected freezing point

FP = 0°C − 5.03°C
FP = −5.03 °C
✗ Common Mistake #2: Using i = 2 for CaCl₂
Wrong: ΔT = 2 × 1.86 × 0.901 = 3.35°C
Right: ΔT = 3 × 1.86 × 0.901 = 5.03°C

Remember: CaCl₂ → Ca²⁺ + Cl⁻ + Cl⁻ = 3 particles!

⚛ Part 2: The Experiment (In Lab)

Part A: Calibration — Check Your Thermometer!

Why this matters: Old thermometers drift. A thermometer that reads 0.8°C in ice water will read 0.8°C too high for ALL measurements. We need to know this offset.

What you do:

  1. Fill a beaker with crushed ice
  2. Add just enough distilled water to make a slurry (ice should be wet, not floating)
  3. Stir gently for 2 full minutes
  4. Insert thermometer, wait 30 seconds, read temperature

Realistic Result (Community College Lab):

Thermometer reading:   0.8   °C

Correction factor:   −0.8   °C

This means: subtract 0.8 from all future readings to get the "true" temperature

※ Instructor tip: If students get readings between −0.5°C and +1.0°C, their thermometer is fine. Anything outside this range — have them try a different thermometer.

Part B: Prepare Your Solutions

What you do:

  1. Label 4 beakers: Control, Light, Heavy, Premium
  2. Use the balance to measure 50 g of crushed ice into each beaker
  3. Add salt to each according to the table:
    • Control: 0 g (nothing)
    • Light: 2.5 g NaCl
    • Heavy: 7.5 g NaCl
    • Premium: 5.0 g CaCl₂
  4. Stir each solution with a glass rod for 30 seconds
⚠ Heads up for CaCl₂: Students will notice the CaCl₂ beaker gets WARM at first! This is the exothermic heat of dissolution. Tell them: "Yes, it's supposed to do that. Keep stirring — it will get cold soon."

Part C: Data Collection — The Main Event

What you do:

  1. Start your timer when you add the salt
  2. Insert thermometer into beaker, stir gently
  3. Record temperature every 60 seconds
  4. Continue until temperature stabilizes (stops changing)

Part D: Cold Limit Test

What you do:

  1. Make a "super-cold bath" — large beaker with lots of ice + lots of salt
  2. This bath should reach about −15°C
  3. Place small test tubes of each solution into the bath
  4. Observe: which ones freeze? Which stay liquid?

💰 Part 4: Cost Analysis

NaCl (Heavy):

Cost of 7.5 g = 7.5 g × ($60 / 907,185 g) = $0.000496
ΔT achieved = 9.56°C
Cost per °C = $0.000496 ÷ 9.56 = $0.000052 per °C

CaCl₂:

Cost of 5.0 g = 5.0 g × ($300 / 907,185 g) = $0.00165
ΔT achieved = 5.03°C
Cost per °C = $0.00165 ÷ 5.03 = $0.000328 per °C

Conclusion:

NaCl is about 6× more cost-effective ($0.000052 vs $0.000328 per degree).

BUT: NaCl only works above −10°C. For extreme cold, you MUST use CaCl₂ despite the higher cost — there's no cheaper alternative that works.

✍️ Part 5: Analysis Questions — Model Answers

1. Why does CaCl₂ produce a greater freezing point depression than NaCl at the same molality?

CaCl₂ has a van't Hoff factor of 3 (it produces 3 ions: Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻), while NaCl only has i = 2 (Na⁺ + Cl⁻). Since ΔT = i × kf × m, a higher i means greater freezing point depression.

2. Why do cities switch from NaCl to CaCl₂ when temperatures drop below −10°C?

NaCl becomes ineffective below about −10°C because it reaches its eutectic point — the solution becomes saturated and can't dissolve more salt, so it stops melting ice. CaCl₂ works down to −29°C, so it's the only option for extreme cold.

3. Why do cities spray salt brine BEFORE a storm instead of spreading dry salt after?

Brine is already dissolved, so it starts working immediately. Dry salt needs liquid water to dissolve first — if the ice is too cold and dry, the salt just sits on top. Pre-treatment also prevents ice from bonding to the pavement, making plowing easier.

4. How does road salt damage the environment? (Name 2 effects)

1) Chloride accumulates in lakes and streams, harming freshwater fish and plants (chloride doesn't break down naturally).
2) Salt contaminates groundwater and drinking wells near roads.

(Also acceptable: kills roadside plants, corrodes bridges and vehicles)

5. Storm forecast: −5°C. Which salt should the city use, and why?

Use NaCl (rock salt). At −5°C, NaCl is effective (works down to −9°C) and costs 5× less than CaCl₂. Save the CaCl₂ for when temperatures drop below −10°C.

☰ Summary: What Students Should Take Away

  1. Salt lowers the freezing point by interfering with ice crystal formation
  2. More particles = more depression: CaCl₂ (3 particles) beats NaCl (2 particles)
  3. There's a limit: NaCl stops working around −10°C
  4. Cost vs. performance: NaCl is cheaper, CaCl₂ works in extreme cold
  5. Real-world trade-offs: Safety, cost, and environmental damage all matter
