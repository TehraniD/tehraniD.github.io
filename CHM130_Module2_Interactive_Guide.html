Atomic Structure

2 Exam Qs
📖 Explanation

Every atom has three parts. Think of it like a hospital room:

ParticleChargeLocationHow to Find
Protons (p⁺)Positive (+)Nucleus= Atomic number
Neutrons (n⁰)Neutral (0)Nucleus= Mass # − Atomic #
Electrons (e⁻)Negative (−)Electron cloud= Protons (neutral atom)

The atomic number is the element's permanent ID — change it and you've got a different element entirely. The mass number can change (that's what isotopes are).

Atomic Size Trends: Big atoms live in the bottom-left corner of the periodic table. Going down a group = bigger (more shells). Going left = bigger (fewer protons pulling inward).

🧠 Memory Aid
The Hospital Wristband
PEN

Protons = Positive  |  Electrons = nEgative  |  Neutrons = Neutral

Think of the atomic number like a patient's wristband at the hospital — it never changes. You can gain weight (mass number), but your hospital ID stays the same. Change the ID and you're literally a different person.

✏️ Example Problem
Find all particles for Neon-21
1
Look up Neon on the periodic table → Atomic number = 10 → 10 protons
2
Mass number is in the name: "Neon-21"
3
Neutrons = 21 − 10 = 11 neutrons
4
Neutral atom → electrons = protons = 10 electrons
Neon-21: 10 p⁺, 11 n⁰, 10 e⁻
🎯 Your Turn

How many neutrons does Carbon-14 have?

Carbon atomic # = 6. Neutrons = 14 − 6 = 8 neutrons. (This is the radioactive isotope used in carbon dating!)

Molecular Weight & Molar Mass

Formula
📖 Explanation

Molar mass = the weight of ONE mole of a substance in g/mol. You find it by shopping the periodic table: look up each element's atomic mass, multiply by how many atoms you have, add it all up.

Once you know the molar mass, you can convert between grams and moles:

grams = moles × molar mass   |   moles = grams ÷ molar mass
🧠 Memory Aid
The Grocery Receipt
G = M × MM

Molar mass is like checking out at a grocery store. Each element has a price tag (atomic mass). You scan each item, multiply by quantity, and your receipt total is the molar mass.

Picture the GMM triangle like a pizza slice: Grams on top, Moles and Molar Mass on the bottom. Cover what you need — what's left is your formula.

✏️ Example Problem
Exam: Find the molar mass of MgCl₂
1
MgCl₂ = 1 Magnesium + 2 Chlorine
2
Mg = 24.31 g/mol  |  Cl = 35.45 g/mol
3
24.31 + 2(35.45) = 24.31 + 70.90 = 95.21 g/mol
MgCl₂ = 95.21 g/mol
Exam: Blanca needs 0.50 mol of CO. How many grams?
1
Molar mass of CO: C(12.01) + O(16.00) = 28.01 g/mol
2
grams = 0.50 mol × 28.01 g/mol = 14.01 g
14.01 grams of CO
🎯 Your Turn

How many grams in 0.70 mol of H₂O₂?

H₂O₂ molar mass: 2(1.008) + 2(16.00) = 34.02 g/mol. Grams = 0.70 × 34.02 = 23.8 g

The Mole Concept

2 Exam Qs
📖 Explanation

A mole is just a counting word. "Dozen" means 12. "Mole" means 6.02 × 10²³. That's it.

The subscript in a chemical formula tells you the mole ratio. In C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ (sucrose), every 1 mole of sucrose contains 22 moles of hydrogen atoms.

The trick: moles of element = moles of compound × subscript.

🧠 Memory Aid
The Med Supply Closet

Nurses don't count individual gauze pads — they order by the box. Each box has a set number inside.

Chemists don't count atoms — they order by the mole. And the subscript on the formula tells you how many are packed in each "box."

If each box of a wound care kit has 22 gauze pads, then 3 boxes = 3 × 22 = 66 gauze pads. Same logic: 3 mol sucrose = 3 × 22 = 66 mol hydrogen.

✏️ Example Problem
Exam: 3 mol of sucrose (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁) → how many moles of hydrogen?
1
Subscript on H = 22
2
3 mol × 22 = 66 mol H
66 moles of hydrogen
🎯 Your Turn

2 mol of fructose (C₆H₁₂O₆). How many moles of oxygen?

Subscript on O = 6. So 2 × 6 = 12 mol O

Avogadro's Number

Exam
📖 Explanation
1 mole = 6.02 × 10²³ particles

Two conversions — that's all you need:

Molecules → Moles: DIVIDE by 6.02 × 10²³ (big → small)

Moles → Molecules: MULTIPLY by 6.02 × 10²³ (small → big)

🧠 Memory Aid
Mole Day: 6:02 on 10/23
6.02 × 10²³

Chemists actually celebrate Mole Day on October 23rd (10/23) at 6:02 AM.

Direction trick: Think of it like hospital shift changes. Going from a BIG number (molecules, like a packed ER) to a SMALL number (moles, like a summary count) → you DIVIDE. Going from a SMALL summary back out → you MULTIPLY.

✏️ Example Problem
Exam: 1.8 × 10²⁴ molecules of CO₂ → how many moles?
1
Big → small, so divide
2
1.8 × 10²⁴ ÷ 6.02 × 10²³ = ~3.0 mol
≈ 3.0 moles of CO₂
🎯 Your Turn

2.4 × 10²⁴ molecules of CH₄. How many moles?

2.4 × 10²⁴ ÷ 6.02 × 10²³ = ~4.0 mol CH₄

Reactants vs. Products

1 Exam Q
📖 Explanation
REACTANTS → PRODUCTS

Left side = Reactants (what goes IN). Right side = Products (what comes OUT).

🧠 Memory Aid
The Kitchen Rule

Reactants are your ingredients. Products are the finished meal.

You READ left to right — Reactants are what you READ first.

Eggs + flour + sugar → cake. Nobody calls the cake an ingredient. Exam question: "What are the substances produced on the right side?" → Products. Always.

✏️ Example Problem
Identify reactants and products: C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O
1
Reactants (left): C₆H₁₂O₆ and O₂
2
Products (right): CO₂ and H₂O
Reactants = C₆H₁₂O₆, O₂  |  Products = CO₂, H₂O

Balancing Chemical Equations

2 Exam Qs
📖 Explanation

Atoms don't appear or vanish. What goes in must come out — just rearranged. Same number of each atom on both sides.

Strategy: Balance metals first, nonmetals next, save H and O for last.

Golden rule: NEVER change subscripts. Subscripts change what the molecule is. Coefficients (the big numbers in front) change how many.

🧠 Memory Aid
The Hospital Census

Every patient who checks IN must also check OUT. No disappearances, no clones.

If 5 patients walk into the ER, 5 must be accounted for in discharge. You can move them to different rooms (rearrange into new compounds), but the head count stays the same. Changing a subscript is like changing a patient's identity — that's fraud. Only change coefficients (how many of each compound you have).

✏️ Example Problem
Balance: Zn + HCl → H₂ + ZnCl₂
1
Count: Left: Zn=1, H=1, Cl=1 | Right: H=2, Zn=1, Cl=2. Not balanced!
2
Fix: Put 2 in front of HCl → Left: H=2, Cl=2
3
Check: Zn: 1=1 ✓ H: 2=2 ✓ Cl: 2=2 ✓
1 Zn + 2 HCl → 1 H₂ + 1 ZnCl₂
🎯 Your Turn

Balance: S₂ + O₂ → SO₂. What are the coefficients?

Put 2 in front of SO₂ (S=2, O=4 on right). Then put 2 in front of O₂ (O=4 on left). Check: S 2=2 ✓ O 4=4 ✓. Coefficients: 1, 2, 2

Endothermic vs. Exothermic

2 Exam Qs
📖 Explanation

🔥 Exothermic

  • Energy is released
  • Surroundings get warmer
  • Fire, breathing, hot packs

🧊 Endothermic

  • Energy is absorbed
  • Surroundings get cooler
  • Ice packs, evaporation, melting
🧠 Memory Aid
The ER Doors
EXO = EXIT  |  ENDO = ENTER

Exothermic: energy exits the reaction (like a patient being discharged — heat leaves and warms the hallway).

Endothermic: energy enters the reaction (like an admission — the reaction sucks heat from the room, so the room feels cold).

The tricky part: "endothermic cools surroundings" sounds backwards. But the reaction is an energy vacuum — it's pulling heat FROM the room into itself. That's why an instant cold pack gets cold. It's not making cold; it's stealing your warmth.

✏️ Example Problem
Exam patterns
1
"Heat is produced during respiration" → Exothermic (heat exits)
2
"A glass of water turns cold" → Endothermic (heat absorbed from water)
3
"Lighting a match / burning paper" → Exothermic
4
"Hot pad for back pain" → Exothermic (releases heat)

Stoichiometry (Equation Conversions)

2 Exam Qs
📖 Explanation

The coefficients in a balanced equation give you the mole ratio. This ratio is your conversion bridge.

3CH₄ → C₃H₈ + 2H₂
3 mol CH₄ : 1 mol C₃H₈ : 2 mol H₂
🧠 Memory Aid
GPS Navigation
Grams A → Moles A → Moles B → Grams B

Think of it like driving with GPS:

🛣️ On-ramp (grams → moles): Divide by molar mass to enter the highway
🔀 Highway (moles A → moles B): Use the coefficient ratio to change lanes
🛣️ Off-ramp (moles → grams): Multiply by molar mass to exit

If you already start in moles, skip the on-ramp. If you only need moles at the end, skip the off-ramp.

✏️ Example Problem
Exam: 0.5 mol C₃H₈ produced → how many mol H₂?
1
Ratio from equation: 1 mol C₃H₈ : 2 mol H₂
2
0.5 × (2/1) = 1.0 mol H₂
1.0 mol H₂
Exam: 3 mol CH₄ → how many grams C₃H₈?
1
Ratio: 3 mol CH₄ → 1 mol C₃H₈. So 3 mol CH₄ = 1 mol C₃H₈
2
Molar mass C₃H₈: 3(12.01) + 8(1.008) = 44.09 g/mol
3
1 mol × 44.09 = 44.09 g
44.09 grams of C₃H₈

Gas Laws

3 Exam Qs
📖 Explanation

Ideal Gas Law:

PV = nRT
P = atm | V = L | n = moles | R = 0.0821 | T = Kelvin

⚠️ ALWAYS convert to Kelvin first: TK = T°C + 273. Forgetting this = wrong answer every time.

Pressure conversion: 1 atm = 760 mmHg. To convert mmHg → atm: divide by 760.

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressures:

PTotal = PA + PB + ...

Each gas contributes its share. Missing one gas? Subtract the others from the total.

🧠 Memory Aid
Two Tricks That Cover All 3 Exam Questions
PIVNERT (say it: "piv-nert")

That's PV = nRT out loud. Say it five times and you'll never forget.

Dalton's Law = "Everyone splits the check."

At a restaurant, each person pays their share (partial pressure). Add everyone's share = total bill (total pressure). One friend forgot their wallet? Total minus everyone else's share = what that friend owes. It's always just addition or subtraction.

✏️ Example Problem
Exam: 2.67 mol gas, 5.00 L, 25.0°C → Find pressure
1
Convert T: 25.0 + 273 = 298 K
2
P = nRT/V = (2.67)(0.0821)(298) / 5.00
3
P = 65.33 / 5.00 = 13.1 atm
P = 13.1 atm
Exam: Blood pressure 100 mmHg → convert to atm
1
100 ÷ 760 = 0.132 atm
0.132 atm
🎯 Your Turn

Neon + Argon mixture: Ptotal = 0.900 atm, PNeon = 0.799 atm. Find PArgon.

PArgon = 0.900 − 0.799 = 0.101 atm. Just subtraction.
