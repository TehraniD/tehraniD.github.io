🛡️ Immune & Hypersensitivity

Hypersensitivity means your immune system has learned to recognize something — and overreacts so badly it causes tissue damage. There are four types, remembered with the mnemonic ACID. Each type differs in which immune cell is responsible and how fast the reaction hits.

🚀 Type I — Allergic / Immediate Hypersensitivity

Fast & Furious — IgE loads the weapon; allergen pulls the trigger

⚡ What Happens

Two-phase process:

  • Sensitization: First exposure → B cells make IgE → IgE binds mast cells & basophils
  • Activation: Re-exposure → allergen crosslinks IgE → mast cells degranulate → HISTAMINE, leukotrienes, prostaglandins released

Onset: 2–30 minutes

👁️ Clinical Signs
  • Urticaria (hives), angioedema
  • Bronchoconstriction, wheezing
  • Rhinorrhea, watery eyes
  • Anaphylaxis: hypotension, airway edema, cardiovascular collapse
  • GI: nausea, vomiting, cramping
🔬 Examples & Dx
  • Anaphylaxis (bee sting, penicillin, peanuts)
  • Allergic rhinitis (hay fever)
  • Asthma (atopic)
  • Food allergies (milk, shellfish, nuts)
  • Latex allergy

Dx: Serum IgE, skin prick test

💊 Treatment
  • Anaphylaxis: Epinephrine 1:1000 IM (thigh) — FIRST LINE
  • O₂, IV fluids, diphenhydramine, corticosteroids
  • Bronchospasm: albuterol nebulizer
  • Chronic: antihistamines, inhaled corticosteroids
  • Desensitization (allergy shots — immunotherapy)
💡

"Type I is like setting a mousetrap. The first exposure SETS the trap — IgE gets loaded onto mast cells. The second exposure? SNAP — mast cells explode histamine everywhere in minutes. You don't react the first time; you react the second."

🩺 Nursing Pearl — Type I
  • Anaphylaxis = Epinephrine FIRST, ALWAYS. Never give antihistamines as monotherapy for anaphylaxis — they don't stop airway edema or cardiovascular collapse.
  • Inject epinephrine in the lateral thigh (vastus lateralis) — fastest absorption. Auto-injectors (EpiPen) go here.
  • Always ask about penicillin allergies — cross-reactivity with cephalosporins is possible (~1–2%).
  • Observe anaphylaxis patients for 4–6 hours after treatment — biphasic reaction can occur hours later.
  • Patient teaching: Carry 2 epinephrine auto-injectors at all times and wear a medical alert bracelet.
📋 At the Bedside — Anaphylaxis

You're a nurse in the outpatient infusion center. A 45-year-old patient is 10 minutes into their first dose of IV ceftriaxone when they suddenly say "my throat feels tight." You look up and notice their lips are swelling, they're developing hives on their chest, and their voice sounds hoarse. Their BP was 128/76 five minutes ago.

What do you do? Tap to reveal →

This is anaphylaxis — a life-threatening Type I hypersensitivity reaction.

Immediate actions (in order):
1. STOP the infusion immediately — disconnect the ceftriaxone, keep the IV patent with NS
2. Call for help — activate rapid response / code team
3. Epinephrine IM 0.3mg (1:1000) in the lateral thigh — THIS IS THE ONLY DRUG THAT SAVES LIVES in anaphylaxis
4. Place patient flat with legs elevated (if not vomiting/dyspneic); high-flow O₂
5. IV NS wide open for hypotension
6. Prepare: diphenhydramine 50mg IV, methylprednisolone 125mg IV, albuterol nebulizer
7. Continuous monitoring: BP, HR, O₂ sat, airway patency
8. Document the allergen, notify pharmacy, update allergy list in the chart

Why you asked about penicillin allergy: Cephalosporins have ~1-2% cross-reactivity with penicillin. This is why we always ask about antibiotic allergies before the first dose. This patient's chart should now list ceftriaxone as an allergy with "anaphylaxis" as the reaction type.

🎯 Type II — Cytotoxic Hypersensitivity

Friendly fire — antibodies target the wrong cell surface and call in the kill squad

⚡ What Happens
  • IgG or IgM antibodies bind to antigens on host cell surfaces
  • Complement system activated → membrane attack complex → cell lysis
  • NK cells and macrophages also recruited (ADCC)
  • Onset: 5–8 hours after exposure
👁️ Clinical Signs
  • Transfusion reaction: fever, chills, back/flank pain, dark urine (hemoglobinuria), hypotension
  • Goodpasture: hemoptysis + hematuria (lungs + kidneys attacked)
  • Hemolytic disease of newborn: jaundice, anemia, hydrops fetalis
  • Myasthenia gravis: muscle weakness (anti-AChR antibodies)
  • Graves' disease: hyperthyroidism (anti-TSH receptor)
🔬 Examples & Dx
  • ABO blood transfusion incompatibility
  • Goodpasture syndrome (anti-GBM antibodies)
  • Hemolytic disease of the newborn (Rh incompatibility)
  • Myasthenia gravis
  • Graves' disease

Dx: Direct Coombs test, complement levels

💊 Treatment
  • Transfusion rx: STOP transfusion immediately, run normal saline
  • Notify provider, collect blood/urine samples
  • Goodpasture: plasmapheresis + immunosuppression + corticosteroids
  • Hemolytic newborn: phototherapy, exchange transfusion if severe
  • Graves'/MG: targeted immunotherapy, thymectomy (MG)
💡

"Imagine the immune system's security guards get handed the wrong wanted poster. They see your own red blood cells and think, 'You look like an enemy — DESTROY.' That's Type II. It's friendly fire with antibody missiles targeting your own cell surfaces."

⚠️ Critical Nursing Action — Transfusion Reaction
  • STOP the transfusion — disconnect from blood bag, keep IV patent with normal saline
  • Stay with the patient — never leave
  • Notify provider and blood bank immediately
  • Return the blood bag to the blood bank
  • Send blood samples and urine for analysis
  • Monitor vitals, UO, and for signs of DIC or acute kidney injury
  • Dark urine = hemoglobinuria = hemolysis = emergency
🏥 Why Does This Matter in Practice?

Transfusion reactions are one of the most common nursing-preventable adverse events. Verification is your firewall. Two nurses independently verify: patient identity (name + DOB + wristband), blood type, unit number, and expiration date — at the bedside, not at the nurses' station. The first 15 minutes are the highest risk period — stay with the patient and take vitals at 5 minutes, 15 minutes, then every 30 minutes. A single mismatch can kill.

🌊 Type III — Immune Complex Hypersensitivity

Trash piles everywhere — antigen-antibody complexes deposit in tissue and spark inflammation

⚡ What Happens
  • Soluble antigen-antibody complexes form in circulation
  • Complexes deposit in vessel walls, joints, kidneys, skin
  • Complement activated → inflammatory cascade → tissue damage
  • Neutrophils recruited → release proteases and ROS → organ damage
  • Onset: 2–8 hours
👁️ Clinical Signs
  • SLE: butterfly rash, arthritis, glomerulonephritis
  • Serum sickness: fever, rash, joint pain, lymphadenopathy 7–14 days after foreign protein injection
  • Rheumatoid arthritis: symmetric joint inflammation
  • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis: dyspnea after antigen inhalation
🔬 Examples & Dx
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)
  • Serum sickness (after antitoxins, biologics)
  • Rheumatoid arthritis
  • Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis
  • Hypersensitivity pneumonitis (farmer's lung)

Dx: Complement levels (C3/C4 low), ANA, anti-dsDNA, biopsy showing immune complex deposition

💊 Treatment
  • NSAIDs for mild inflammation
  • Corticosteroids for moderate-severe
  • Hydroxychloroquine (SLE)
  • Immunosuppressants (methotrexate, azathioprine)
  • Remove triggering antigen when possible
  • Monitor renal function closely (nephritis risk)
💡

"Type III is like your immune system leaving garbage bags all over your body. The trash piles (immune complexes) sit in your kidney filters, joint spaces, and blood vessel walls — and the inflammatory response trying to clean them up destroys the tissue around them."

⏳ Type IV — Delayed / Cell-Mediated Hypersensitivity

The slow burn — T cells hold a grudge and call in reinforcements 1–3 days later

⚡ What Happens
  • NO antibodies involved — purely T-cell mediated
  • Sensitized CD4+ T cells encounter antigen → release cytokines (IFN-γ, IL-2)
  • Macrophages recruited and activated → chronic granulomatous inflammation
  • CD8+ cytotoxic T cells also involved in some reactions
  • Onset: 24–72 hours (hence "delayed")
👁️ Clinical Signs
  • Contact dermatitis: erythema, papules, vesicles at contact site (poison ivy, nickel, latex, fragrances)
  • PPD test: induration at injection site (not just redness)
  • Stevens-Johnson Syndrome: severe mucocutaneous reaction
  • Chronic graft rejection: slowly progressive organ dysfunction
  • Granuloma formation (TB, sarcoidosis, Crohn's)
🔬 Examples & Dx
  • Contact dermatitis (poison ivy, nickel jewelry, latex)
  • Tuberculin skin test (PPD/TST) — read at 48–72 hours
  • Stevens-Johnson Syndrome / TEN
  • Chronic graft rejection (transplants)
  • Type 1 diabetes (autoimmune β-cell destruction)

Dx: Patch testing (contact dermatitis), clinical correlation

💊 Treatment
  • Remove the triggering antigen/contact
  • Topical corticosteroids (contact dermatitis)
  • Oral prednisone for severe reactions
  • Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus cream)
  • Stevens-Johnson: hospital admission, wound care, ophthalmology
  • Graft rejection: immunosuppression (cyclosporine, tacrolimus)
🩺 Nursing Pearl — Type IV (PPD Test)
  • The PPD (tuberculin skin test) is a classic Type IV reaction — read it at 48–72 hours, not immediately.
  • Measure the induration (hardened raised bump), NOT just redness. Use a ruler, in millimeters.
  • Positive cutoffs: ≥5mm (immunocompromised, recent TB contact), ≥10mm (high-risk groups), ≥15mm (general population)
  • A big red bump that disappears by next morning = non-specific. A firm bump that's still there at 72 hours = positive T-cell response.
  • BCG vaccine can cause false-positives — IGRA (QuantiFERON-TB Gold) is preferred in BCG-vaccinated patients.

Immunoglobulins (Antibody Classes) — GAMED Mnemonic
IgG, IgA, IgM, IgE, IgD — what each does and why it matters clinically
▼
🧠 GAMED Mnemonic
  • G = IgG — Greatest abundance; crosses placenta; long-term protection; secondary response
  • A = IgA — At mucosal surfaces (saliva, tears, breast milk, GI, respiratory); secretory immunity
  • M = IgM — Most immediate (first responder in infection); pentameric star shape; largest antibody
  • E = IgE — Eosinophilia and allergies; binds mast cells; elevated in parasitic infections
  • D = IgD — Decorates naive B cells (surface receptor); function still not fully understood; extremely low levels
Most Abundant

IgG

Only Ig that crosses the placenta — gives passive immunity to newborns

Predominant in secondary immune response (long-lasting memory)

Activates complement; opsonizes bacteria (tags for phagocytosis)

Clinical: Low IgG = recurrent bacterial infections. Basis of IVIG therapy.

Mucosal Guardian

IgA

Found in saliva, tears, colostrum, breast milk, GI secretions, respiratory mucus

Secretory IgA (sIgA) — first line of defense at mucosal barriers

Exists as dimer in secretions, monomer in blood

Clinical: Selective IgA deficiency = most common primary immunodeficiency. Recurrent respiratory/GI infections.

First Responder

IgM

First antibody produced in a primary immune response

Pentameric structure (5 units joined) — largest antibody

Excellent complement activator; ABO blood group antibodies are IgM

Clinical: High IgM early in infection. Elevated in Waldenström's macroglobulinemia.

Allergy & Parasites

IgE

Lowest serum concentration but most potent effector for allergies

Binds to mast cells and basophils via Fc receptor → degranulation

Elevated in allergic diseases (asthma, eczema, anaphylaxis) and parasitic infections

Clinical: Serum IgE levels used to diagnose atopic conditions and monitor immunotherapy response.

🔬 Autoimmune Disorders

Autoimmune = your immune system loses tolerance to SELF. Instead of only attacking foreign invaders, it starts attacking your own tissue. Women are disproportionately affected — especially with SLE, where the female-to-male ratio is 9:1. The reason: sex hormones (especially estrogen) modulate immune activity.

🦋 Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

"The Great Imitator" — SLE can look like almost any disease, attacking almost any organ system

⚡ Pathophysiology
  • Type III hypersensitivity — immune complexes deposit in multiple organs
  • Defective apoptosis → nuclear antigens spill into circulation
  • B cells produce autoantibodies (ANA, anti-dsDNA, anti-Smith) against nuclear components
  • Immune complexes deposit in kidneys, skin, joints, blood vessels, CNS → inflammation
  • Complement consumed (C3, C4 levels fall)
👁️ SOAP BRAIN MD Criteria
  • S = Serositis (pericarditis, pleuritis)
  • O = Oral ulcers (painless)
  • A = Arthritis (non-erosive, bilateral)
  • P = Photosensitivity
  • B = Blood disorders (anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia)
  • R = Renal (lupus nephritis)
  • A = ANA positive
  • I = Immunologic (anti-dsDNA, anti-Smith)
  • N = Neurologic (seizures, psychosis)
  • M = Malar (butterfly) rash
  • D = Discoid rash
🔍 Diagnosis & Labs
  • ANA — positive in >95% of SLE (sensitive, not specific)
  • Anti-dsDNA — most SPECIFIC; correlates with disease activity
  • Anti-Smith — highly specific but less sensitive
  • ↓ Complement (C3, C4) during flares
  • CBC: anemia, thrombocytopenia, leukopenia
  • Urinalysis: proteinuria, hematuria, casts
  • Demographics: Women 20–40, African American/Hispanic > White
💊 Treatment
  • Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil) — mainstay; reduces flares, eye exam annually
  • NSAIDs — for mild joint/serositis symptoms
  • Corticosteroids — for flares (pred dose based on severity)
  • Immunosuppressants: mycophenolate, azathioprine, cyclophosphamide (nephritis)
  • Belimumab (Benlysta) — B-lymphocyte stimulator inhibitor; biologic
  • Nephritis: ACE inhibitor + aggressive immunosuppression
🧠 SOAP BRAIN MD — SLE Diagnostic Criteria

Need 4 of 11 criteria for diagnosis (or biopsy-proven lupus nephritis + ANA/anti-dsDNA).

Most dangerous: Renal involvement (lupus nephritis) — leading cause of morbidity and historically of death. Monitor creatinine, BUN, and urine protein EVERY visit.

📋 Clinical Scenario

You're caring for a 28-year-old African American woman admitted with facial rash that worsened after a beach trip last week, bilateral hand joint pain, and new-onset hypertension. Her urinalysis shows 3+ protein and 10–15 RBCs/hpf. She asks you, "Why are my kidneys involved? I thought this was a skin problem."

Tap to reveal clinical answer →

This patient has SLE (lupus). SLE is a Type III hypersensitivity disease — immune complexes containing DNA and antibodies are deposited in the glomeruli, triggering complement activation and inflammation (lupus nephritis). The kidneys act as a filter, and immune complexes get trapped there. UV light exposure (beach trip) triggered her flare by causing more nuclear antigen release. You explain: "Lupus can affect many organs because immune complexes travel through your bloodstream and deposit wherever blood flows — including the kidneys. We'll monitor your kidney function closely with daily weights, urine output, blood pressure, and lab work." Nursing priorities: BP control, daily weights, strict I&O, renal function labs (creatinine, BUN, urinalysis), educate about sun protection and medication adherence.

🏥 Why Does This Matter in Practice?

SLE disproportionately affects young Black women — with more severe disease, earlier onset, and higher rates of lupus nephritis. Health disparities in SLE outcomes are driven by delayed diagnosis, socioeconomic barriers, and racial bias in pain assessment. As a nurse, you play a critical role: take complaints seriously regardless of demographics, advocate for timely rheumatology referral, and educate patients about medication adherence (especially hydroxychloroquine — the most important drug in SLE management). A 25-year-old woman with joint pain and a rash deserves the same diagnostic workup as anyone else.

🩺 Nursing Pearls — SLE
  • Sun protection is NOT optional — UV light triggers SLE flares. Teach SPF 50+, protective clothing, avoid peak sun hours (10am–4pm).
  • Fatigue is the #1 complaint — it's real, not laziness. Validate the patient's experience. Energy conservation strategies matter.
  • Monitor renal function every visit — proteinuria, creatinine, blood pressure. Lupus nephritis is the leading cause of SLE-related death.
  • SLE flares during pregnancy are common — requires high-risk obstetrics, do not stop hydroxychloroquine (it's actually protective in pregnancy).
  • Hydroxychloroquine: Annual ophthalmology exams — retinal toxicity is a rare but serious long-term side effect.
  • Watch for signs of opportunistic infection in patients on immunosuppressants — even a mild fever warrants investigation.

🦴 Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)

Autoimmune attack on synovial joints — tiny construction workers gone rogue, dismantling your own cartilage

⚡ Pathophysiology
  • Autoimmune attack on synovial membrane → synovitis
  • T cells activate macrophages → release TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6
  • Pannus formation — vascular fibrous granulation tissue invades cartilage and bone
  • Erosion of cartilage → narrowing of joint space
  • Bone erosion → joint deformity over years
  • Systemic inflammation affects other organs (lungs, heart, eyes)
👁️ Clinical Signs
  • Morning stiffness >1 hour — key hallmark
  • Symmetric joint involvement (both hands, both wrists)
  • Warm, swollen, tender joints
  • Improves with movement (vs OA which worsens)
  • Ulnar deviation of fingers
  • Swan-neck deformity, Boutonnière deformity
  • Rheumatoid nodules (subcutaneous, over bony prominences)
  • Systemic: fatigue, weight loss, low-grade fever
🔍 Diagnosis
  • Rheumatoid Factor (RF) — positive in ~80%; not specific (also + in other diseases)
  • Anti-CCP (cyclic citrullinated peptide) — more specific; positive years before symptoms
  • ↑ ESR, ↑ CRP — markers of inflammation
  • X-ray: joint space narrowing, periarticular erosions, osteopenia
  • MRI: earlier detection of synovitis and erosions
  • Synovial fluid: cloudy, high WBC, low glucose
💊 Treatment Ladder
  • NSAIDs — symptom relief only; do NOT stop disease progression
  • DMARDs (Disease-Modifying): Methotrexate — FIRST LINE; weekly dose; monitor LFTs + CBC
  • Hydroxychloroquine, sulfasalazine, leflunomide
  • Biologics: TNF inhibitors (adalimumab, etanercept, infliximab); anti-IL-6 (tocilizumab); anti-CD20 (rituximab)
  • JAK inhibitors (tofacitinib, baricitinib) — newer oral DMARDs
  • Corticosteroids: bridge therapy during flares
💡

"Imagine tiny construction workers in your joints that are supposed to maintain the building — but they've gone haywire and are demolishing it from the inside. The pannus tissue is the wrecking crew eating away at cartilage and bone. Morning stiffness is like the wrecking crew starting fresh each night and you having to restart the joint machinery every morning."

✅ Normal Joint

• Thin synovial membrane — produces lubricating fluid
• Smooth articular cartilage — cushions bone ends
• Narrow, uniform joint space
• Full range of motion, no swelling
• Morning stiffness: none or <15 minutes

❌ RA Joint

• Inflamed, thickened synovium — produces pannus tissue
• Pannus invades and erodes cartilage and bone
• Narrowed, irregular joint space with erosions
• Warm, swollen, tender; ulnar deviation, deformities
• Morning stiffness: >1 hour (improves with movement)

🩺 Nursing Pearls — Rheumatoid Arthritis
  • Morning stiffness lasting >1 hour = classic RA flag. Ask every joint-pain patient: "How long does your morning stiffness last?" OA stiffness resolves in <30 min and worsens with use.
  • Methotrexate: Monitor LFTs and CBC monthly (hepatotoxicity + bone marrow suppression). Folic acid supplementation prevents side effects — it's prescribed alongside MTX.
  • Patients on biologics (adalimumab, etanercept) are immunocompromised — no live vaccines. Screen for TB (latent TB can reactivate) before starting TNF inhibitors.
  • Encourage warm water therapy in the morning before activity — joint mobilization reduces stiffness.
  • "Motion is lotion" — gentle, regular exercise helps RA. Immobility accelerates joint damage.
  • Sjögren's overlap: RA patients commonly have dry eyes and dry mouth. Ask about these symptoms.

⚠️ Common Trap

Students often confuse RA and OA. Remember: RA wakes you up stiff for hours in the morning and loosens up as you move. OA starts okay in the morning and gets worse the more you use the joint throughout the day. RA involves inflammation and is systemic; OA is degenerative and localized.

🏥 Immunodeficiency

Immunodeficiency means the body's defense army is undermanned, undertrained, or completely absent. Primary immunodeficiencies are genetic — you're born with them. Secondary immunodeficiencies are acquired — HIV is the most important example globally. The clinical clue: patients who get recurrent, severe, or unusual infections that healthy immune systems would handle easily.

🔑 Clinical Clue — When to Suspect Immunodeficiency
  • 4+ ear infections per year (in children)
  • 2+ serious bacterial infections (pneumonia, meningitis, sepsis) per year
  • Infections with unusual organisms (opportunistic pathogens like PCP, Cryptococcus)
  • Failure to thrive, persistent oral thrush beyond infancy
  • Family history of immunodeficiency or unexplained infant deaths
  • Poor response to antibiotics

🛡️ Selective IgA Deficiency

Most common primary immunodeficiency — the mucosal security guard is missing

⚡ What Happens
  • Body lacks IgA — specifically secretory IgA at mucosal surfaces
  • IgA normally guards: mouth, nose, lungs, GI tract, GU tract
  • Without IgA, pathogens colonize mucosal surfaces more easily
  • IgG and IgM levels are NORMAL — that's why many patients stay healthy
  • Most common primary immunodeficiency: ~1 in 600 in Western populations
👁️ Clinical Features
  • Most patients: ASYMPTOMATIC — discovered incidentally on bloodwork
  • When symptomatic: recurrent sinusitis, otitis media, bronchitis, pneumonia
  • GI infections — especially Giardia lamblia
  • Increased risk of celiac disease and inflammatory bowel disease
  • Increased autoimmune risk (RA, lupus, thyroid disease)
  • Anaphylaxis risk with blood products containing IgA
🔍 Diagnosis
  • Serum IgA undetectable or very low (<7 mg/dL)
  • IgG and IgM normal or elevated (compensating)
  • Normal T-cell counts and function
  • Diagnosis often incidental — routine labs, pre-surgery workup
  • Rule out secondary causes (medications, malignancy)
💊 Treatment & Nursing
  • No curative treatment — most require no specific therapy
  • Treat infections aggressively with antibiotics
  • Prophylactic antibiotics in recurrent infection cases
  • CRITICAL: If IVIG needed, use IgA-depleted IVIG — regular IVIG can trigger life-threatening anaphylaxis (anti-IgA antibodies)
  • Medical alert bracelet recommended
  • Reassure: most patients live completely normal lives
🩺 Nursing Pearl — IgA Deficiency
  • Always screen patients for IgA deficiency before giving blood products — document and alert blood bank.
  • A child getting recurrent sinus infections and pneumonias despite good hygiene, antibiotics, and no anatomic issues — think IgA deficiency. A simple serum IgA level diagnoses it.
  • Reassure families: most IgA-deficient patients live fully normal lives. The condition is not dangerous for the majority.

🧬 DiGeorge Syndrome (22q11.2 Deletion)

No thymus = no T-cell training camp — and no parathyroids either

⚡ What Happens
  • Deletion of chromosome 22q11 → failure of 3rd and 4th pharyngeal pouches to develop
  • Thymus absent or hypoplastic → no T-cell maturation
  • Parathyroid glands absent → hypocalcemia (tetany, seizures)
  • B cells present but without T-cell help → poor antibody response
  • Susceptible to viral, fungal, and intracellular bacterial infections
👁️ CATCH-22 Features
  • C = Cardiac defects (conotruncal — ToF, truncus arteriosus, interrupted aortic arch)
  • A = Abnormal facies (small ears, wide-set eyes, bulbous nose)
  • T = Thymic aplasia → absent T cells → recurrent viral/fungal infections
  • C = Cleft palate / velopharyngeal insufficiency
  • H = Hypocalcemia → tetany (carpopedal spasm), seizures in newborns
🔍 Diagnosis
  • FISH or chromosomal microarray: 22q11.2 deletion
  • Low T-cell counts (CD3, CD4, CD8)
  • Hypocalcemia (↓ Ca²⁺, ↑ PTH absent)
  • Absent thymic shadow on chest X-ray (newborns)
  • Echo: evaluate cardiac defects
  • Flow cytometry: T-cell enumeration
💊 Treatment
  • Calcium supplementation (IV calcium gluconate for acute hypocalcemia)
  • Vitamin D supplementation (chronic management)
  • Cardiac surgery for congenital heart defects
  • Thymic tissue transplant (for complete DiGeorge)
  • Prophylactic TMP-SMX (PCP prevention)
  • No live vaccines — can cause fatal disseminated infection
  • Speech therapy (velopharyngeal dysfunction)
💡

"The thymus is the T-cell training camp. Without it, immature T cells never graduate — they can't learn to recognize self vs. non-self, can't coordinate immune attacks on viruses or fungi. DiGeorge is like closing down the military academy while still expecting a functioning army."

🏥 Why Does This Matter in Practice?

A newborn with DiGeorge presents as a medical puzzle: the NICU team sees a baby with a heart murmur, low calcium causing seizures, and unusual facial features. Your job as a nurse is to connect the dots. If you see a neonate with hypocalcemia + cardiac defect — think DiGeorge, think T-cell deficiency, and think: NO live vaccines, infection precautions, and calcium monitoring. One diagnosis explains everything. Document the 22q11.2 deletion prominently — every provider for life needs to know.

☣️ Severe Combined Immunodeficiency (SCID)

"Bubble Boy Disease" — both T and B cells absent; fatal without treatment in the first year of life

⚡ Pathophysiology
  • Both T cells AND B cells absent or severely dysfunctional
  • Most common: X-linked SCID — mutation in IL-2 receptor γ-chain (common gamma chain); males affected
  • Also: ADA deficiency (adenosine deaminase) — toxic metabolites kill lymphocytes
  • Without T cells: no viral/fungal immunity
  • Without B cells (or T-cell help): no antibody production
  • Total immune collapse — every pathogen is lethal
👁️ Clinical Presentation
  • Recurrent, severe infections from birth (bacterial, viral, fungal)
  • Failure to thrive, poor weight gain
  • Persistent oral thrush (Candida)
  • Chronic diarrhea
  • PCP pneumonia (Pneumocystis jirovecii)
  • Absent lymph nodes and tonsils (no lymphoid tissue)
  • Fatal without BMT typically within first year of life
🔍 Diagnosis
  • Absent T and B cells on flow cytometry
  • Very low immunoglobulins (all classes)
  • Absent thymic shadow on CXR
  • Lymphopenia on CBC
  • Newborn screening (TREC assay) — standard in US since 2011
  • Genetic testing confirms specific mutation type
💊 Treatment
  • Hematopoietic stem cell (bone marrow) transplant — only cure; best outcomes before 3 months of age
  • Gene therapy (approved for ADA-SCID and X-linked SCID) — emerging curative option
  • Protective isolation until transplant
  • IVIG supplementation (interim)
  • Prophylactic antibiotics, antifungals, antivirals
  • NO live vaccines EVER — vaccine strain can cause fatal infection
  • Irradiated, CMV-negative blood products
⚠️ Critical Safety — SCID and Vaccines

Live vaccines are absolutely contraindicated in SCID patients. Even the attenuated vaccine strain can cause fatal disseminated infection. This includes MMR, varicella, rotavirus, and BCG. If a sibling is vaccinated with live virus, the SCID patient must avoid contact for 2–3 weeks (rotavirus especially — shed in stool). Alert ALL providers and the family.

🔴 HIV / AIDS

Secondary immunodeficiency — HIV hacks the immune command center, leaving the body defenseless

⚡ Pathophysiology
  • HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) — retrovirus with RNA genome
  • Binds CD4+ T cells (and macrophages, dendritic cells) via CD4 + CCR5/CXCR4 co-receptors
  • Reverse transcriptase converts viral RNA → DNA → integrates into host genome
  • Host cell destroyed during viral replication → CD4 count falls progressively
  • Without CD4 T cells: B cells can't mount antibody responses; CD8 cells lose coordination → global immune failure
📊 CD4 Count & Disease Staging
  • >500 cells/µL — Asymptomatic / early infection; flu-like acute retroviral syndrome
  • 200–499 — Symptomatic: oral thrush, recurrent herpes, night sweats, lymphadenopathy
  • <200 — AIDS-defining: PCP, toxoplasma, wasting syndrome, major OIs
  • <100 — Cryptococcal meningitis, MAC (Mycobacterium avium complex)
  • <50 — CMV retinitis (risk of blindness), disseminated MAC
🦠 Opportunistic Infections
  • PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii) — most common AIDS-defining illness; treat with TMP-SMX
  • Toxoplasma gondii — CNS lesions (ring-enhancing on MRI); reactivation from latent infection
  • Cryptococcal meningitis — severe headache, altered mental status; Dx: cryptococcal antigen, India ink
  • CMV retinitis — floaters, vision changes; urgently treat to prevent blindness
  • MAC — fever, weight loss, anemia, diarrhea in advanced AIDS
  • TB co-infection — most common co-infection globally
💊 Treatment & Prevention
  • cART (combination antiretroviral therapy) — suppresses viral load to undetectable; 3+ drugs from different classes
  • Goal: viral load undetectable, CD4 recovery
  • PrEP (pre-exposure prophylaxis) — tenofovir/emtricitabine for high-risk uninfected individuals; very effective
  • PEP (post-exposure prophylaxis) — within 72 hours of exposure; 28-day course
  • Prophylaxis: TMP-SMX (PCP, toxo) when CD4 <200; azithromycin (MAC) when CD4 <50
  • Annual flu vaccine; avoid live vaccines when CD4 <200
💡

"HIV is like a spy that infiltrates and destroys your military command center — the CD4 T cells. Once enough generals are gone, the troops lose their orders. Opportunistic infections — organisms that would be easily defeated in a healthy body — waltz right in. AIDS is what happens when the command center has been so thoroughly destroyed that even common pathogens become life-threatening."

🩺 Nursing Pearls — HIV/AIDS
  • U = U: Undetectable = Untransmittable — teach patients on cART that if their viral load is consistently undetectable, they cannot sexually transmit HIV. This is empowering and well-established science.
  • Adherence is everything — missing even occasional doses leads to resistance. Explore barriers (cost, side effects, stigma, depression) at every visit.
  • Screen for TB at every visit — TB is the #1 infectious killer of people with HIV globally.
  • Lipodystrophy (fat redistribution — central obesity, facial wasting) is a side effect of older ARVs; reassure patients and document.
  • Psychosocial support is as critical as medication — depression, anxiety, and social stigma dramatically reduce adherence and outcomes.
  • Any new neurological symptoms (headache, confusion, focal deficits) in an AIDS patient = urgent evaluation (rule out toxoplasmosis, cryptococcal meningitis, PML).
  • Monitor CD4 count and viral load every 3–6 months on cART; every 3 months in the first year of therapy.

His CXR shows diffuse bilateral ground-glass opacities. His rapid HIV test comes back positive, and labs reveal a CD4 count of 87 cells/µL and a viral load of 450,000 copies/mL. He looks terrified and whispers, "Am I going to die?"

Clinical picture: This is AIDS (CD4 <200) with PCP pneumonia (Pneumocystis jirovecii — bilateral ground-glass opacities on CXR + immunocompromised). This is the most common AIDS-defining illness.

Immediate clinical priorities:

👶 ALL — Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia

Most common childhood cancer — immature B-cell blasts flood the marrow, crowding out everything healthy

⚡ Pathophysiology
  • Malignant transformation of immature lymphoid precursors (mostly B cells, ~15% T cells)
  • Lymphoblasts proliferate uncontrollably → crowd out normal RBC, WBC, platelet precursors
  • Marrow failure → anemia, thrombocytopenia, neutropenia
  • Peak age: 2–5 years; second peak in adults >50
  • Associated: Down syndrome (10–20x increased risk), previous chemotherapy
👁️ Clinical Signs
  • Pallor, fatigue, dyspnea (anemia)
  • Petechiae, bruising, bleeding gums (thrombocytopenia)
  • Recurrent infections, fever (neutropenia)
  • Bone pain — especially at night; children limp without injury
  • Lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, hepatomegaly
  • CNS involvement: headache, cranial nerve palsies, papilledema
  • Mediastinal mass (T-cell ALL) — may compress airway!
🔍 Diagnosis
  • CBC: high WBC with lymphoblasts; low RBC, Hgb, platelets
  • Bone marrow biopsy: >20% blasts = diagnostic
  • Blood smear: lymphoblasts (large nuclei, scant cytoplasm)
  • Flow cytometry: immunophenotyping (B vs T cell markers)
  • Cytogenetics: Philadelphia chromosome (BCR-ABL) in some adult ALL — affects treatment
  • LP: CSF evaluation for CNS involvement
💊 Treatment & Prognosis
  • Chemotherapy in phases: Induction → Consolidation → Maintenance
  • Induction drugs: vincristine, prednisone, L-asparaginase, daunorubicin
  • CNS prophylaxis: intrathecal chemotherapy (MTX directly into spinal fluid)
  • Ph+ ALL (Philadelphia chromosome): add imatinib (TKI) to chemo
  • BMT: for high-risk or relapsed cases
  • Prognosis in children: 85–90% cure rate! Adults fare significantly worse (~40% 5-year survival)
🩺 Nursing Pearl — ALL in Children
  • Bone pain in a child (especially limping without injury) + fatigue + recurrent infections + pallor = think leukemia. Refer for urgent CBC.
  • During chemotherapy: neutropenic precautions — private room, HEPA filter, no fresh flowers/fruits, anyone with illness stays away.
  • Tumor lysis syndrome (TLS) — a potentially fatal complication when treatment begins. Watch for: hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia, hyperuricemia (gout + renal failure). Treat with aggressive hydration, allopurinol, rasburicase.
  • Emotional support for the family is as important as medical management — connect to child life specialists and social work.
📋 At the Bedside — Childhood Leukemia

You're a pediatric nurse. A 4-year-old boy is brought in by his mother who says, "He won't stop crying about his legs hurting at night, and he's been bruising so easily — I barely touch him." On exam, you notice pallor, scattered petechiae on his trunk, and palpable cervical lymph nodes. He has a low-grade fever of 100.4°F. His mom asks, "Is this just a virus?"

What are you thinking? Tap to reveal →

This constellation is classic for ALL: bone pain (marrow expansion from blasts) + easy bruising/petechiae (thrombocytopenia) + pallor (anemia) + lymphadenopathy + low-grade fever (neutropenia or disease-related). Peak age 2-5 years.

Your nursing actions:
1. Draw a STAT CBC with differential — you're looking for blasts on the differential, anemia, thrombocytopenia, and abnormal WBC
2. Document the timeline of symptoms for the oncology team
3. If CBC is suspicious (blasts present, pancytopenia), prepare the family for: bone marrow biopsy, likely admission, potential oncology transfer
4. Be honest with mom: "These symptoms together concern me. The blood work will give us important answers quickly."
5. Emotional support: Don't say "I'm sure it's nothing" — but also don't diagnose cancer. Let the labs guide the conversation.

Clinical pearl: Night-time bone pain in a child that wakes them from sleep is NOT growing pains — growing pains are bilateral, in the muscles (not bones), and don't cause petechiae or pallor. This child needs a CBC today.

👴 CLL — Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia

Most common adult leukemia — often found accidentally on a routine CBC in an asymptomatic 70-year-old

⚡ Pathophysiology
  • Malignant proliferation of mature-appearing but non-functional B lymphocytes
  • B cells accumulate — look like mature lymphocytes but can't fight infections (immunocompromised despite "normal" WBC)
  • Median age at diagnosis: 71 years
  • Most common adult leukemia in the Western world
  • Indolent — slow growing, often years from diagnosis to treatment
👁️ Clinical Signs
  • Often ASYMPTOMATIC — incidentally found on CBC
  • Lymphadenopathy (rubbery, non-tender)
  • Splenomegaly and hepatomegaly
  • Fatigue, weight loss (late disease)
  • Recurrent infections (non-functional lymphocytes can't produce antibodies)
  • Autoimmune hemolytic anemia (warm AIHA — Coombs positive)
🔍 Diagnosis
  • CBC: markedly elevated lymphocyte count (>5,000 monoclonal B cells/µL)
  • Smudge cells on peripheral smear — pathognomonic finding (fragile CLL cells crushed during slide prep)
  • Flow cytometry: CD5+, CD19+, CD23+ (characteristic CLL phenotype)
  • Low immunoglobulin levels despite elevated lymphocytes
  • Cytogenetics: del(17p) = worst prognosis; del(13q) = best
💊 Treatment
  • Watch and wait — asymptomatic, low-risk CLL does NOT require immediate treatment
  • When treated: BTK inhibitors (ibrutinib — now preferred); venetoclax (BCL-2 inhibitor)
  • Older: FCR chemo + rituximab (anti-CD20)
  • IVIG for recurrent infections (hypogammaglobulinemia)
  • Monitor for: Richter's transformation — CLL converting to aggressive DLBCL (sudden deterioration)
⚠️ Key Trap — CLL and Infection

CLL patients look like they have an adequate WBC count, but their lymphocytes are non-functional — they can't produce normal immunoglobulins. A CLL patient with a "minor" infection can deteriorate rapidly. Never dismiss infections in CLL patients as trivial. Any fever or signs of infection in a CLL patient warrants prompt evaluation and a low threshold for antibiotic treatment.

🏥 Why Does This Matter in Practice?

CLL teaches a critical nursing concept: the WBC count can lie. A patient with CLL may have a WBC of 80,000 — sounds like their immune system is working overtime, right? Wrong. Those 80,000 lymphocytes are non-functional. They can't make antibodies. The patient is effectively immunocompromised despite an impressive-looking number. Always look at the differential and the immunoglobulin levels, not just the total WBC. This same principle applies to any hematologic malignancy — quantity ≠ quality.

🦉 Hodgkin's Lymphoma (HL)

Reed-Sternberg "owl eye" cells — the pathognomonic hallmark; highly curable with modern treatment

⚡ Pathophysiology
  • Malignant B lymphocytes undergo clonal transformation → Reed-Sternberg cells (RS cells)
  • RS cells are large binucleated cells with prominent eosinophilic nucleoli — "owl eyes"
  • Associated with Epstein-Barr virus (EBV)
  • Bimodal age distribution: 20s and 60s
  • Spreads contiguously — orderly progression from one lymph node group to the adjacent group
  • Arises most commonly in cervical lymph nodes
👁️ Clinical Signs
  • Painless lymphadenopathy — usually cervical/supraclavicular; rubbery, firm
  • B symptoms (worse prognosis): Fever, Drenching Night Sweats, Weight loss >10% in 6 months
  • Pruritus (itching) without rash — from cytokines
  • Pel-Ebstein fever: alternating fever/afebrile cycles — classic but rare
  • Alcohol-induced nodal pain — unusual but classic sign of HL
  • Mediastinal involvement → cough, dyspnea, SVC syndrome
🔍 Diagnosis & Staging
  • Excisional lymph node biopsy — must see Reed-Sternberg cells
  • RS cells: CD15+, CD30+; negative for CD45 and B-cell markers
  • CT scan (chest/abdomen/pelvis) and PET scan for staging
  • Ann Arbor staging (I–IV) based on nodal regions involved and B symptoms

💊 Treatment & Prognosis
  • ABVD chemotherapy: Adriamycin (doxorubicin), Bleomycin, Vinblastine, Dacarbazine
  • Radiation therapy — for early stage or residual disease
  • Prognosis: EXCELLENT — 86% overall 5-year survival; early stage >95%
  • Brentuximab vedotin (anti-CD30): relapsed/refractory HL
  • PD-1 inhibitors (nivolumab, pembrolizumab): for R/R HL — immunotherapy revolution
  • Late effects of treatment: secondary cancers, cardiac toxicity (doxorubicin), pulmonary fibrosis (bleomycin)
🩺 Nursing Pearl — Hodgkin's Lymphoma
  • Painless cervical lymphadenopathy in a young adult + B symptoms (night sweats, fever, weight loss) = Hodgkin's until proven otherwise. Don't dismiss "rubbery" neck nodes in a 22-year-old.
  • ABVD side effects to monitor: doxorubicin (cardiotoxicity — LVEF before and during), bleomycin (pulmonary toxicity — PFTs, watch for O₂ requirement), vinblastine (neuropathy, constipation).
  • Alcohol-induced nodal pain is a rare but classic clue — ask about it directly if you suspect HL.
  • Long-term survivors need ongoing monitoring: secondary malignancies (breast cancer in women who received chest radiation), thyroid dysfunction, cardiovascular disease.

🌐 Non-Hodgkin's Lymphoma (NHL)

5x more common than Hodgkin's — over 60 subtypes, spreads unpredictably, affects B and T cells

⚡ Key Features
  • Heterogeneous group — >60 distinct subtypes of lymphoma
  • Median age >50; males slightly more affected
  • Affects B cells (most), T cells, NK cells
  • Spreads non-contiguously — can skip lymph node groups, involve extranodal sites (stomach, brain, skin, bone marrow, GI)
  • Risk factors: immunosuppression (HIV, transplant), EBV (Burkitt's), H. pylori (MALT lymphoma), autoimmune disease
👁️ Clinical Signs
  • Painless lymphadenopathy (can be waxing and waning in low-grade)
  • B symptoms less reliable than in HL
  • Extranodal symptoms: abdominal pain/mass, neurological changes, GI bleeding
  • Waxing/waning lymphadenopathy = low-grade follicular lymphoma hallmark
  • Constitutional symptoms (fatigue, weight loss, night sweats)
🔬 Key Subtypes
  • DLBCL (Diffuse Large B-Cell Lymphoma) — most common in adults; aggressive but curable in ~60%
  • Follicular lymphoma — second most common; indolent (low-grade); not curable with standard therapy but long survival
  • Burkitt's lymphoma — children/young adults; "starry sky" appearance on biopsy; African type = jaw mass; very aggressive; highly curable with intensive chemo
  • MALT lymphoma — associated with H. pylori; gastric; treat H. pylori → lymphoma regresses!
  • Mantle cell lymphoma — poor prognosis; older men
💊 Treatment
  • Aggressive subtypes (DLBCL): R-CHOP — Rituximab (anti-CD20) + Cyclophosphamide, Hydroxydaunorubicin, Oncovin (vincristine), Prednisone
  • Follicular lymphoma: watch and wait (low-grade) → rituximab ± chemo when symptomatic
  • Burkitt's: intensive multi-drug chemotherapy; CNS prophylaxis required
  • CAR-T cell therapy: approved for relapsed/refractory DLBCL — revolutionary cellular immunotherapy
  • BMT: for eligible relapsed patients
  • Diagnosis requires: excisional lymph node biopsy — core needle biopsy insufficient for subtyping
🩺 Nursing Pearl — NHL
  • "Waxing and waning" lymphadenopathy (nodes swell, shrink, swell again) = classic low-grade NHL (follicular lymphoma). Report any unexplained lymphadenopathy persisting >2–4 weeks.
  • Rituximab (anti-CD20): watch for infusion reactions during first infusion — pre-medicate with acetaminophen + diphenhydramine. Slow infusion rate, have emergency medications ready.
  • CAR-T therapy patients: risk of cytokine release syndrome (CRS) — fever, hypotension, hypoxia — and neurotoxicity (ICANS). These patients need specialized monitoring post-infusion.

🩸 Iron-Deficiency Anemia (IDA)

Most common anemia worldwide — small, pale red cells from an empty iron tank

⚡ Pathophysiology
  • Iron required for hemoglobin synthesis (heme component)
  • Iron depletion → ↓ heme production → small (microcytic), pale (hypochromic) RBCs
  • Causes: blood loss (heavy menstruation, GI bleed — most common), poor dietary intake (vegetarians, infants), malabsorption (celiac disease, Crohn's, post-gastrectomy)
  • At-risk: premenopausal women, children, vegans, elderly with GI disease
👁️ Clinical Signs
  • Universal anemia signs (fatigue, pallor, tachycardia)
  • PICA — craving non-food items: ice (pagophagia — most common), dirt, clay, starch
  • Koilonychia — spoon-shaped nails
  • Glossitis (smooth, red, sore tongue), angular stomatitis
  • Hair loss (telogen effluvium)
  • Restless leg syndrome
  • Plummer-Vinson syndrome: IDA + dysphagia + esophageal web
🔍 Diagnosis
  • CBC: ↓ Hgb, ↓ MCV, ↓ MCH (hypochromic)
  • ↓ Serum ferritin (best marker of iron stores — falls first!)
  • ↑ TIBC (total iron-binding capacity) — more transferrin available to bind iron
  • ↓ Serum iron
  • Peripheral smear: small, pale RBCs with enlarged central pallor
  • Reticulocytes: low (marrow can't make new cells without iron)
  • IDA in adults especially older men → GI malignancy until ruled out!
💊 Treatment
  • Oral ferrous sulfate 325mg TID — take on EMPTY stomach for best absorption
  • Take with vitamin C (orange juice) — enhances absorption
  • Avoid: calcium, antacids, dairy with iron (block absorption)
  • IV iron (ferric carboxymaltose, iron sucrose): when oral not tolerated, malabsorption, or rapid correction needed
  • Blood transfusion: only for symptomatic severe anemia or hemodynamic compromise
  • Recheck CBC in 4–6 weeks (reticulocytosis first, then Hgb rises)
  • Iron stores take 3–6 months to replenish — continue therapy!
  • Address underlying cause (GI endoscopy, treat heavy periods)
🩺 Nursing Pearls — Iron-Deficiency Anemia
  • Iron deficiency in an older adult (especially men, post-menopausal women) without obvious cause = GI cancer until proven otherwise. Arrange colonoscopy/upper endoscopy, not just iron supplements.
  • Teach iron administration: empty stomach (or with OJ), not with milk or antacids. If GI upset, try taking with a small amount of food but absorption will be slightly reduced.
  • Warn about dark/black stools — normal with oral iron. Green stools can also occur. Reassure the patient; don't confuse with GI bleeding (melena is sticky and malodorous).
  • Liquid iron formulations: take through a straw or dropper to prevent tooth staining.
  • Constipation is the #1 side effect of oral iron — increase fluid and fiber intake; stool softeners as needed.
  • Ice craving (pagophagia) in a patient with fatigue and pallor = think IDA immediately. Ask about it.
📋 At the Bedside — The Iron Puzzle

You're doing intake on a 68-year-old retired teacher, Mr. Davis, who was referred by his PCP for "anemia workup." His Hgb is 8.2 (low), MCV 72 (microcytic), ferritin 6 (very low). He says he feels "run down" and has been craving ice chips constantly. He tells you, "My doctor just wants to put me on iron pills, but I eat red meat every week. How can I be low on iron?"

What's really going on here? Tap to reveal →

Red flag alarm: Iron deficiency anemia in a 68-year-old male who eats meat is NOT a dietary problem — it's a GI blood loss problem until proven otherwise. This could be colon cancer, gastric ulcer, or another GI malignancy causing chronic, occult blood loss.

Your nursing response:
1. This patient needs a colonoscopy and upper endoscopy BEFORE just taking iron pills — iron pills will treat the symptom but miss a potential cancer diagnosis
2. Order a stool guaiac (FOBT/FIT) — check for occult blood
3. Ask about: dark/tarry stools (melena), blood in stool, unintentional weight loss, change in bowel habits, abdominal pain
4. Ice craving (pagophagia) is a classic sign of IDA — document it
5. Explain to Mr. Davis: "Even though you eat well, your body may be losing iron faster than you can replace it. We need to find out where it's going."

The lesson: Never just hand an older adult an iron prescription and send them home. Find the source. IDA in older men and postmenopausal women = GI malignancy screening is mandatory.

🧠 Pernicious Anemia (B12 Deficiency)

The doorman is gone — without intrinsic factor, B12 can never get through the door, no matter how much you eat

⚡ Pathophysiology
  • Autoimmune destruction of gastric parietal cells → no intrinsic factor (IF) produced
  • IF binds B12 in the stomach → complex absorbed in terminal ileum
  • Without IF: B12 not absorbed → deficiency despite adequate dietary intake
  • B12 required for: DNA synthesis (cell division) + myelin synthesis (nerve function)
  • Megaloblastic anemia: large, oval RBCs (DNA can't replicate fast enough)
  • Neurological damage: demyelination of posterior/lateral spinal columns
👁️ Clinical Signs

The key: ANEMIA + NEUROLOGY together!

  • Macrocytic anemia: fatigue, pallor, weakness, dyspnea
  • Peripheral neuropathy: symmetric tingling/numbness in feet ("stocking-glove" pattern), progressing upward
  • Subacute combined degeneration: posterior columns (loss of proprioception/vibration) + lateral columns (spastic weakness, positive Babinski)
  • Ataxia, balance difficulty, falls
  • Cognitive changes: memory loss, depression, dementia
  • Glossitis: smooth, beefy-red, sore tongue
🔍 Diagnosis
  • CBC: ↑ MCV (>100), ↓ Hgb
  • Hypersegmented neutrophils on peripheral smear (5-lobed neutrophil = classic)
  • Oval macrocytes (large, oval RBCs)
  • ↓ Serum B12 (<200 pg/mL)
  • ↑ Methylmalonic acid (MMA) and homocysteine — confirms B12 deficiency at tissue level
  • Positive anti-intrinsic factor antibodies — diagnostic of pernicious anemia
  • Positive anti-parietal cell antibodies
  • Low gastric acid (achlorhydria)
💊 Treatment
  • Intramuscular (IM) B12 injections — bypass the GI problem entirely
  • Daily for 1 week → weekly for 1 month → monthly indefinitely
  • Hematologic improvement in weeks; neurological improvement slower
  • Neurological damage may be irreversible if treatment delayed >6 months
  • High-dose oral B12 (1000 µg daily) can work in dietary deficiency — NOT if intrinsic factor is absent
  • Monitor: reticulocyte count, Hgb, potassium (hypokalemia as marrow ramps up production)
💡

"Intrinsic factor is the doorman for B12 at the terminal ileum. Pernicious anemia destroys the doorman — the door stays permanently locked. You can eat all the B12 in the world (meat, eggs, dairy), but without the doorman, none of it gets in. That's why you have to go around — inject B12 directly into the muscle, bypassing the locked door entirely."

⚠️ Common Trap — Folate vs B12 Deficiency

Both folate and B12 deficiency cause macrocytic (megaloblastic) anemia and elevated homocysteine. KEY DISTINCTION: Only B12 deficiency causes neurological symptoms (peripheral neuropathy, subacute combined degeneration). Giving folate to a B12-deficient patient may correct the anemia but will NOT stop the neurological damage — it can even mask the anemia while nerve damage continues. Always check B12 AND folate levels before treating macrocytic anemia. Also distinguish from dietary B12 deficiency (vegans) — they need oral B12 supplementation, not IM injections.

🔴 Sickle Cell Anemia

One wrong amino acid — and red cells become crescent-shaped missiles that block every small vessel they enter

⚡ Pathophysiology
  • Autosomal recessive mutation: glutamic acid → valine at position 6 of beta-globin chain
  • HbS polymerizes when deoxygenated → RBCs become rigid, crescent-shaped ("sickle")
  • Sickled cells: can't pass through capillaries → vaso-occlusion
  • Also fragile → destroyed early → hemolysis (RBC lifespan 10–20 days vs normal 120)
  • Triggers for sickling: hypoxia, dehydration, cold, acidosis, infection, stress, high altitude
  • Chronic organ damage from repeated vaso-occlusion
👁️ Clinical Crises & Complications
  • Vaso-occlusive crisis (pain crisis) — most common; excruciating bone/joint/abdominal pain
  • Acute chest syndrome — fever + chest pain + new pulmonary infiltrate + hypoxia = most common cause of death
  • Stroke — especially in children; cerebral vessel occlusion
  • Splenic sequestration — blood pools in spleen → sudden drop in Hgb → shock
  • Aplastic crisis — parvovirus B19 infects RBC precursors → sudden severe anemia
  • Priapism, avascular necrosis (femoral head), chronic leg ulcers
  • Autosplenectomy: repeated infarcts → functional asplenia by age 5 → susceptible to encapsulated bacteria
🔍 Diagnosis
  • Peripheral smear: sickle-shaped cells, target cells, Howell-Jolly bodies (asplenia)
  • Hemoglobin electrophoresis: HbS predominant
  • Newborn screening (standard in US)
  • CBC: normocytic anemia, reticulocytosis (high, as marrow tries to compensate)
  • ↑ LDH, ↑ indirect bilirubin (hemolysis)
  • Trait (HbAS): one normal + one sickle gene; generally asymptomatic (can sickle under extreme stress)
💊 Treatment
  • Hydroxyurea — increases fetal hemoglobin (HbF), which prevents sickling; reduces crisis frequency by 50%
  • Pain crisis: IV fluids, oxygen, opioid analgesia (do NOT withhold due to stigma)
  • Voxelotor (Oxbryta), crizanlizumab — newer targeted agents
  • Chronic transfusions: for stroke prevention in children
  • Bone marrow/stem cell transplant — only cure; mainly for severe pediatric cases
  • Gene therapy: FDA-approved (betibeglogene — 2023) — potentially curative
  • Prophylactic penicillin (until age 5) — functional asplenia
  • Vaccines: pneumococcal, meningococcal, Hib — encapsulated organisms most dangerous
🩺 Nursing Pearls — Sickle Cell Anemia
  • Pain management is a PRIORITY — believe the pain. Sickle cell patients are chronically undertreated due to opioid stigma. Pain crises are real, severe, and require adequate opioid analgesia. Undertreating pain is a nursing failure.
  • Hydration is your most powerful prevention tool — encourage 8–10 glasses of water daily. Even mild dehydration triggers sickling. IV fluids during hospitalization should run briskly unless contraindicated.
  • Acute chest syndrome (fever + chest pain + new infiltrate on CXR + hypoxia) = medical emergency. Notify provider immediately, oxygen, IV fluids, analgesia, and likely exchange transfusion. It can progress to respiratory failure in hours.
  • Children with sickle cell + fever: treat as functional asplenic — blood cultures, antibiotics, and hospital admission standard until cultures cleared.
  • Monitor for parvovirus B19 exposure — causes aplastic crisis. Fever + rapidly dropping Hgb + low reticulocyte count = aplastic crisis (rare but life-threatening).
✅ Normal Red Blood Cell

• Biconcave disc shape — flexible, squeezes through tiny capillaries
• HbA (normal hemoglobin) — carries O₂ efficiently
• Lifespan: ~120 days
• Smooth flow through vessels — no occlusion
• Spleen filters normally

• Rigid crescent/sickle shape — gets stuck in capillaries
• HbS polymerizes when deoxygenated — distorts cell

👶 Hemolytic Disease of the Newborn (Rh Incompatibility)

Mom's antibodies cross the placenta and attack the baby's red cells — prevented by RhoGAM

⚡ Pathophysiology
  • Rh-negative mother + Rh-positive fetus
  • First pregnancy: Fetal Rh+ RBCs enter maternal circulation at delivery → mother sensitized → produces anti-Rh IgG antibodies
  • Subsequent pregnancies: Anti-Rh IgG crosses placenta (only IgG crosses) → attacks fetal Rh+ RBCs → hemolysis
  • Type II hypersensitivity (antibody against cell surface antigen)
  • Also occurs with: ABO incompatibility (milder, can occur in first pregnancy — mom already has anti-A/B antibodies)
👁️ Fetal/Neonatal Consequences
  • Hemolytic anemia in the fetus → extramedullary hematopoiesis → hepatosplenomegaly
  • Severe cases: hydrops fetalis — heart failure, massive edema, effusions
  • Jaundice (hyperbilirubinemia) — from hemolysis releasing unconjugated bilirubin
  • Kernicterus — bilirubin crosses blood-brain barrier → deposits in basal ganglia → permanent brain damage, deafness, cerebral palsy
  • Fetal death if untreated severe cases
🔍 Diagnosis
  • Blood type and Rh status at first prenatal visit
  • Indirect Coombs test (maternal blood): detects anti-Rh antibodies
  • If positive: monitor antibody titers, fetal ultrasound (middle cerebral artery Doppler for fetal anemia)
  • Direct Coombs test (infant cord blood): confirms antibody coating of infant's RBCs
  • Neonatal: total bilirubin, Hgb/Hct, blood type
💊 Prevention & Treatment
  • PREVENTION: RhoGAM (Rh immunoglobulin)
  • Given to Rh-negative mothers: at 28 weeks gestation AND within 72 hours of delivery/miscarriage/abortion/trauma
  • Mechanism: RhoGAM mops up fetal Rh+ cells before mom's immune system can make antibodies
  • Works ONLY before sensitization — once antibodies made, RhoGAM won't help!
  • Fetal treatment (if sensitized): intrauterine transfusion
  • Neonatal treatment: phototherapy (breaks down bilirubin through skin), exchange transfusion for severe cases
🩺 Nursing Pearl — RhoGAM
  • Always check mom's blood type at the first prenatal visit. Rh-negative = RhoGAM at 28 weeks AND after delivery.
  • RhoGAM is also indicated after: miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy, amniocentesis, CVS, any trauma causing fetal-maternal hemorrhage. Don't miss these indications.
  • Teach mothers: "This injection prevents your body from recognizing future Rh-positive babies as foreign. It only works if given before your immune system has already made antibodies." RhoGAM is a prevention tool, not a treatment once sensitization occurs.
  • Neonatal jaundice: phototherapy — ensure eyes are covered, assess bilirubin levels per protocol, monitor temperature, encourage feeding (helps eliminate bilirubin through stool).

💉 Coagulation & Vascular Disorders

Hemostasis is the body's ability to stop bleeding without turning the entire circulatory system into a solid clot. It requires three coordinated steps: vasoconstriction, platelet plug formation (primary hemostasis), and the coagulation cascade (secondary hemostasis). When any part breaks down — either toward bleeding or toward clotting — vascular and coagulation disorders occur.

🟣 Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP)

Your immune system turns bully — IgG antibodies destroy your own platelets, leaving you unable to clot

⚡ Pathophysiology
  • Autoimmune: IgG antibodies bind platelet surface antigens (GPIIb/IIIa)
  • Opsonized platelets destroyed by macrophages in the spleen
  • Often triggered by viral infection (EBV, CMV, VZV, HIV) in children
  • Chronic ITP (>12 months) more common in adults; acute ITP more common in children
  • Platelet count low, but clotting factors and PT/PTT are NORMAL
👁️ Clinical Signs
  • Petechiae — pinpoint, non-blanching red/purple dots (blood outside vessels)
  • Easy bruising (ecchymoses), purpura
  • Mucosal bleeding: gum bleeding, epistaxis (nosebleeds)
  • Heavy menstrual periods (menorrhagia)
  • GI bleeding (serious)
  • Intracranial hemorrhage (rare but most feared complication, especially platelet <10,000)
  • NO hepatosplenomegaly (unlike leukemia — key distinction)
🔍 Diagnosis
  • CBC: isolated thrombocytopenia (low platelets, normal WBC and RBC)
  • PT and PTT: NORMAL — coagulation factors intact
  • Peripheral smear: decreased platelets, may see large platelets (megakaryocytes working overtime)
  • Bone marrow: increased megakaryocytes (marrow trying hard — making platelets; spleen destroying them)
  • Diagnosis of exclusion — rule out other causes (SLE, HIV, heparin-induced, drug-induced)
💊 Treatment
  • Children: often self-limiting — observe if mild; steroids if needed
  • Adults: Corticosteroids (prednisone) — first line; reduce antibody production + spleen clearance
  • IVIG: rapidly raises platelet count (competes with antibody for Fc receptors)
  • Anti-D immunoglobulin (Rh+ patients)
  • Rituximab (anti-CD20): for persistent/chronic ITP
  • TPO receptor agonists (eltrombopag, romiplostim): stimulate platelet production
  • Splenectomy: for refractory ITP (removes main site of platelet destruction)
🩺 Nursing Pearls — ITP
  • Petechiae do NOT blanch when pressed — this distinguishes them from rashes. Press a glass (or your finger) on the spots: if they don't fade, it's blood under the skin, not a vascular rash.
  • Platelet count thresholds for nursing actions:
    • <100,000: begin fall precautions, soft-bristled toothbrush, electric shaver
    • <50,000: avoid IM injections, hold NSAIDs and aspirin, restrict contact activities
    • <20,000: spontaneous bleeding risk — strict activity restriction, pad side rails, monitor for intracranial bleed (worst headache of life, altered MS)
    • <10,000: critical — emergency transfusion likely needed
  • Child with recent viral illness + bruising + petechiae = ITP until proven otherwise. CBC immediately.
  • Avoid all NSAIDs and aspirin in any thrombocytopenic patient — they impair platelet function on top of the already low count.

🔴 Hemophilia A & B

X-linked factor deficiency — the clotting cascade breaks at Factor VIII (A) or IX (B)

⚡ Pathophysiology
  • X-linked recessive — males affected, females are carriers (rarely mildly symptomatic)
  • Hemophilia A: Factor VIII deficiency — most common (1 in 5,000 males)
  • Hemophilia B: Factor IX deficiency — "Christmas disease" (1 in 30,000 males)
  • Intrinsic pathway defective → PTT prolonged
  • Platelet plug forms normally — problem is the fibrin reinforcement doesn't happen
  • Severity: Mild (>5% factor), Moderate (1–5%), Severe (<1%)
👁️ Clinical Signs
  • Prolonged bleeding from cuts, dental procedures, surgery
  • Spontaneous bleeding into joints and muscles (severe cases)
  • Hemarthrosis — joint bleeds (most common and debilitating): warm, swollen, painful joints; knees, ankles, elbows most common
  • Chronic hemarthrosis → hemophilic arthropathy → joint destruction
  • Deep hematomas (muscle bleeding)
  • Intracranial hemorrhage — most feared; any head injury is an emergency
🔍 Lab Findings
  • PTT: PROLONGED (intrinsic pathway affected)
  • PT: NORMAL (extrinsic pathway intact)
  • Platelet count: NORMAL
  • Bleeding time: NORMAL (platelet plug forms fine)
  • ↓ Factor VIII (Hemo A) or ↓ Factor IX (Hemo B) — specific assays confirm
  • Genetic testing: identifies carriers and mutation type
💊 Treatment
  • Factor replacement: recombinant Factor VIII (Hemo A) or Factor IX (Hemo B) — on-demand or prophylactic
  • DDAVP (desmopressin): releases stored Factor VIII from endothelial cells — for mild Hemophilia A only
  • Emicizumab (Hemlibra): bispecific antibody mimicking Factor VIII; subcutaneous injection; game-changer for Hemo A
  • Gene therapy: FDA-approved therapies now available — potential for functional cure
  • Joint bleeds: RICE + factor replacement; physiotherapy to maintain range of motion
  • Antifibrinolytics (tranexamic acid) for mucous membrane bleeds
🩺 Nursing Pearls — Hemophilia
  • Avoid NSAIDs and aspirin — impair platelet function, worsening bleeding risk. Use acetaminophen for pain.
  • Avoid IM injections — use subcutaneous or IV routes instead. If IM unavoidable, apply firm pressure for 5–10 minutes.
  • Joint bleeds (hemarthrosis): RICE (Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation) + factor replacement immediately. Do not aspirate the joint — it can introduce infection. Treat pain but avoid NSAIDs.
  • Any head injury in a hemophilic child = emergency CT scan — intracranial bleeding can occur even with mild head trauma. Administer factor replacement first, then image.
  • Teach families to recognize hemarthrosis early — warmth, tingling, tightness in joint before pain begins. Early treatment prevents chronic arthropathy.
  • Gene therapy: some patients now on gene therapy may have sustained factor correction — verify current treatment status before procedures.

🦵 Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)

Virchow's Triad — stasis + hypercoagulability + endothelial injury = clot formation in deep veins

⚡ Pathophysiology — Virchow's Triad
  • 1. Venous stasis: Immobility, long flights/car rides, post-surgical, heart failure, venous insufficiency
  • 2. Hypercoagulability: Cancer (Trousseau's syndrome), pregnancy, oral contraceptives, genetic thrombophilia (Factor V Leiden, protein C/S deficiency), antiphospholipid syndrome
  • 3. Endothelial injury: Surgery, trauma, IV catheter, infection
  • Clot forms in deep veins (usually lower extremity — popliteal, femoral, iliac)
👁️ Clinical Signs
  • Unilateral leg swelling, warmth, erythema, tenderness (calf/thigh)
  • Dilated superficial veins
  • Pitting edema
  • Homan's sign (calf pain with dorsiflexion) — UNRELIABLE; only 50% sensitive
  • Many DVTs are ASYMPTOMATIC — discovered incidentally or after PE
  • Bilateral leg swelling suggests systemic cause (HF, hypoalbuminemia), NOT DVT
🔍 Diagnosis
  • D-dimer: High sensitivity (99%), low specificity — useful to RULE OUT DVT if negative + low pre-test probability
  • Compression duplex ultrasound: Gold standard — visualizes clot; non-compressible vein = DVT
  • CT venography or MRI for proximal/pelvic DVT
  • Wells score: pre-test probability clinical tool
💊 Treatment & Prevention
  • Anticoagulation: Heparin (IV or LMWH subcutaneous) → bridge to warfarin (INR 2–3) OR start DOAC directly (rivaroxaban, apixaban — no bridging needed)
  • DOACs preferred for most patients (no monitoring, oral, effective)
  • Duration: 3 months (provoked) → 6–12 months or indefinite (unprovoked/cancer)
  • PE prevention: Early ambulation post-surgery, compression stockings, SCDs (sequential compression devices), prophylactic LMWH/heparin
  • IVC filter: only if anticoagulation absolutely contraindicated
  • Thrombolytics (tPA): only for massive iliofemoral DVT with limb threat
🔺 Virchow's Triad — The Three Ingredients for DVT
⚠️ DVT → Pulmonary Embolism (PE)

DVT clot can break off → travel through right heart → lodge in pulmonary arteries → PE. Classic PE triad: sudden dyspnea + pleuritic chest pain + tachycardia. Also: hemoptysis, hypoxia, anxiety, syncope. Massive PE → obstructive shock → cardiac arrest. If PE suspected: immediate CT pulmonary angiography (gold standard). Large PE → systemic thrombolytics (tPA) if hemodynamically unstable. PE is the most preventable cause of hospital death.

🏥 Why Does This Matter in Practice?

Pulmonary embolism is the #1 preventable cause of hospital death. Most fatal PEs originate from DVTs that nobody caught. As a nurse, your DVT prevention actions — getting patients up to walk, ensuring SCDs are actually on and working, administering prophylactic heparin on time — are literally the difference between life and death. Every time you ambulate a post-surgical patient, you are preventing a PE. That's not a chore — that's saving a life.

🩺 Nursing Pearls — DVT Prevention & Management
  • Get patients OUT OF BED post-surgery ASAP. "Dangle and walk" on post-op day 0 or 1. Immobility = DVT risk equation. Every hour of bed rest matters.
  • Check SCDs (sequential compression devices) every shift — ensure they're plugged in, properly fitted, and ON the patient's legs (not on the bed rail).
  • New unilateral leg swelling + recent surgery + prolonged bed rest = DVT until proven otherwise. Don't massage the leg (could dislodge the clot → PE).
  • Warfarin teaching: consistent vitamin K intake (don't suddenly change diet), report any unusual bleeding (gums, dark stools, excessive bruising), carry bleeding-risk card, INR monitoring schedule.
  • DOACs: no routine monitoring needed but ensure patient isn't on interacting drugs (many are cytochrome P450 substrates). Cannot be reversed by protamine (use specific reversal agents: idarucizumab for dabigatran; andexanet alfa for Xa inhibitors).

🌀 Varicose Veins (Varicosity)

Incompetent venous valves + gravity = blood pooling, veins stretching, and skin breaking down at the ankle

⚡ Pathophysiology
  • Venous valves in superficial veins become incompetent → blood refluxes backward (retrograde flow)
  • Blood pools in lower extremities with gravity → veins become dilated, tortuous, elongated
  • Venous hypertension → fluid leaks into interstitium → edema
  • Chronic venous hypertension → hemosiderin deposits (brown staining), lipodermatosclerosis, skin breakdown
  • Risk factors: prolonged standing, obesity, pregnancy, female sex, family history, advanced age
👁️ Clinical Signs
  • Visible, dilated, tortuous superficial veins (saphenous distribution)
  • Aching, heaviness, fatigue in legs — worse with prolonged standing, improved with elevation
  • Ankle/leg edema (pitting)
  • Pruritus over veins
  • Skin changes: hemosiderin staining (brownish discoloration from iron deposition), lipodermatosclerosis (indurated, thickened skin)
  • Complication: Venous stasis ulcers — medial malleolus (ankle), irregular borders, shallow, weeping, painful, difficult to heal
🔍 Diagnosis
  • Clinical examination (visible varicosities)
  • Duplex ultrasound: confirms venous reflux, identifies incompetent perforators, rules out DVT
  • ABI (ankle-brachial index): must check before applying compression — ensures no arterial disease (compression contraindicated with ABI <0.6)
💊 Treatment
  • Conservative: compression stockings (20–30 mmHg) — cornerstone; leg elevation, exercise, weight loss
  • Sclerotherapy: injection of sclerosant → vein fibroses and closes
  • Thermal ablation: endovenous laser (EVLA) or radiofrequency ablation — closure of great saphenous vein
  • Surgical: vein stripping (rarely needed with modern ablation techniques)
  • Venous ulcers: compression bandaging + wound care; may require weeks to months of treatment

The major arterial disorders — know the pathophysiology, risk factors, and nursing priorities

🧱 Atherosclerosis & Hyperlipidemia

Lipid-driven chronic arterial inflammation — the foundation of most cardiovascular disease

⚡ 4-Step Plaque Formation
  • Step 1: Endothelial injury (smoking, HTN, diabetes, dyslipidemia)
  • Step 2: LDL enters vessel wall → oxidized LDL accumulates
  • Step 3: Macrophages engulf oxidized LDL → become foam cells
  • Step 4: Foam cells accumulate + smooth muscle migration → fibrous plaque grows → lumen narrows
  • Plaque rupture → thrombus → MI or stroke
💊 Treatment
  • Statins (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors) — gold standard; reduce LDL by 30–50%; also anti-inflammatory/plaque-stabilizing effects
  • Lifestyle: Mediterranean diet, exercise, smoking cessation, weight loss
  • Ezetimibe: reduces intestinal cholesterol absorption
  • PCSK9 inhibitors (evolocumab, alirocumab): for statin-intolerant or very high-risk patients; injectable; very effective
🩺 Nursing Pearl

Statins → myopathy. Ask every patient on a statin: "Are you having any muscle aches or weakness?" Monitor CK if symptoms. Also: take statins at NIGHT (cholesterol synthesis peaks at night). Monitor LFTs at baseline and if symptoms develop.

💓 Hypertension

"The silent killer" — BP ≥130/80 with no symptoms, destroying organs for years before you know it

⚡ Pathophysiology
  • 90% = primary (essential) HTN — no single cause; genetic + obesity + high-Na diet + sedentary lifestyle + alcohol
  • 10% = secondary HTN (renal artery stenosis, primary aldosteronism, sleep apnea, pheochromocytoma, Cushing's)
  • Chronic high pressure → vessel wall thickening → arteriolosclerosis
  • Consequences: left ventricular hypertrophy → HF; stroke; CKD; retinopathy; aortic dissection
💊 Treatment & Nursing
  • Lifestyle first: DASH diet, ↓ sodium (<2g/day), ↓ alcohol, exercise 150 min/week, quit smoking, weight loss
  • Medications: ACEI/ARB (especially if DM or CKD), CCB (calcium channel blockers), thiazide diuretics, beta-blockers
  • Hypertensive urgency: BP >180/120 WITHOUT end-organ damage → lower BP over 24–48h with oral meds
  • Hypertensive emergency: BP >180/120 WITH end-organ damage (acute MI, stroke, AKI, aortic dissection) → IV meds, ICU, lower BP by 25% in 1 hour
🩺 Nursing Pearl
  • ACEI side effect: dry cough (10–15% of patients) — switch to ARB if intolerable. ACEI can cause angioedema — STOP immediately if tongue/throat swelling occurs.
  • White coat hypertension is real — average 2–3 readings, different times, both arms.
  • DASH diet: rich in potassium, magnesium, calcium; low in saturated fat and sodium.

💥 Aortic Aneurysm

Silent dilation of the aortic wall — until it ruptures and you have minutes to act

⚡ Pathophysiology & Types
  • Localized dilation of vessel wall >1.5x normal diameter due to weakening of the vessel wall
  • AAA (Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm): Below renal arteries; most common; usually asymptomatic until rupture
  • Thoracic aortic aneurysm: associated with Marfan syndrome, bicuspid aortic valve, syphilis
  • Risk factors: smoking (strongest!), male sex, age >65, HTN, atherosclerosis, family history
💊 Management
  • Screening: One-time abdominal ultrasound for men 65–75 who have ever smoked ≥100 cigarettes (USPSTF recommendation)
  • Repair threshold: AAA >5.5 cm, or expanding >0.5–1 cm/year, or symptomatic
  • Repair options: open surgical repair or endovascular aneurysm repair (EVAR)
  • Control risk factors: BP control, smoking cessation, statins
⚠️ Ruptured AAA — Surgical Emergency

Classic triad: Sudden severe "tearing" abdominal/back pain + pulsatile abdominal mass + hypotension = ruptured AAA until proven otherwise. Mortality >80% without emergent surgery. Do NOT delay with extensive workup — straight to OR. Start 2 large-bore IVs, type & crossmatch, alert surgery NOW.
