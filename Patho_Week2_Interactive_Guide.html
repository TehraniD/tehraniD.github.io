🧠
Tay-Sachs Disease Q6
Autosomal recessive · Fatal neurological · Hex-A enzyme deficiency
▼
  • Cause: Deficiency of Hex-A (hexosaminidase A) enzyme → GM2 ganglioside accumulates in neurons
  • Inheritance: Autosomal recessive — BOTH parents must carry the gene
  • Onset: Symptoms begin ~6 months of age (appears normal at birth)
  • Progression: Vision loss → motor weakness → paralysis → inability to swallow/breathe
  • Hallmark sign: Cherry-red spot on macula (eye exam)
  • Prognosis: Fatal by early childhood (ages 2–5), no cure
  • Population: Higher prevalence in Ashkenazi Jewish population
🚨 Critical Exam Point

Q6 tests recognition: progressive neurological decline starting at 6 months, progressive blindness and paralysis, fatal = Tay-Sachs. The child appears normal at birth then deteriorates.

💊 Clinical Pearl

"Cherry-red spot" = lipid accumulation around the macula. The fovea (which lacks ganglion cells) appears red against the pale surrounding retina.

Q6 — Exam Practice
A 10-month-old is brought in with progressive neurological decline starting at 6 months. The infant was normal at birth but now shows vision loss and increasing weakness. What condition matches this?
Tay-Sachs presents with progressive neurological decline starting around 6 months, after a normal birth. Key features: vision loss, progressive paralysis, fatal by early childhood — classic Hex-A enzyme deficiency.
🧬
Down Syndrome (Trisomy 21) Q8
Extra chromosome 21 · Diagnosis by karyotyping · Most common chromosomal disorder
▼
  • Mechanism: Nondisjunction during meiosis → extra copy of chromosome 21 (trisomy 21)
  • Diagnosis: KARYOTYPING — exam answer for Q8
  • Features: Intellectual disability, hypotonia (low muscle tone), epicanthal folds, flat nasal bridge, single palmar crease (simian crease)
  • Associated conditions: Congenital heart defects (AV canal/septal defects), increased Alzheimer's risk, leukemia risk, thyroid issues
  • Risk factor: Advanced maternal age increases risk significantly
⚠️ Exam Tip — Q8

Q8 asks HOW Down Syndrome is confirmed. Answer = KARYOTYPING. Karyotyping analyzes chromosome number and structure, revealing the extra chromosome 21.

🧠 Mnemonic

"21 is DOWN the list (after 18 and 13)" — Trisomy 21 = Down, Trisomy 18 = Edwards, Trisomy 13 = Patau

Q8 — Exam Practice
A newborn presents with hypotonia, epicanthal folds, and a single palmar crease. What test CONFIRMS the diagnosis?
Karyotyping is the definitive test that reveals the extra chromosome 21. It shows the full chromosome picture (46+1 = 47 chromosomes total).
🫁
Cystic Fibrosis Q7
Autosomal recessive · CFTR gene mutation · Thick sticky mucus
▼
  • Gene: CFTR mutation on chromosome 7
  • Inheritance: Autosomal recessive — both parents must carry the gene
  • Pathophysiology: Defective chloride channel → thick, sticky mucus → clogs lungs and pancreas
  • Diagnosis: Sweat chloride test (elevated sweat chloride >60 mEq/L)
  • Lung complications: Recurrent infections (Pseudomonas!), bronchiectasis
  • Pancreatic complications: Enzyme insufficiency → malabsorption
🚨 Exam Trap — Q7: Diet!

CF patients need a HIGH-CALORIE, HIGH-FAT diet (due to malabsorption from pancreatic insufficiency). The WRONG answer is "low-fat diet." They need MORE calories/fat, not less!

⚠️ Q7 Treatments — What's CORRECT
  • ✅ Chest physiotherapy (airway clearance)
  • ✅ Bronchodilators (open airways)
  • ✅ Corticosteroid inhalers (reduce inflammation)
  • ✅ Pancreatic enzyme replacement
  • ❌ Low-fat diet — WRONG! They need high-fat/high-calorie
Q7 — Exam Practice
Which treatment would NOT be appropriate for a child with cystic fibrosis?
CF patients have malabsorption due to pancreatic insufficiency and need a HIGH-calorie, HIGH-fat diet. A low-fat diet would worsen their nutritional status. The other options are all appropriate CF treatments.
🧬
Huntington's Disease Q9
Autosomal dominant · CAG repeat · Chorea → behavioral changes → dementia
▼
  • Inheritance: Autosomal DOMINANT — only ONE parent needs the gene (50% risk to each child)
  • Gene: CAG trinucleotide repeat expansion in HTT gene on chromosome 4
  • Onset: Symptoms typically begin age 30–50 years
  • Progression (IN ORDER):
1
Chorea — involuntary dance-like movements (first symptom)
2
Behavioral/Psychiatric changes — depression, irritability, personality changes
3
Cognitive decline — dementia, loss of function
  • Prognosis: Progressive, fatal (15–20 years after onset), no cure
  • Testing: Genetic testing available — ethical dilemma (presymptomatic testing)
🧠 Mnemonic: "HD = 1 parent, starts with CHOREA"

Huntington's = Half your kids get it (dominant) · Dance before you decline (chorea first)

⚠️ Exam Tip — Q9

The exam asks for "typical symptoms." Answer = Chorea and involuntary movements FIRST, followed by behavioral changes. This order matters!

Q9 — Exam Practice
A 42-year-old patient with Huntington's disease is being assessed. Which symptom presentation is MOST typical for early-stage Huntington's?
Huntington's follows a specific progression: FIRST chorea (involuntary dance-like movements) → THEN behavioral/psychiatric changes → THEN cognitive decline/dementia. Chorea is the hallmark first symptom.

Q33 — Exam Practice
A 28-year-old woman has a butterfly rash across her cheeks, joint pain, and positive ANA antibodies. This presentation is consistent with which type of hypersensitivity?
SLE is a classic Type III hypersensitivity — immune complexes (ANA antibodies + nuclear antigens) deposit in tissues like skin, joints, and kidneys, triggering inflammation. The butterfly rash + ANA = Type III.
Q34 — Exam Practice
A patient receives a blood transfusion and develops a reaction that destroys red blood cells. What type of hypersensitivity is this?
Blood transfusion reactions = Type II Cytotoxic hypersensitivity. IgG/IgM antibodies directly attack the surface antigens on transfused RBCs, activating complement and causing hemolysis (RBC destruction).
🦋
Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) Q36
Type III hypersensitivity · Butterfly rash · Great imitator
▼
  • Type: Chronic autoimmune disease — Type III Immune Complex Hypersensitivity
  • Mechanism: ANA (antinuclear antibodies) and anti-dsDNA antibodies form immune complexes that deposit in tissues
  • Hallmark: Butterfly (malar) rash across cheeks and nose bridge
  • "Great imitator" — can affect almost any organ system
  • Organ involvement: Skin, joints (arthritis), kidneys (lupus nephritis), heart (pericarditis), lungs (pleuritis), CNS
  • Key lab: Positive ANA, anti-dsDNA antibodies, anti-Smith antibodies
  • Demographics: Young women of childbearing age (9:1 female-to-male), African American women at higher risk
  • Triggers: Sunlight, stress, infections, certain drugs
  • Treatment: NSAIDs (mild), corticosteroids, hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil), immunosuppressants
⚠️ Q36 Clinical Picture

Q36 scenario: butterfly rash + joint pain + hypertension = SLE. The hypertension comes from kidney involvement (lupus nephritis). Know the triad: butterfly rash + joints + kidneys.

Q36 — Exam Practice
A 26-year-old woman presents with a butterfly-shaped rash across her cheeks, bilateral joint pain, and newly elevated blood pressure. Labs show positive ANA. What is the most likely diagnosis?
The classic triad: butterfly rash + joint pain + kidney involvement (hypertension from lupus nephritis) + positive ANA = SLE. SLE is the "great imitator" and can affect multiple organ systems simultaneously.

⚠️ Q32 — IgE is the Answer

Q32 asks which immunoglobulin is responsible for immediate hypersensitivity and allergic reactions. Answer = IgE. IgE binds to mast cells and basophils — when allergen cross-links IgE → mast cells degranulate → histamine release → allergy symptoms.

Q32 — Exam Practice
Which immunoglobulin class is primarily responsible for immediate hypersensitivity reactions and allergic responses including anaphylaxis?
IgE is the immunoglobulin responsible for Type I (immediate) hypersensitivity. IgE binds to mast cells. When an allergen binds IgE, mast cells release histamine → allergic symptoms. This is why antihistamines work for allergies.
🔥
Immune Response: Mast Cells & Pyrogens Q19 Q69 Q73
Histamine sources · Fever mechanism · Chemotaxis
▼
  • Mast cells: Primary source of histamine in allergic AND inflammatory responses
  • Histamine release: Causes vasodilation, increased vascular permeability → classic allergy symptoms (redness, swelling, itching)
  • Pyrogens: Chemicals that induce fever by acting on the hypothalamus (thermostat)
  • Endogenous pyrogens: IL-1, IL-6, TNF (produced by our own immune cells)
  • Exogenous pyrogens: Bacterial endotoxins (LPS), viral products
  • Chemotaxis: Movement of leukocytes (WBCs) toward the site of injury/infection following chemical signals
⚠️ Q19, Q69, Q73 — Exam Answers
  • Q19: "Where is histamine released from in inflammation?" → MAST CELLS
  • Q69: "What substance induces fever?" → PYROGENS
  • Q73: "Central cells in hay fever/allergies?" → MAST CELLS
💊 Clinical Pearl: Mast Cells are EVERYWHERE

Mast cells are found in connective tissue throughout the body, especially near blood vessels and in the respiratory/GI/skin mucosa — that's why allergic reactions affect these systems first.

🏥 Immunodeficiency

Primary and secondary immune deficiencies — when the immune system fails to protect

📋 Blueprint: Q35 — 1 Question (but foundational knowledge)
🛡️
Selective IgA Deficiency Q35
Most common primary immunodeficiency · Recurrent mucosal infections
▼
  • Classification: Most common primary immunodeficiency
  • Defect: Low or absent IgA — normal IgG and IgM levels
  • IgA's role: Mucosal immunity (protects respiratory tract, GI tract, genitourinary tract)
  • Presentation: Recurrent respiratory infections (sinusitis, pneumonia), GI infections, many patients are ASYMPTOMATIC
  • Age: Often first noticed in infancy/childhood when maternal IgG wanes (~6 months)
  • Risk: Anaphylaxis to blood products (contain IgA → patient's body reacts to foreign IgA)
  • Treatment: Treat infections as they occur; IgA cannot be replaced (unlike IgG)
⚠️ Q35 — Exam Answer

Q35 scenario: 2-year-old with recurrent respiratory infections = Selective IgA Deficiency. Think: child can't protect mucosal surfaces → keeps getting respiratory infections. IgA is the guard at every body opening.

Q35 — Exam Practice
A 2-year-old child has had multiple episodes of pneumonia and sinusitis. Lab work shows low IgA but normal IgG and IgM levels. What is the most likely diagnosis?
Selective IgA deficiency = most common primary immunodeficiency. Low IgA with normal IgG/IgM. Results in recurrent mucosal/respiratory infections because IgA normally guards mucosal surfaces. Many patients are asymptomatic.

⚠️ Q42 — CLL Key Answer

Q42 asks the CHARACTERISTIC feature of CLL. Answer = "B cells that failed to differentiate from precursor B cells into mature B cells." These immature B cells accumulate and crowd out normal blood cells. Smudge cells on smear = CLL signature.

Q42 — Exam Practice
Which statement BEST characterizes Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia (CLL)?
CLL's defining characteristic: B cells fail to mature, accumulating as non-functional immature B cells. "Smudge cells" appear on blood smear (fragile cells crushed by glass slide). Most common adult leukemia. Often diagnosed incidentally.
🔬
Hodgkin's Lymphoma Q40 Q41
B-cell malignancy · Reed-Sternberg "Owl Eye" cells · Excellent prognosis
▼
  • Cell origin: B LYMPHOCYTES only (NOT T cells — that's NHL!)
  • Diagnostic hallmark: Reed-Sternberg cells — giant cells with "Owl Eyes" appearance (bilobed nucleus with prominent nucleoli)
  • Symptoms: Painless lymphadenopathy (cervical nodes first), B symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss), pruritus (itching)
  • Risk factors: Male > female, prior Epstein-Barr Virus (EBV) infection
  • Spread: Contiguous (orderly, node-to-node — predictable)
  • Age: Bimodal — peak at age 15–35 AND >55
  • Prognosis: Excellent — ~86% 5-year survival (one of the most curable cancers)
  • Treatment: Chemotherapy (ABVD) + radiation
🚨 Q40 & Q41 — Critical Distinctions
  • Q40: Reed-Sternberg cells arise from → B LYMPHOCYTES
  • Q41: TRUE statements about Hodgkin's:
    • ✅ Characterized by Reed-Sternberg cells
    • ✅ More common in males than females
    • ✅ Associated with EBV infection
    • ❌ "Involves both B and T cells" — This is NHL, NOT Hodgkin's!
🧠 Remember: "Hodgkin = B cells with Owl Eyes"

Hodgkin = B cells (not T) · Reed-Sternberg = Owl eyes · EBV link · Males more common

Q40 — Exam Practice
Reed-Sternberg cells, the diagnostic hallmark of Hodgkin's Lymphoma, arise from which cell type?
Reed-Sternberg cells are malignant B lymphocytes that have undergone transformation. They have the characteristic "owl eye" appearance — large bilobed nuclei. This distinguishes Hodgkin's (B-cell only) from NHL (B or T cells).
Q41 — Exam Practice
Which of the following is TRUE about Hodgkin's Lymphoma? (Select the CORRECT statement)
All three correct features: Reed-Sternberg cells (diagnostic hallmark), males > females, and EBV association. The wrong answer trap is "B and T cells" — that describes NHL. Hodgkin's is B-cell only and has an excellent prognosis (~86% 5-year survival).
🩸
Non-Hodgkin's Lymphoma (NHL)
B AND T cells · 5x more common · Less predictable spread
▼
  • Cell origin: BOTH B AND T lymphocytes (key difference from Hodgkin's)
  • Frequency: 5x more common than Hodgkin's
  • Spread: Non-contiguous — unpredictable, can be extranodal (outside lymph nodes)
  • Presentation: More varied — lymphadenopathy, constitutional symptoms
  • Childhood forms: More aggressive than adult forms
  • Types: Many subtypes (Diffuse Large B-Cell, Follicular, Burkitt's, Mantle Cell, etc.)

🔴 Anemia & Blood Loss

BIGGEST exam topic — 7 questions. Master this section!

📋 Blueprint: Q43, Q44, Q60, Q61, Q62, Q68, Q70, Q75 — 7+ Questions
🔑 Anemia Definition (Q61)

Anemia = Decreased hemoglobin concentration → reduced oxygen delivery to tissues. Not just "low RBC count" — it's specifically about hemoglobin.

General symptoms: fatigue, pallor, shortness of breath, dizziness, tachycardia (compensatory — heart speeds up to deliver more O₂), hypotension (from low blood volume)

🔴
Iron-Deficiency Anemia Q62 Q68 Q75
Most common worldwide · PICA · Ferrous sulfate treatment · NOT B12!
▼
  • Prevalence: Most common cause of anemia worldwide (Q75 answer)
  • Cause: Insufficient iron → can't synthesize hemoglobin
  • Classic symptoms: Fatigue, pale skin, PICA (cravings for non-food items)
  • PICA examples: Pagophagia (ice), geophagia (dirt), amylophagia (chalk/starch) — ice cravings are CLASSIC!
  • Other signs: Hair loss (alopecia), koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails), glossitis (smooth tongue), angular cheilitis
  • Labs: ↓ MCV (microcytic, small RBCs), ↓ ferritin (iron stores), ↑ TIBC (body tries to grab more iron), ↓ serum iron
  • Causes: Poor diet, blood loss (heavy periods, GI bleeding), malabsorption, pregnancy
⚠️ Q62 — Clinical Findings

Q62 asks for clinical findings of iron-deficiency anemia. Key answer: hair loss AND ice cravings (PICA/pagophagia). This pairing is characteristic and distinguishes IDA from other anemias.

⚠️ Q68 — Treatment (What's CORRECT vs WRONG)
  • ✅ Oral ferrous sulfate — first-line treatment
  • ✅ IV iron supplementation — when oral not tolerated
  • ✅ Vitamin C — enhances iron absorption (take with OJ!)
  • ❌ High-dose B12 injections — WRONG! B12 treats pernicious anemia, NOT iron deficiency
🧠 Mnemonic: "IRON" symptoms

Ice cravings (pagophagia) · Red blood cell decrease (microcytic) · Onychoschisis (spoon nails) · No hair (alopecia)

Q75 — Exam Practice
What is the most common cause of anemia worldwide?
Iron-deficiency anemia is the most common cause of anemia worldwide, affecting approximately 2 billion people. It is especially prevalent in developing countries and in women of childbearing age.
Q62 — Exam Practice
A nurse is assessing a patient with suspected iron-deficiency anemia. Which combination of findings is MOST characteristic?
Hair loss (alopecia) + ice cravings (pagophagia = a type of PICA) are hallmark features of iron-deficiency anemia. Tingling/memory issues = pernicious anemia (B12 deficiency). Joint pain/rash = SLE.
🩸
Acute Blood Loss & Hematocrit Trap Q43 Q44
Normal hematocrit RIGHT after bleeding = expected! Drops only after fluids
▼
  • Mechanism: Sudden hemorrhage → ↓ blood volume → ↓ O₂ delivery → compensatory tachycardia + hypotension
  • Q43 — Primary concern: HYPOTENSION (due to low blood volume) — not just "low hemoglobin"
  • Hematocrit after acute blood loss: Initially NORMAL (equal loss of plasma and cells)
  • Hematocrit drops ONLY AFTER fluid replacement dilutes the remaining RBCs
  • Treatment: Blood transfusion + IV fluids
🚨 Q44 — BIGGEST EXAM TRAP

Patient loses 30% of blood volume. Hematocrit is measured = 48% (normal).

This is the UNEXPECTED/abnormal finding BECAUSE: when you lose blood acutely, you lose plasma AND cells together — the ratio stays the same. Hematocrit only FALLS after fluid resuscitation dilutes remaining cells.

So: Normal hematocrit immediately after major blood loss is the TRICK — it will look normal when you expect it to be low!

Q44 — Exam Practice
A trauma patient lost approximately 30% of their blood volume. Lab work drawn immediately after the injury shows a hematocrit of 48% (normal). Which statement BEST explains this finding?
In acute hemorrhage, whole blood is lost — plasma AND cells leave together, so the ratio (hematocrit) stays the same initially. After IV fluid replacement, the plasma volume is restored but RBCs are not immediately replaced, diluting the hematocrit. Normal hematocrit right after blood loss is EXPECTED, not reassuring.
🔴
Sickle Cell Anemia
Autosomal recessive · Homozygous = MORE SEVERE · Vaso-occlusive crises
▼
  • Inheritance: Autosomal recessive — BOTH parents must carry the HbS gene
  • Homozygous (SS): SICKLE CELL DISEASE — MORE SEVERE (both abnormal genes)
  • Heterozygous (SA): Sickle cell TRAIT — mild or no symptoms (one normal, one abnormal gene)
  • Mechanism: HbS polymerizes under low O₂ → crescent/sickle-shaped RBCs
  • Crises: Vaso-occlusive (pain crisis) → tissue infarction; Aplastic crisis; Splenic sequestration
  • Complications: Stroke, avascular necrosis, acute chest syndrome, increased infections (especially pneumococcal)
  • Treatment: Hydroxyurea (↓ crisis frequency), pain management, O₂, transfusions
  • Protective effect: Sickle cell trait provides partial protection against malaria
⚠️ Exam Point: Homozygous = MORE Severe

The exam specifically tests that the homozygous form (SS) is MORE SEVERE than heterozygous sickle cell trait (SA). Both parents must carry the gene for a child to have sickle cell disease.

👶
Hemolytic Anemia & Erythroblastosis Fetalis Q60
Rh incompatibility · RhoGAM prevention · Phototherapy treatment
▼
  • Hemolytic anemia: Premature RBC destruction → ↑ bilirubin → jaundice
  • Erythroblastosis fetalis mechanism: Rh-negative mother + Rh-positive baby → at birth/miscarriage, baby's Rh+ blood enters mother's circulation → mother forms anti-Rh IgG antibodies
  • Second pregnancy: Maternal IgG anti-Rh crosses placenta → attacks fetal Rh+ RBCs → hemolytic disease of newborn
  • Prevention: RhoGAM (anti-D immunoglobulin) given to Rh-negative mothers at 28 weeks and within 72 hrs of delivery
  • Treatment: Phototherapy (blue light breaks down bilirubin in skin), exchange transfusion for severe cases
  • Signs in newborn: Jaundice, pallor, hydrops fetalis (severe), high-pitched cry
⚠️ Q60 — "All of the Above"

Q60 about erythroblastosis fetalis = All of the above. All statements are correct: antibody crosses placenta ✓, antigen-antibody response destroys RBCs ✓, phototherapy is treatment ✓.

Q60 — Exam Practice
Regarding erythroblastosis fetalis (hemolytic disease of the newborn), which statement is TRUE?
All three statements are correct for erythroblastosis fetalis: (A) maternal IgG crosses placenta (IgG is the only Ig small enough to cross), (B) Rh antigen-antibody reaction destroys fetal RBCs causing hemolysis, (C) phototherapy converts bilirubin to a water-soluble form that can be excreted.
💉
Pernicious Anemia (B12 Deficiency)
No intrinsic factor · Macrocytic · Treat with B12 injections
▼
  • Cause: Autoimmune destruction of parietal cells → no intrinsic factor → can't absorb B12 in terminal ileum
  • RBC type: Macrocytic (large RBCs — B12 needed for DNA synthesis)
  • Symptoms: Fatigue, neurological symptoms (paresthesia = tingling in hands/feet), memory problems, glossitis
  • Treatment: B12 injections (bypass GI absorption problem) — NOT oral supplements (can't absorb!)
  • Contrast with IDA: IDA = microcytic (small RBCs); Pernicious = macrocytic (large RBCs)

ITP — Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura Q47
Post-viral · Antibodies destroy platelets · Petechiae + bruising in child
▼
  • Mechanism: Autoimmune — antibodies target and destroy platelets
  • Trigger: Often follows viral infection (chickenpox, EBV, CMV) — immune system goes awry after virus
  • Timing: Symptoms appear ~1–3 weeks AFTER viral infection
  • Symptoms: Easy bruising, petechiae (tiny purple pinpoint spots), mucosal bleeding (gums, nose), heavy periods
  • Lab: ↓ platelet count, normal PT/PTT (coagulation cascade intact)
  • Treatment: Corticosteroids (prednisone), IVIG (intravenous immunoglobulin), splenectomy (last resort)
  • Analogy: "Your immune system becomes a bully to your own platelets"
⚠️ Q47 — Classic Clinical Picture

Q47 scenario: Child had chickenpox 1 week ago → now presents with bruising, petechiae, and gum bleeding. Answer = ITP (Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura). Viral infection → immune system attacks platelets → bleeding symptoms.

Q47 — Exam Practice
A 5-year-old child had chickenpox one week ago. The parents bring the child in because of multiple bruises, small purple spots all over the skin, and gum bleeding. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?
Classic ITP presentation: recent viral infection (chickenpox) + petechiae (purple spots) + bruising + mucosal bleeding (gums). Viral infection triggers autoantibodies against platelets → thrombocytopenia → bleeding symptoms. ITP in children is often self-limiting.
🩸
Hemophilia A & B Q63 Q64
Factor VIII or IX deficiency · X-linked · Prolonged bleeding time
▼
  • Hemophilia A: Factor VIII deficiency (most common, 1 in 5,000 males)
  • Hemophilia B: Factor IX deficiency ("Christmas disease")
  • Inheritance: X-linked recessive → males AFFECTED, females CARRIERS (only sons get disease)
  • Mechanism: Absence/low clotting factor → coagulation cascade interrupted → can't form stable fibrin clot
  • Symptoms: Prolonged bleeding from minor injuries, spontaneous joint bleeds (hemarthrosis), deep muscle bleeds
  • Lab: Prolonged PTT (intrinsic pathway affected) — PT is normal
  • Treatment: Recombinant clotting factors (VIII or IX), desmopressin (DDAVP for mild Hemophilia A)
  • Lifestyle: Avoid contact sports, avoid aspirin/NSAIDs
⚠️ Q63 & Q64 — Exam Answers
  • Q63: Prolonged PT = coagulation factor dysfunction (extrinsic pathway — factors VII, X, II, I)
  • Q64: Hemophilia = prolonged bleeding time due to ABSENCE of clotting factor (factor VIII or IX)
💊 PT vs PTT — Which is Prolonged?
  • PT (Prothrombin Time) = tests extrinsic pathway → prolonged in warfarin use, liver disease, Factor VII deficiency
  • PTT (Partial Thromboplastin Time) = tests intrinsic pathway → prolonged in hemophilia A & B, heparin use
  • Hemophilia = PTT prolonged, PT normal

  • Symptoms: Unilateral leg swelling, pain, redness, warmth, palpable cord (hardened vein)
  • Danger: Clot can break off → pulmonary embolism (PE) = life-threatening
  • Diagnosis: D-dimer (sensitive screening), Doppler ultrasound (definitive)
  • Treatment: Heparin (acute) → warfarin or DOAC (long-term anticoagulation)
  • Prevention: Early ambulation, sequential compression devices (SCDs), anticoagulation
⚠️ Q45 & Q46 — Exam Answers
  • Q45: Primary risk factor in Virchow's Triad for DVT = IMMOBILITY (venous stasis)

Q45 — Exam Practice
According to Virchow's Triad, which factor is considered the PRIMARY risk factor for developing DVT in a hospitalized patient?
Of Virchow's Triad components (stasis, hypercoagulability, endothelial injury), IMMOBILITY causing venous stasis is the primary and most modifiable risk factor in hospitalized patients. This is why nurses encourage early ambulation and use SCDs for bedridden patients.
Q46 — Exam Practice
Which patient is MOST at risk for developing a DVT?
This patient hits ALL three components of Virchow's Triad: (1) Venous stasis — post-surgical immobility, (2) Hypercoagulability — obesity + surgery + age, (3) Endothelial injury — hip surgery. Multiple converging risk factors = highest DVT risk.

Varicosity (Varicose Veins) Q65
Abnormally dilated superficial vein · Incompetent valves · Blood pools
▼
  • Definition: Abnormally DILATED superficial veins (Q65 answer)
  • Mechanism: Impaired/incompetent venous valves → blood pools → increased pressure → veins enlarge and become tortuous
  • Locations: Legs most common; also esophageal varices (liver disease), hemorrhoids
  • Risk factors: Prolonged standing (nurses!), pregnancy, obesity, family history
  • Symptoms: Visible bulging veins, aching/heaviness in legs, edema, skin changes (hyperpigmentation)

  • Treatment: Compression stockings, elevation, sclerotherapy, surgery
⚠️ Q65 Definition

Q65 defines varicosity as: "Abnormally dilated superficial vein" — the key words are DILATED and SUPERFICIAL. Varicose veins are in superficial (not deep) veins.

A 2-year-old has had multiple bouts of pneumonia and sinusitis. Lab results show low IgA but normal IgG and IgM. What is the most likely diagnosis?
Q35 — Selective IgA deficiency is the MOST COMMON primary immunodeficiency. IgA deficiency = low/absent IgA with normal IgG and IgM. Results in recurrent MUCOSAL infections (sinopulmonary). Many patients are actually asymptomatic.
Question 3 of 10
Reed-Sternberg cells — the diagnostic hallmark of Hodgkin's Lymphoma — originate from which cell type?
Q40 — Reed-Sternberg cells arise from B LYMPHOCYTES that have undergone malignant transformation. They have the characteristic "owl eye" bilobed nucleus with prominent nucleoli. This is key: Hodgkin's = B cells ONLY. NHL = B or T cells.
Question 4 of 10
A nurse assessing a patient with anemia should be most concerned about which cardiovascular complication?
Q43 — The primary cardiovascular concern in anemia is HYPOTENSION from low blood volume. Additionally, the body compensates with TACHYCARDIA (not bradycardia) to maintain oxygen delivery. Hypotension from anemia can lead to inadequate tissue perfusion and organ damage.
Question 5 of 10
A patient lost 30% of blood volume in a trauma. Blood work drawn immediately at the ER shows hematocrit of 48% (within normal range). Which statement BEST explains this?
Q44 — CRITICAL CONCEPT: In acute hemorrhage, whole blood is lost (plasma + cells together), so the hematocrit RATIO stays the same initially. After IV fluids are given, plasma is diluted but RBCs are not replaced → hematocrit FALLS. Normal hematocrit right after blood loss does NOT mean the patient is fine!
Question 6 of 10
A 5-year-old had chickenpox 1 week ago. Now the parents notice multiple bruises, tiny purple pinpoint spots on the skin, and gum bleeding. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?
Q47 — Classic ITP presentation: RECENT VIRAL INFECTION (chickenpox) + petechiae (purple spots) + bruising + mucosal bleeding (gums) = ITP. The viral infection triggers autoantibodies that destroy platelets. ITP in children often resolves on its own within months.

Which patient is at HIGHEST risk for developing DVT?

A patient is diagnosed with iron-deficiency anemia. The provider orders treatment. Which treatment is INAPPROPRIATE for iron-deficiency anemia?
Q68 — B12 injections treat PERNICIOUS anemia (lack of intrinsic factor → can't absorb B12), NOT iron-deficiency anemia. For IDA: oral ferrous sulfate (first-line) ✓, IV iron (when oral not tolerated) ✓, Vitamin C (enhances iron absorption) ✓. Don't mix up B12 with iron treatments!
Question 9 of 10
A 42-year-old whose parent had Huntington's Disease begins experiencing involuntary, dance-like movements of the limbs. What is the pattern of inheritance for Huntington's Disease?
Q9 — Huntington's Disease is AUTOSOMAL DOMINANT — only ONE copy of the mutated HTT gene (on chromosome 4) is needed to cause disease. Each child of an affected parent has a 50% chance of inheriting it. The involuntary movements (chorea) are typically the FIRST symptom, appearing at age 30–50.

A child with cystic fibrosis requires dietary guidance. Which dietary recommendation is CORRECT for this patient?
Q7 — CF patients need HIGH-CALORIE, HIGH-FAT diet because pancreatic enzyme insufficiency impairs fat/nutrient absorption. They burn more calories fighting infections and breathing. Low-fat diet would cause malnutrition. Also need pancreatic enzyme replacement with meals.
