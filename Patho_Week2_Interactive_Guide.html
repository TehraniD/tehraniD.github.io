Module 2: Hematology & Immunity

Immune System Basics

How your body recognizes and fights threats -- from immediate defenses to allergic overreactions.

Innate vs. Adaptive Immunity

  • Innate (nonspecific): First line of defense. Skin, mucous membranes, phagocytes, natural killer cells, inflammation.
  • Adaptive (specific): Develops after exposure. B cells produce antibodies; T cells attack infected cells directly.
  • Pyrogens are substances that induce fever -- fever is part of the innate immune response to fight infection.
  • Histamine is released by mast cells during inflammation -- causes vasodilation, increased permeability, swelling.

Immunoglobulins (Antibodies)

Antibodies are proteins produced by B cells. Each class has a specific role:

Ig ClassKey RoleRemember

High-Yield: IgE is responsible for immediate hypersensitivity (allergic) responses. It binds to mast cells and triggers histamine release.

Immune Deficiency

  • Immune deficiencies = inability to mount an adequate immune response.
  • Can be primary (genetic/born with it) or secondary (acquired -- e.g., HIV, medications).
  • Selective IgA deficiency: Most common primary immunodeficiency. Presents as recurrent respiratory and GI infections, especially in young children.
Clinical Pearl

Selective IgA deficiency is the most common primary immunodeficiency. Most patients are asymptomatic, but those who do have symptoms present with recurrent respiratory and GI infections -- because IgA protects mucosal surfaces (mouth, nose, gut, lungs).

Hypersensitivity Reactions (Types I-IV)

Hypersensitivity = an exaggerated immune response that causes tissue damage.

TypeMechanismExample
Type I
(Immediate/Allergic)		 IgE antibodies bind mast cells. Re-exposure triggers mast cell degranulation and histamine release. Hay fever, asthma, anaphylaxis, food allergies
Type II
(Cytotoxic)		 Antibodies (IgG/IgM) bind to cell surface antigens, leading to cell destruction via complement activation. Transfusion reactions, hemolytic disease of newborn
Type III
(Immune Complex)		 Antigen-antibody complexes deposit in tissues, triggering inflammation. Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)
Type IV
(Delayed / T-cell)		 T-cell mediated. Takes 24-72 hours. No antibodies involved. Contact dermatitis (poison ivy), TB skin test

Key Concept: Mast cells are central to allergic (Type I) reactions. They are the primary source of histamine. When IgE on a mast cell surface encounters its allergen, the mast cell degranulates -- releasing histamine, which causes itching, bronchoconstriction, and vasodilation.

Type I Hypersensitivity Pathway
Clinical Pearl

Mast cells are the central player in all allergic reactions. When IgE antibodies on mast cell surfaces encounter an allergen, the mast cell releases histamine -- causing vasodilation, bronchoconstriction, and the itching, swelling, and redness you see in allergic reactions. This is why antihistamines help allergies but only epinephrine saves lives in anaphylaxis.

NCLEX Focus

Know the 4 types of hypersensitivity by their mechanism, timing, and one classic example each. The key distinction: Types I-III involve antibodies; Type IV involves T cells only (no antibodies). Type I is the only one mediated by IgE.

Which immunoglobulin class is responsible for immediate allergic reactions?
IgE binds to mast cells and triggers degranulation upon re-exposure to an allergen. It's the only Ig class involved in Type I hypersensitivity.

Dig Deeper: Mast Cells & Histamine Pathway ▼
  • First exposure to allergen: B cells produce IgE, which binds to mast cell surfaces (sensitization).
  • Second exposure: allergen cross-links IgE on mast cells, causing degranulation.
  • Mast cells release histamine, leukotrienes, prostaglandins.
  • Histamine effects: vasodilation, increased capillary permeability, bronchoconstriction, itching.
  • Treatment: antihistamines block histamine receptors; epinephrine reverses anaphylaxis.

Autoimmune & Post-Viral Conditions

When the immune system targets the body's own tissues, or when a viral infection triggers an immune complication.

Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

  • Type III hypersensitivity: Immune complexes (antigen-antibody) deposit in tissues, causing widespread inflammation.
  • Affects multiple organ systems: skin, joints, kidneys, heart, lungs, brain.
  • Classic signs: Butterfly (malar) rash across cheeks and nose, joint pain/swelling, fatigue, photosensitivity.
  • Key labs: Elevated ANA (antinuclear antibody) and anti-dsDNA antibodies are hallmark findings.
  • More common in women of childbearing age; course is relapsing-remitting.
  • Treatment: anti-inflammatory drugs, immunosuppressants, sun avoidance.

Remember: SLE = Type III hypersensitivity. Think: immune complexes depositing everywhere, causing inflammation in multiple organs. Butterfly rash + joint pain + positive ANA/anti-dsDNA = think SLE.

NCLEX Focus

SLE is a Type III hypersensitivity. The hallmark triad: butterfly rash + joint pain + positive ANA/anti-dsDNA. Remember that SLE is a "great imitator" -- it mimics many diseases, which is why the ANA and anti-dsDNA labs are essential for diagnosis.

Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP)

  • What it is: Autoimmune destruction of platelets, leading to low platelet count (thrombocytopenia).
  • Post-viral trigger: In children, ITP often occurs 1-3 weeks after a viral infection (e.g., chickenpox, upper respiratory infection).
  • Signs/symptoms: Easy bruising, petechiae (tiny red/purple dots on skin), bleeding gums, nosebleeds.
  • Why it happens: Antibodies mistakenly target and destroy platelets after viral illness.
  • Most childhood cases are self-limiting (resolve on their own).
Clinical Pearl

ITP is a common sequela of viral infections in children. A child who had chickenpox 1-2 weeks ago and now has bruising, petechiae (tiny purple spots), and gum bleeding = think ITP. The immune system, revved up from fighting the virus, starts mistakenly destroying platelets.

Quick Check

SLE is a Type III immune complex hypersensitivity. Immune complexes deposit in tissues (joints, kidneys, skin) causing inflammation.
A 6-year-old who recently had chickenpox presents with bruising and small purple spots on the skin. What condition should you suspect?
ITP (Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura) commonly follows viral infections in children. The immune response triggered by the virus also destroys platelets.

Blood Cell Cancers

Cancers of white blood cells -- leukemias and lymphomas. Focus on the key distinguishing features of each.

Leukemia Overview

  • Leukemia = cancer of white blood cells in the bone marrow.
  • Acute: Rapid onset, immature cells (blasts), aggressive. Requires urgent treatment.
  • Chronic: Slower onset, more mature (but abnormal) cells, may be found incidentally.

Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL)

  • Most common cancer in children (peak age 2-5 years).
  • Involves immature lymphoblasts that multiply rapidly in bone marrow.
  • Crowds out normal blood cell production: anemia, thrombocytopenia, neutropenia.
  • Signs: fatigue, easy bruising/bleeding, frequent infections, bone pain.
  • Good prognosis with treatment in children (~90% cure rate).

Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia (CLL)

  • Most common leukemia in adults (usually age 60+).
  • Involves B cells that fail to differentiate/mature properly -- they accumulate but do not function.
  • Often found incidentally on routine blood work (elevated WBC with lymphocytosis).
  • May be asymptomatic for years; "watch and wait" approach is common early on.
  • Later symptoms: fatigue, lymphadenopathy, recurrent infections.

Hodgkin Lymphoma

  • Cancer of the lymphatic system, characterized by Reed-Sternberg cells (large, abnormal B lymphocytes).
  • Reed-Sternberg cells arise from B lymphocytes -- this is NOT a T-cell cancer.
  • Risk factors: males more at risk, bimodal age (young adults 15-35, then >55), EBV (Epstein-Barr virus) association.
  • Presents with painless lymph node enlargement (often cervical), "B symptoms" (fever, night sweats, weight loss).
  • Highly curable with treatment, especially when caught early.

Key Fact: Reed-Sternberg cells = B lymphocyte origin. Hodgkin lymphoma is associated with EBV and is more common in males. Do not confuse it with a T-cell cancer.

Clinical Pearl

Reed-Sternberg cells (the diagnostic hallmark of Hodgkin lymphoma) arise from B lymphocytes -- not T cells. Hodgkin lymphoma is more common in males and is associated with prior Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) infection. These are key distinctions from Non-Hodgkin lymphoma.

Quick Check

Reed-Sternberg cells are malignant B lymphocytes. Their presence is pathognomonic for Hodgkin lymphoma.
Which leukemia is characterized by B cells that fail to mature properly and is most common in elderly adults?
CLL involves accumulation of immature B cells that fail to differentiate properly. It's the most common leukemia in adults, especially the elderly.

Dig Deeper: Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma (NHL) -- Brief ▼
  • NHL is more common than Hodgkin lymphoma.
  • Can arise from B cells or T cells.
  • More likely to spread to extranodal sites (outside lymph nodes).
  • Does NOT have Reed-Sternberg cells (that is Hodgkin-specific).
  • Prognosis varies widely depending on type and grade.

Anemias & Blood Loss

The biggest topic on the exam. Anemia means decreased hemoglobin concentration, which reduces oxygen delivery to tissues.

What Is Anemia?

  • Anemia = decreased hemoglobin concentration in the blood, leading to reduced oxygen-carrying capacity.
  • Common symptoms: fatigue, pallor, shortness of breath, dizziness, tachycardia.
  • The heart compensates by increasing heart rate (tachycardia) to maintain oxygen delivery.
  • Concern: client may develop hypotension due to low blood volume (especially in blood loss anemia).

Iron Deficiency Anemia (IDA)

  • Most common cause of anemia worldwide.
  • Iron is needed to make hemoglobin. Without enough iron, RBCs are small and pale (microcytic, hypochromic).
  • Causes: Poor dietary intake, chronic blood loss (heavy menstruation, GI bleeding), pregnancy.

Classic Manifestations

  • Fatigue, weakness, pallor
  • Hair loss
  • Pica/pagophagia -- craving and chewing ice (a hallmark sign!)
  • Brittle nails (koilonychia -- spoon-shaped nails)

Treatment

  • Oral ferrous sulfate (first-line treatment for most patients)
  • IV iron if oral iron is not tolerated or not effective
  • Vitamin C enhances iron absorption -- take iron with orange juice or a vitamin C supplement
  • High-dose B12 is NOT the treatment for IDA (that is for pernicious anemia)

Exam Tip: If a question describes fatigue + ice cravings + hair loss, think IDA. Treatment = ferrous sulfate + vitamin C. NOT B12.

Iron Deficiency Anemia Pathway
Clinical Pearl

The classic IDA triad to remember: fatigue + ice cravings (pagophagia) + hair loss. Treatment is oral ferrous sulfate + vitamin C (enhances absorption). IV iron is used when oral isn't tolerated. High-dose B12 injections are NOT used for IDA -- that's for pernicious anemia.

NCLEX Focus

Anemia is defined as decreased hemoglobin concentration -- not low RBC count or low hematocrit alone. Iron deficiency is the #1 cause of anemia worldwide. For acute blood loss, remember the "hematocrit trap": hematocrit looks NORMAL immediately after hemorrhage because whole blood (cells + plasma) is lost proportionally. It only drops after fluid replacement dilutes the remaining cells.

Sickle Cell Disease

  • Autosomal recessive: Both parents must carry the sickle cell gene for a child to have the disease.
  • Homozygous form (HbSS) is more severe than heterozygous (sickle cell trait, HbAS).
  • Abnormal hemoglobin S causes RBCs to "sickle" (become crescent-shaped) under low-oxygen conditions.
  • Sickled cells are rigid and sticky -- they block small blood vessels (vaso-occlusive crisis).
  • Vaso-occlusive crisis: severe pain, tissue ischemia, organ damage.
  • Treatment: hydration, pain management, hydroxyurea (increases fetal hemoglobin, reduces sickling).

Pernicious Anemia

  • Caused by inability to absorb vitamin B12 due to lack of intrinsic factor (produced by stomach parietal cells).
  • Without B12, RBCs are large and immature (macrocytic/megaloblastic anemia).
  • Neurological symptoms: numbness/tingling in extremities, balance problems, cognitive changes.
  • Treatment: lifelong B12 injections (oral B12 won't be absorbed without intrinsic factor).

Erythroblastosis Fetalis (Hemolytic Disease of the Newborn)

  • Occurs when an Rh-negative mother carries an Rh-positive fetus.
  • Mother develops Rh antibodies (usually after first pregnancy) that cross the placenta in subsequent pregnancies.
  • Antigen-antibody response destroys fetal RBCs -- leads to hemolytic anemia in the fetus/newborn.
  • Destruction of RBCs releases bilirubin, causing jaundice.
  • Phototherapy (blue light) breaks down bilirubin in the newborn's skin.
  • Prevention: RhoGAM (Rh immunoglobulin) given to Rh-negative mothers during pregnancy and after delivery.

Coagulation & Clotting

Hemostasis Steps

  • Step 1: Vasoconstriction -- blood vessel narrows to reduce blood flow.
  • Step 2: Platelet plug -- platelets adhere to damaged vessel wall and aggregate.
  • Step 3: Coagulation cascade -- clotting factors activate in sequence.
  • Step 4: Fibrin clot -- fibrin strands reinforce the platelet plug into a stable clot.

Key Lab: PT (Prothrombin Time)

  • PT measures the extrinsic pathway of coagulation.
  • Prolonged PT = dysfunction in coagulation factors (e.g., liver disease, warfarin therapy, factor deficiency).

Hemophilia

  • Genetic disorder causing prolonged bleeding due to absence of a clotting factor.
  • Hemophilia A: missing Factor VIII (most common). Hemophilia B: missing Factor IX.
  • X-linked recessive -- primarily affects males.
  • Signs: easy bruising, prolonged bleeding after injury, hemarthrosis (bleeding into joints).
Clinical Pearl

Hemophilia = prolonged bleeding due to absence of a clotting factor (VIII or IX). Key lab finding: prolonged PTT with normal PT and normal platelet count. The platelet plug forms normally -- the problem is the fibrin reinforcement step fails.

Hemostasis Steps

Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)

  • DVT = blood clot (thrombus) in a deep vein, usually in the legs.
  • Danger: clot can break off and travel to the lungs (pulmonary embolism).

Virchow's Triad (3 Major Risk Factors)

  • Venous stasis (immobility/reduced mobility) -- primary risk factor
  • Endothelial injury (vessel wall damage -- surgery, trauma)
  • Hypercoagulability (blood clots too easily -- cancer, pregnancy, clotting disorders)

Highest Risk Populations

  • Elderly, obese, post-surgical patients (especially hip/knee replacement)
  • Prolonged bed rest or immobility
  • Long flights/travel without movement

Mnemonic -- Virchow's Triad = "SHE":

  • Stasis (immobility)
  • Hypercoagulability (clotting tendency)
  • Endothelial injury (vessel damage)
Quick Check
A patient with acute blood loss from a motor vehicle accident has a hematocrit of 45%. Is this expected?
In acute hemorrhage, whole blood is lost (cells AND plasma), so the ratio stays normal initially. Hematocrit drops only AFTER fluid resuscitation dilutes the remaining cells.
A patient has prolonged PT. This suggests dysfunction in which aspect of hemostasis?
PT (prothrombin time) measures the extrinsic + common coagulation pathway. A prolonged PT indicates dysfunction in coagulation factors (especially VII, X, V, II, or fibrinogen).
Which of the following is the primary risk factor for DVT based on Virchow's triad?
Virchow's triad consists of venous stasis (immobility), endothelial injury, and hypercoagulability. Immobility/reduced mobility is the most common and modifiable risk factor for DVT.

Venous Insufficiency & Varicosity

  • Varicosity = abnormally dilated, tortuous superficial vein.
  • Caused by incompetent venous valves -- blood pools in the veins instead of returning to the heart.
  • Risk factors: prolonged standing, obesity, pregnancy, family history.
  • Signs: visible bulging veins (usually legs), aching, heaviness, edema.
  • Chronic venous insufficiency can lead to skin changes, stasis dermatitis, and ulcers.

Think It Through

A client arrives at the ER after a car accident with significant blood loss. Initial hematocrit is 42% (normal). After 2 liters of IV saline, repeat hematocrit drops to 28%. Why was the initial reading normal?

Tap to reveal answer
After acute blood loss, both RBCs and plasma are lost together, so the ratio (hematocrit) stays the same initially. Once IV fluids are given, the plasma volume increases but RBCs are not replaced -- this dilutes the blood and the hematocrit drops, revealing the true extent of the loss.

Question 2 of 15
Two parents both carry the sickle cell trait (HbAS). Which statement about their children is accurate?
Correct! Sickle cell is autosomal recessive. Both parents must carry the gene. With two carriers (HbAS x HbAS), each child has a 25% chance of HbSS (disease), 50% chance of HbAS (trait), and 25% chance of normal (HbAA). The homozygous form is more severe.

A trauma patient has significant hemorrhage. The initial hematocrit drawn in the ER is within normal limits. What explains this finding?
Correct! In acute blood loss, whole blood (cells + plasma) is lost together, so the hematocrit appears normal initially. It only drops after fluid resuscitation dilutes the remaining blood.
Question 4 of 15
A newborn with Rh-positive blood born to an Rh-negative mother develops jaundice shortly after birth. Which statement about this condition is true?
Correct! In erythroblastosis fetalis, maternal Rh antibodies cross the placenta and mount an antigen-antibody response that destroys fetal RBCs. The resulting bilirubin buildup causes jaundice, and phototherapy breaks down bilirubin through the skin.
Question 5 of 15
A pathology report shows Reed-Sternberg cells in a lymph node biopsy from a 20-year-old male with a history of mononucleosis. Which statement about this cancer is correct?
Correct! Reed-Sternberg cells are the hallmark of Hodgkin lymphoma and originate from B lymphocytes. Males are more at risk, and EBV (which causes mononucleosis) is a known risk factor.
Question 6 of 15
A 68-year-old has a routine CBC showing markedly elevated WBCs with lymphocytosis. He has no symptoms. Which condition is most likely, and what is the underlying cell problem?
Correct! CLL is the most common leukemia in older adults. It involves B cells that fail to properly differentiate/mature. It is often discovered incidentally on routine blood work and may be asymptomatic initially.
Question 7 of 15

Question 8 of 15

Correct! Transfusion reactions are a classic example of Type II hypersensitivity, where antibodies bind to cell surface antigens (on donor RBCs) and destroy them through complement activation and cell lysis.
Question 9 of 15

Question 12 of 15
A patient with a bacterial infection develops a temperature of 102.4F. What substance is primarily responsible for inducing this fever response?
Correct! Pyrogens are substances (released by bacteria or by immune cells) that act on the hypothalamus to raise body temperature. Fever is a protective immune response that helps fight infection.
Question 13 of 15
A patient with seasonal allergies has elevated levels of a specific immunoglobulin that binds to mast cells. Which class of antibody is responsible for this immediate allergic response?
Correct! IgE is the immunoglobulin responsible for immediate hypersensitivity (allergic) reactions. It binds to mast cell surfaces, and upon allergen re-exposure, triggers mast cell degranulation and histamine release.
