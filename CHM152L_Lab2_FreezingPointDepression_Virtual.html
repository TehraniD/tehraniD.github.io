🌡️ The Big Picture
Every winter, cities spend millions dumping salt on roads. Why? Because salt lowers the freezing point of water, preventing ice from forming. This is called freezing point depression — one of the four colligative properties of solutions. Today you'll calculate exactly how much protection different salts provide and discover why salt stops working in extreme cold.

🔢 Pre-Lab Calculations
Complete all four steps for each calculation: (1) moles, (2) molality, (3) ΔT, (4) new freezing point. All answers round to 2–3 significant figures.
Example 1 — Light NaCl (2.5 g NaCl in 50 g water) — Given / Worked
Given: Molar mass NaCl = 58.44 g/mol, kf = 1.86 °C/m, i = 2
Step 1 — Moles of NaCl:   2.5 g ÷ 58.44 g/mol = 0.0428 mol
Step 2 — Molality:   0.0428 mol ÷ 0.050 kg = 0.856 m
Step 3 — ΔT:   i × kf × m = 2 × 1.86 × 0.856 = 3.18 °C
Step 4 — Freezing Point:   0°C − 3.18°C = −3.18°C

💬 Analysis Questions
1. Why does CaCl₂ produce a greater freezing point depression than NaCl at the same molality?

Explain using the ΔT formula and the concept of van't Hoff factor.

0 characters
💡 Expected answer: CaCl₂ has i = 3 (Ca²⁺ + 2Cl⁻) vs NaCl i = 2 (Na⁺ + Cl⁻). Since ΔT = i × kf × m, a higher i produces a larger ΔT at the same molality. At m = 0.9 mol/kg: NaCl ΔT = 2 × 1.86 × 0.9 = 3.35°C vs CaCl₂ ΔT = 3 × 1.86 × 0.9 = 5.03°C.
2. Why do cities switch from NaCl to CaCl₂ when temperatures drop below −10°C?

Consider the effective temperature range of each salt and the practical implications.

0 characters
💡 Expected answer: NaCl is only effective to about −9°C (15°F). Below that, it cannot lower the freezing point enough to prevent ice formation. CaCl₂ remains effective to −29°C (−20°F) because its higher i factor (3 vs 2) produces a much greater ΔT per unit of molality, keeping the solution liquid at far colder temperatures.
