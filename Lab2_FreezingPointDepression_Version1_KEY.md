## Pre-Lab Calculation Answers

### Solution A (0.5 m NaCl):
- Moles of NaCl = 2.92 g ÷ 58.44 g/mol = **0.050 mol**
- Molality = 0.050 mol ÷ 0.100 kg = **0.50 m**
- ΔT = i × k_f × m = 2 × 1.86 × 0.5 = **1.86°C**
- Expected freezing point = 0°C - 1.86°C = **-1.86°C**

### Solution B (1.0 m NaCl):
- Moles of NaCl = 5.85 g ÷ 58.44 g/mol = **0.100 mol**
- Molality = 0.100 mol ÷ 0.100 kg = **1.0 m**
- ΔT = 2 × 1.86 × 1.0 = **3.72°C**
- Expected freezing point = 0°C - 3.72°C = **-3.72°C**

### Solution C (2.0 m NaCl):
- Moles of NaCl = 11.69 g ÷ 58.44 g/mol = **0.200 mol**
- Molality = 0.200 mol ÷ 0.100 kg = **2.0 m**
- ΔT = 2 × 1.86 × 2.0 = **7.44°C**
- Expected freezing point = 0°C - 7.44°C = **-7.44°C**

## Healthcare Application Questions — ANSWER KEY

### Question 1: IV Fluid Design

**Normal saline (0.9% NaCl) freezing point calculation:**

Given:
- 0.9 g NaCl per 100 g water
- Molar mass NaCl = 58.44 g/mol
- k_f (water) = 1.86°C/m
- i = 2

Step 1: Calculate moles
$$\text{moles NaCl} = \frac{0.9 \text{ g}}{58.44 \text{ g/mol}} = 0.0154 \text{ mol}$$

Step 2: Calculate molality
$$m = \frac{0.0154 \text{ mol}}{0.100 \text{ kg}} = 0.154 \text{ m}$$

Step 3: Calculate ΔT
$$\Delta T = 2 \times 1.86 \times 0.154 = 0.57°C$$

Step 4: Calculate freezing point
$$FP = 0°C - 0.57°C = -0.57°C$$

**Answer: The freezing point of normal saline is approximately -0.57°C**

**Clinical significance:** This freezing point depression is nearly identical to blood plasma (-0.56°C), which confirms that normal saline is isotonic with blood. This is why it's safe for IV use — it won't cause cells to swell or shrink due to osmotic pressure differences. The matching osmotic properties are critical for patient safety.

### Question 2: Blood Osmolarity

**Answer:** A blood freezing point of -0.52°C (compared to normal -0.56°C) indicates that the blood is MORE DILUTE than normal — meaning fewer dissolved particles per unit volume.

**Clinical interpretation:** This patient may be:
- **Overhydrated** (too much water intake, possibly water intoxication)
- Experiencing **hyponatremia** (low sodium)
- Having **kidney issues** affecting fluid regulation
- Showing signs of **SIADH** (Syndrome of Inappropriate Antidiuretic Hormone)

**The reasoning:** Since ΔT = i × k_f × m, a smaller freezing point depression (less negative) means lower molality (fewer dissolved particles). Blood normally has a specific concentration of electrolytes, proteins, and other solutes. A deviation indicates concentration abnormality.

### Question 3: Cryopreservation Challenge

**Answer:** Freezing point depression alone is insufficient for several reasons:

1. **Ice crystal formation:** Even with a lowered freezing point, ice crystals can still form slowly. These crystals pierce cell membranes, causing irreversible damage. Cryoprotectants must also be **vitrifying agents** — substances that help the solution transition directly to a glass-like solid state without crystallization.

2. **Rate of cooling:** Rapid cooling (flash freezing) combined with cryoprotectants prevents crystal nucleation. This requires specialized equipment.

3. **Osmotic stress:** During freezing and thawing, water moves in and out of cells. The cryoprotectant must be able to permeate the cell membrane to balance internal and external concentrations.

4. **Toxicity:** Many effective freezing point depressants (like DMSO) are toxic at high concentrations. There's a balance between protection and toxicity.

5. **Dehydration effects:** As ice forms in the extracellular space, it pulls water from cells, potentially causing lethal dehydration even before complete freezing.

**Clinical cryoprotectants include:** DMSO, glycerol, ethylene glycol, propylene glycol (in controlled, non-toxic concentrations)

---

### Question 4: The Antifreeze Poisoning Case

**Answer:** Ethylene glycol (antifreeze) causes an unusually low blood freezing point because:

1. **It's a small molecule:** Ethylene glycol (C₂H₆O₂, MW = 62.07 g/mol) is relatively small and highly soluble in water/blood.

2. **High particle count:** When dissolved, it significantly increases the total number of particles in solution, causing substantial freezing point depression.

3. **Metabolites compound the effect:** The body metabolizes ethylene glycol into glycolic acid, glyoxylic acid, and oxalic acid. These metabolites ALSO contribute to the particle count, further lowering the freezing point.

4. **Rapid absorption:** Ethylene glycol is quickly absorbed from the GI tract, leading to high blood concentrations rapidly.

**Clinical note:** This freezing point depression is actually used diagnostically! An osmolar gap (measured osmolality vs. calculated osmolality) helps identify toxic alcohol ingestion. The treatment includes fomepizole (inhibits alcohol dehydrogenase) and potentially dialysis.

### Question 5: Medication Storage

**Answer:** Freezing can compromise medication effectiveness through several mechanisms:

1. **Emulsion breaking:** Many liquid medications are emulsions or suspensions. Freezing causes ice crystals to form that physically separate the emulsion phases. Upon thawing, the medication may not remix properly, leading to uneven dosing.

2. **Protein denaturation:** Biological medications (insulin, vaccines, antibodies) contain proteins that can denature when ice crystals form. The crystal edges physically tear protein structures.

3. **Container damage:** Expansion during freezing can crack containers, compromising sterility.

4. **pH changes:** Ice formation concentrates solutes in the remaining liquid, potentially causing localized pH extremes that degrade active ingredients.

5. **Precipitation:** Some dissolved medications may precipitate out of solution during freezing and not redissolve completely.

**Example:** Insulin is a classic example — frozen insulin loses its potency because the protein structure is damaged. This is why diabetic patients are instructed never to let their insulin freeze.

## Critical Thinking Section — ANSWER KEY

### Question 1: Organ Preservation Solution Design

**Model answer should include:**

1. **Appropriate concentration:** High enough to significantly lower freezing point and prevent ice formation, but not so high as to cause osmotic damage or toxicity.

2. **Non-toxic cryoprotectants:** Substances like trehalose (a sugar found in organisms that survive freezing), DMSO at controlled concentrations, or glycerol.

3. **Impermeant and permeant agents:** A mix of substances that stay outside cells (controlling extracellular ice) and penetrate cells (protecting intracellular contents).

4. **Buffering capacity:** To maintain stable pH during temperature changes.

5. **Electrolyte balance:** Similar to intracellular or extracellular fluid, depending on design philosophy.

6. **Oncotic agents:** Large molecules to prevent cellular edema.

**Real-world example:** University of Wisconsin (UW) solution is used for organ preservation and contains lactobionic acid, raffinose, potassium, and other components.

---

### Question 2: Why 0.9% Saline (Not Pure Water) for IVs

**Model answer should include:**

1. **Isotonicity:** 0.9% saline has the same osmotic pressure as blood (isotonic). Pure water is hypotonic.

2. **Cell protection:** If pure water were infused:
   - Water would flow INTO red blood cells by osmosis
   - Cells would swell and burst (**hemolysis**)
   - This releases hemoglobin into plasma, causing kidney damage

3. **Electrolyte balance:** Sodium and chloride ions are essential for:
   - Nerve function
   - Muscle contraction
   - Fluid balance
   - pH regulation

4. **Historical note:** The 0.9% concentration was determined empirically in the late 1800s to match the "saltiness" of blood. While not perfectly physiological (blood has many other electrolytes), it's close enough for most purposes.

**Freezing point connection:** The matching freezing points (-0.56°C to -0.57°C) between blood and saline is a direct result of matching solute concentrations (isotonicity).

---

### Question 3: Environmental Impact of Road Salt

**Model answer should include:**

1. **Aquatic organism freezing tolerance:** Many aquatic organisms have evolved to tolerate specific freezing conditions. Fish produce antifreeze proteins, amphibian eggs have specific ice-nucleation points for survival.

2. **Disruption of natural cycles:**
   - Spring spawning triggered by temperature changes would be affected
   - Eggs that normally survive partial freezing might not develop properly in saltier water
   - Freshwater fish adapted to low-salt environments experience osmotic stress

3. **Food chain effects:**
   - Invertebrates (insects, crustaceans) sensitive to salinity changes die off
   - This reduces food availability for larger organisms
   - Entire ecosystem balance can shift

4. **Long-term accumulation:** Unlike temperature changes, salt doesn't disappear — it accumulates in soils and sediments, creating progressively saltier environments.

5. **Drinking water impact:** Elevated chloride levels in groundwater affect human water supplies.

**Connection to lesson:** This demonstrates that colligative properties have real-world consequences beyond the chemistry lab.

## Common Misconceptions to Address

1. **"Salt makes ice colder"** — No! Salt lowers the freezing point, allowing the ice/salt mixture to absorb more heat from surroundings before reaching its new, lower freezing point.

2. **"All salts have the same effect"** — No! The van't Hoff factor varies. CaCl₂ produces 3 particles, so it's more effective per mole than NaCl (2 particles).

3. **"Molarity and molality are the same thing"** — No! Molality uses mass of solvent (doesn't change with temperature); molarity uses volume (changes with temperature).

4. **"The van't Hoff factor is always exact"** — No! At higher concentrations, ion pairing reduces the effective i value. A 1 m NaCl solution doesn't actually have i = 2.00; it's closer to 1.9.

2. Have students practice reading the thermometer during calibration
3. Emphasize GENTLE stirring — vigorous stirring introduces heat
4. Demonstrate what a cooling curve plateau looks like (show example graph)
