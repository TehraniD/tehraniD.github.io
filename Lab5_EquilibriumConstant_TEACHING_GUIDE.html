Key Teaching Points

1. Why Excess Fe³⁺ in Standards?

Students often ask why the standard uses 0.2 M Fe(NO₃)₃ while the equilibrium runs use 2.00 × 10⁻³ M. Explain: The 100× excess of Fe³⁺ pushes the equilibrium so far right that essentially ALL the SCN⁻ converts to FeSCN²⁺. This means [FeSCN²⁺] ≈ [SCN⁻]initial, giving us known concentrations for the standard curve.

2. The Dilution Factor

The most common calculation error: students forget to account for dilution. When 2.00 mL of 2.00 × 10⁻³ M KSCN is diluted to 50 mL, the final [SCN⁻] = 2.00 × 10⁻³ × (2.00/50.0) = 8.00 × 10⁻⁵ M. Walk through this calculation explicitly.

3. Why KC Values Vary Between Runs

KC should theoretically be identical for all 5 runs. Variations come from: pipetting errors, temperature fluctuations, and spectrophotometer precision. A spread of ±20-30% is normal for undergraduate labs. Use this as a teaching moment about experimental uncertainty.

4. Connection to Le Chatelier's Principle

The post-lab questions connect equilibrium constants to temperature effects. Use the analogy:
  • Endothermic: Reactants + Heat → Products. Heat is like a "reactant" — adding it makes more products, increasing KC.
  • Exothermic: Reactants → Products + Heat. Heat is like a "product" — adding it pushes reaction backward, decreasing KC.

Troubleshooting Guide

ProblemLikely CauseSolution
Standard curve is not linear C5 concentration is listed as 8.00 × 10⁻⁴ in original PDF — this appears to be a typo; it should be 2.00 × 10⁻⁴ Use corrected value: [FeSCN²⁺] = 2.00 × 10⁻³ × (5.00/50.0) = 2.00 × 10⁻⁴ M
Absorbance readings > 1.5 Solutions too concentrated; Beer's Law breaks down at high absorbance Dilute solutions; ensure Spec 20 is properly calibrated
All KC values are very small (< 10) Student used the wrong Fe(NO₃)₃ solution (0.2 M instead of 2.00 × 10⁻³ M) for equilibrium runs Have student redo with correct dilute solution
KC varies wildly between runs Pipetting errors or not diluting to proper volume Check their technique; ensure volumetric flasks filled to the line
Negative [Fe³⁺]eq or [SCN⁻]eq [FeSCN²⁺]eq read from graph is larger than initial concentration Student read the standard curve incorrectly; help recalculate

Extended Discussion Questions (Optional)

  1. If you added more Fe(NO₃)₃ to one of your equilibrium test tubes, what would you observe visually? How would this affect KC?
  2. Why do we use HNO₃ in all our solutions instead of water?
  3. If this lab were performed at 50°C instead of room temperature, would you expect KC to change? Why or why not?
  4. Explain why Beer's Law is essential to this experiment. What would happen if the relationship between absorbance and concentration were not linear?
  5. A student's KC values were: 45, 180, 95, 110, 102. Which value is likely an outlier? What experimental error could cause this?
