# Lab 3: Heat of Fusion of Water — Instructor Walkthrough

## Complete Step-by-Step Guide with Realistic Data

**Scenario:** You're completing this lab in a community college chemistry lab. The calorimeters are basic aluminum cups, the ice comes from a standard lab freezer, and the thermometers have seen a few semesters of student handling. This walkthrough shows realistic results you might actually see.

---

## PRE-LAB SETUP (Instructor Prep — Before Students Arrive)

### Checklist
- [ ] Ice machine running + ice trays ready (crushed ice preferred)
- [ ] Warm water available (~35-40°C) — a hot plate with a large beaker works
- [ ] Paper towels at each station (for drying ice — CRITICAL)
- [ ] Each station: 1 calorimeter, 1 thermometer, 1 balance, 1 stirring rod
- [ ] Pre-Lab Quiz copies printed (or projected)

### Key Prep Note
**Ice should be at 0°C, not -18°C from a deep freezer.** The best way: put crushed ice in a large bowl of water 15 minutes before lab. Let it sit as an ice-water slurry. This guarantees the ice is at exactly 0°C. Drain before distributing.

## THE EXPERIMENT — Trial 1

### Step 1: Measure the Empty Calorimeter (2 min)

**What you're doing:** Getting the baseline mass so you can calculate water and ice masses by subtraction.

**Procedure:**
1. Place the clean, dry calorimeter (aluminum cup + stirrer) on the balance
2. Record the mass

**Realistic Result:**
- Mass of empty calorimeter: **45.2 g**

*Instructor Note: These vary from 30-65 g depending on your calorimeter model. The important thing is that students include the stirrer in this mass AND every subsequent weighing.*

---

### Step 2: Add Warm Water (3 min)

**What you're doing:** Adding the "heat source" — the warm water whose temperature drop will tell us how much energy went into melting the ice.

**Procedure:**
1. Fill the calorimeter about halfway with warm water (~35°C, about 10°C above room temp)
2. Place calorimeter + water on the balance
3. Record the mass

**Realistic Result:**
- Mass of calorimeter + warm water: **155.8 g**
- Mass of warm water: 155.8 − 45.2 = **110.6 g**

*Instructor Note: Aim for 80-130 g of water. Too little water (<50 g) means the temperature change will be too large and heat loss to air becomes significant. Too much (>200 g) means you need a LOT of ice to get a 20°C drop.*

---

### Step 3: Record Initial Temperature (1 min)

**What you're doing:** Establishing the starting temperature. This is your T_i.

**Procedure:**
1. Place thermometer in the warm water
2. Stir gently for 30 seconds
3. Record the stable temperature

**Realistic Result:**
- Initial temperature: **35.4°C**

*Instructor Note: If the water is above 45°C, it's too hot — excessive heat loss to air. If below 30°C, the temperature drop may be too small to measure accurately. Sweet spot: 33-38°C.*

---

### Step 4: Prepare the Ice (1 min)

**What you're doing:** Getting ice ready to add QUICKLY.

**Procedure:**
1. Scoop crushed ice from the ice-water slurry
2. Place on paper towels
3. **GENTLY pat dry** to remove surface water
4. Have it sitting next to the calorimeter, ready to add

**⚠️ THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT STEP FOR ACCURACY.**

*Instructor Note: Walk around and check that students are actually drying their ice. Wet ice is the #1 source of error. The surface water gets counted as "ice" but doesn't need fusion energy → ΔHf comes out too low.*

---

### Step 5: Add Ice (3-5 min)

**What you're doing:** Adding the ice and watching the temperature drop.

**Procedure:**
1. Add crushed ice a little at a time to the warm water
2. Stir CONTINUOUSLY (gentle circular motion)
3. Watch the thermometer — keep adding ice until temperature drops about 20°C from T_i
4. Stop adding ice
5. Continue stirring until ALL ice is melted

**What students will observe:**
- Temperature drops rapidly at first (ice melting = absorbing energy)
- Small ice pieces melt quickly; larger chunks take longer
- The rate of temperature drop slows as temperatures equalize
- Temperature stabilizes once all ice is melted

**Common mistakes to watch for:**
- ❌ Dumping all ice in at once (hard to control the 20°C drop)
- ❌ Stopping too early while ice chunks remain
- ❌ Not stirring (temperature gradient in the water)

---

### Step 6: Record Final Temperature (1 min)

**What you're doing:** Recording the equilibrium temperature after all ice has melted.

**Procedure:**
1. Make sure ALL ice is melted (look carefully — tiny chips!)
2. Stir gently for 30 more seconds
3. Record the lowest stable temperature

**Realistic Result:**
- Lowest temperature: **14.8°C**

*Instructor Note: "Lowest stable" means the temperature has stopped dropping and holds steady for 30+ seconds. It may creep up slightly as room heat enters — record the MINIMUM before the creep starts.*

---

### Step 7: Measure Final Mass (1 min)

**What you're doing:** Getting the total mass so you can calculate how much ice was added.

**Procedure:**
1. Place the calorimeter (with all the water = original warm water + melted ice) on the balance
2. Record the total mass

**Realistic Result:**
- Mass of calorimeter + warm water + ice: **183.5 g**
- Mass of ice: 183.5 − 155.8 = **27.7 g**

*Instructor Note: Expect 20-40 g of ice for a 20°C drop with ~110 g of warm water. If students report >60 g of ice, they probably didn't drain excess water. If <15 g, the temperature drop is probably small.*

---

### Trial 1 — CALCULATIONS

**Step 1:** Mass of warm water
```
mw = 155.8 g − 45.2 g = 110.6 g
```

**Step 2:** Mass of ice
```
mice = 183.5 g − 155.8 g = 27.7 g
```

**Step 3:** Temperature change
```
ΔT = Tf − Ti = 14.8°C − 35.4°C = −20.6°C
```
*Note the NEGATIVE sign — temperature went DOWN. This is correct!*

**Step 4:** Heat lost by warm water
```
Qwarm = mw × cw × ΔT
Qwarm = 110.6 g × 4.184 J/(g·°C) × (−20.6°C)
Qwarm = −9,531.9 J
```
*Negative = heat was LOST by the warm water.*

**Step 5:** Heat gained by ice
```
Qice = −Qwarm = −(−9,531.9 J) = +9,531.9 J
```
*Positive = heat was GAINED by the ice.*

**Step 6:** Heat of Fusion
```
ΔHf = Qice / mice = 9,531.9 J / 27.7 g = 344.1 J/g
```

### Trial 2 — CALCULATIONS

```
mw = 150.3 − 45.2 = 105.1 g
mice = 176.8 − 150.3 = 26.5 g
ΔT = 15.3 − 36.1 = −20.8°C

Qice = +9,146.5 J
ΔHf = 9,146.5 / 26.5 = 345.2 J/g
```

## AVERAGE & PERCENT ERROR

```
Average ΔHf = (344.1 + 345.2) / 2 = 344.6 J/g

Percent Error = |344.6 − 333.55| / 333.55 × 100 = 3.31%
```

**This is an EXCELLENT result** — within the "Excellent" range of <5%. 

In a real lab, expect most groups to get 5-15% error. Here's what their results tell you:

| Student Result | What Happened |
|---------------|---------------|
| 280-310 J/g | Wet ice — didn't dry it properly |
| 310-330 J/g | Good technique, minor heat loss or wet ice |
| 330-350 J/g | Excellent technique |
| 350-380 J/g | Slight heat loss to air, or ice from freezer was below 0°C |
| 380-420 J/g | Significant heat loss + sub-zero ice |
| >420 J/g | Something went very wrong — check their math |

## POST-LAB QUESTIONS — WALKTHROUGH

### Question 1: Basic Calorimetry

**"What quantity of heat is required to raise the temperature of 500 grams of water from 25°C to 85°C?"**

Walk students through:
```
Given: m = 500 g, c = 4.18 J/(g·°C), ΔT = 85 − 25 = 60°C

Q = m × c × ΔT
Q = 500 × 4.18 × 60
Q = 125,400 J = 125.4 kJ
```

**Common mistake:** Students forgetting units or using ΔT = 25 instead of 60.

---

### Question 2: Unknown Metal

**"20 g metal at 25°C placed in 50 g water at 90°C. Final temp = 80°C. Find specific heat of metal."**

Walk students through:
```
Heat lost by water = Heat gained by metal

Qwater = 50.0 g × 4.184 × (80 − 90) = 50 × 4.184 × (−10) = −2,092 J

The water lost 2,092 J, so the metal gained 2,092 J.

Qmetal = mmetal × cmetal × ΔTmetal
2,092 = 20 × cmetal × (80 − 25)
2,092 = 20 × cmetal × 55
2,092 = 1,100 × cmetal
cmetal = 2,092 / 1,100 = 1.90 J/(g·°C)
```

**Important teaching moment:** This value (1.90 J/g·°C) is higher than any common metal. Discuss: this is actually a realistic problem with "imperfect" data — in a real lab, your numbers don't always match a textbook metal perfectly. This can be used to discuss experimental error, heat loss, and the challenges of real measurements.

## TROUBLESHOOTING DURING LAB

| Problem | Quick Fix |
|---------|-----------|
| "My thermometer reads 0.5°C in ice water" | That's normal. Note a correction factor but don't redo everything. |
| "I added too much ice and it won't all melt" | Discard trial. Start over with less ice. |
| "My temperature won't stop dropping" | Still have unmelted ice. Keep stirring. Check for tiny ice chips. |
| "I got ΔHf = 500 J/g" | Check math first. If math is right, ice was likely sub-zero or significant heat loss. |
| "I got ΔHf = 200 J/g" | Ice was very wet. Or mass measurement error. |
| "My two trials don't agree at all" | This is a learning moment! Discuss why technique matters. |
| Student forgot to dry ice | Let them see the low result, then explain WHY it's low. More memorable than preventing the error. |

---

## WRAP-UP TALKING POINTS (Last 3 minutes)

1. **"How close did you get?"** — Poll the class. Celebrate groups with <10% error.

2. **"Why was your value off?"** — Most will be a little high (heat loss to air). Some will be low (wet ice). Use this to reinforce error analysis.

3. **"The big takeaway:"** — It takes as much energy to melt ice as it does to heat water from 0°C to 80°C. That's why ice in your drink lasts so long. That's why ice packs work. That's why polar ice melt is such a big deal for climate.

4. **"Next time you see ice melting, think about the 334 joules per gram being quietly absorbed."**
